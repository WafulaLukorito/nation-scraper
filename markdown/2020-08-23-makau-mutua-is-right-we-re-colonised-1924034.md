## What you need to know:

* For instance, the hope of Sony Sugar FC and Chemelil FC faded away mid-season due to bankruptcy.

The August 16 article by *Sunday Nation* columnist Makau Mutua, titled: “Why Kenyan EPL fans are traitors”, summoned local football fans to place their priorities right. The message was simple and clear: Patriotism and loyalty must be practised in all sports.

Considering the excessive local following of the English Premier League (EPL), Kenya football should also be on board.

But what makes the “colonial” approach of supporting foreign football clubs against their teams more appealing to the fans?

First, on management of the entities, Kenya football clubs have missed the point. Though fully funded by donors, sponsors and Ministry of Sports, accountability is an alien concept among them.

Some clubs were formed after Independence yet, to date, they haven’t built a sports stadium for their home ground. Players and technical staff go for months without pay. What will motivate fans to support such misery?

The organisation of our football clubs needs scrutiny by auditors. Let’s not write the obvious: When players are not paid well, the results shall be a mess, and the fan base will shrink.

## Tax collection

Secondly, the government of Britain has invested a lot in football and it’s believed that tax collection in sports is higher than in any other sector.

Through creation of a supportive environment in viewership, sufficient budgeting and supportive labour laws, football clubs have to deliver. To bet on such a conducive environment, the clubs flourish even without the support of fans or gate collection.

The fact is, in Kenya, football clubs exist, but the environment does not give anyone the zeal to give support.

Monies that can be used to nurture young footballers in academies and promote equal stability of teams financially can’t be accounted for.

For instance, the hope of Sony Sugar FC and Chemelil FC faded away mid-season due to bankruptcy. The heartbreak gives supporters second thoughts to cheer the well-organised EPL.

Lastly, the marketing strategy is horrible. Kenya football clubs need to find new ways of selling themselves. Most EPL teams perform excellently financially because of sales from match tickets, jerseys and other merchandise.

Their agents have the quality to identify talents in schools and across the world to build up sensational players.

Through overhype by the conventional and social media, the players get excellent ratings, hence increasing value in jerseys and quality.

“Traitors” will increase, leaving the country’s football clubs in a dilemma. Government must support the teams through FKF and Fifa. Media houses must also air Kenyan Premier League matches more and cut on the politics.

**Oluchina Antony, Uasin Gishu**
