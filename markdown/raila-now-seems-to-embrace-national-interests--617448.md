# Raila now seems to embrace national interests

## What you need to know:

* This is the only way to explain his courageous stance on the Mau forest

I have in the past been quite critical of Prime Minister Raila Odinga. While that will not change – because I am a critic of society – I must admit that Mr Odinga is a changed man.

Mr Odinga, the scion of Jaramogi Oginga Odinga -- the late doyen of opposition politics -- has evolved into a statesman since the cataclysmic violence following the 2007 elections.

There is little doubt that the ensuing pogroms were a defining moment for Mr Odinga. That experience seems to have forged him into a true national leader. In a word, Mr Odinga has come of age.

In Twelfth Night, William Shakespeare wrote that “some are born great, some achieve greatness, and some have greatness thrust upon ‘em.” Mr Odinga may have been “born great” because he carries the Odinga name, but heredity alone cannot explain his uncanny ability to stay at the centre of the political stage.

The fact that the son of Jaramogi is the centre of gravity of Kenyan politics is without doubt. He is both a political strategist and tactician of enormous talent. It’s rare for him to miss an opportunity, which explains why he sometimes keeps the company of a few strange bedfellows.

But that is in the nature of the beast called politics and is not unique to Mr Odinga. The point is that being “born great” has little to do with Mr Odinga’s political success.

Nor has greatness been thrust upon Mr Odinga. He has laboured in the vineyards of political wilderness for ages, including long stretches in detention.

The Kenyan political establishment has expended enormous energy trying to deny Mr Odinga greatness. That is the scheme behind the resurgent alliance which seeks to reunite Kanu behind Vice-President Kalonzo Musyoka, Deputy Prime Minister Uhuru Kenyatta, Rift Valley kingpin William Ruto, and Tourism minister Najib Balala, Mr Odinga’s erstwhile ally from the Coast.

This alliance is nothing but a “stop-Odinga” syndicate by the conservative wing of the political class that has traditionally ruled Kenya.

But will Mr Odinga “achieve greatness” come 2012? Or has he achieved greatness already? I think the jury is still out on those questions although I must say that Mr Odinga is a changed man since the accord that saved Kenya from collapse last year.

He has evolved into a national figure of serious significance. He has transcended regional politics and thrown off the albatross of being the leader of the Luo.

Mr Odinga’s transformation seems to have started with the signing of the accord. His decision to accept what he calls “half a loaf of bread” – although I personally think it was more like “one third” – was statesmanlike and the most significant move of his political life. It snatched Kenya back from the precipice.

Since becoming Prime Minister, Mr Odinga has struggled mightily to balance his political ambitions with the national interest.

**National interest**

Unlike the past when he was too quick to act in pure self interest, Mr Odinga now seems to calibrate broader national interests in his decision-making. On a number of critical matters, he has taken steps that could be costly, or even fatal, to his political future.

Increasingly, he is wearing the hat of the nationalist that the political children of Jaramogi have for years demanded of him.

In what is the last quarter of his political life, Mr Odinga appears to be shunning opportunism and returning to the reformist character that made him the mortal enemy of the one-party state. This is the most important distinction between him and the other pretenders to the presidency.

This is the only way to explain his courageous stance on the Mau Forest in the face of withering attacks from Mr Ruto and Mr Balala and their minions within ODM.

Mr Odinga has stuck to his guns even at the risk of giving legs to the KKK alliance which seeks to unite the Kalenjin, Kamba, and Kikuyu in a tribal plot to deny him the State House in 2012.

Although I would like him to fight harder for a local tribunal to bring the perpetrators of post-election violence to book, Mr Odinga has openly committed the state to handing over suspects to Mr Luis Moreno-Ocampo, the Prosecutor of the International Criminal Court, once indictments are issued.

This has enraged Mr Ruto and driven him closer to Mr Kenyatta who is also a vociferous critic of the ICC.

A particular sickness of Kenya’s political class has been the cancer of ethnicity. It is a given that Kenya will not go anywhere until and unless its leaders become Kenyans in fact, not just in law.

I am going to keep my eye on Mr Odinga because I think he has the potential to catalyse the transformation of the political class. He will achieve greatness if he does so.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights Commission.*
