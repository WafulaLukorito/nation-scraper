In the past several weeks, I have engaged in a spirited and often bitter debate with some of my fellows Kenyans on the vexatious issue of the boda boda industry. As is now known to everyone, unless you live in a cave, I’ve called for a total ban on boda boda in Kenya’s cities and major towns.

I have charged the industry – and everyone knows this to be fact – with causing mayhem, chaos, and death on our roads. I have termed the industry an existential threat to the security of the republic. I believe they are ripe for weaponisation by demagogic and thuggish politicians into gangs, militias, or even terror groups. In this column I now suggest alternatives to boda boda.

The pro-boda boda choir has correctly argued that the industry supports the livelihoods of many Kenyans. No argument there. But so do many undesirable and illicit trades and pursuits, including the oldest profession. If putting food on the table was the only criterion for permitting an industry even petty theft rings and corruption networks would get a pass. No – that can’t and shouldn’t be the criterion.

Some folks have suggested that we sanitise boda boda by regulating them. Even those well-wishers know that’s impossible in Kenya. The industry is so lawless that it’s passed the door of no return. It can’t – and won’t – submit to regulation. The boda boda industry is utterly incapable of regulation. The traffic police are either incapable, or unwilling, to enforce traffic laws.

There is only one country where regulation of boda boda seems to be successful in Africa. Rwanda has done what others have failed to, or can’t, do. Compared to Rwanda, Kenya’s is so vast and complex that a large industry such as boda boda can go completely rogue, which it has. The only solution is a complete ban to return sanity to our roads. Our urban transportation infrastructure can’t cope anymore with population growth under the existing plans and models, if one can call them that. The boda boda sector – together with the chaotic matatu and their primitive cousins, tuk-tuks – have retuned us to the Stone Age. It’s intolerable and we must sober up our transport sector and infrastructure.

Time was when transportation in Nairobi and other major towns was a thing of beauty. Rare was the matatu – that raging ugly, usually un-roadworthy, smoke-belching, and noisy excuse of a bus – careening down our roads threatening pedestrians and other vehicles with annihilation. I remember the era of Kenya Bus Service, which were timely, clean, and courteous road warriors carrying city passengers. Then some clever knucklehead decided to licence the matatu, and not regulate them. The result has been traffic snarls and death on our roads, the likes of which are only comparable to the most unmanageable cities on the continent. Money and corruption have led to this rot on Kenya’s roads. We need political courage and the strict adherence to the rule of law to rid ourselves of this mess.

Let me address boda boda. They are clearly unviable and intolerable if our major cities and towns aspire to be world-class. Or barely livable for our populations. I lump boda boda and tuk-tuk together for both are a total abomination. The one thing I don’t like about Mombasa is the ubiquitous tuk-tuk. The county government there should grow cojones and ban them completely. Thank goodness Nairobi doesn’t have them, or we would be forced to go into exile. So ban tuk-tuk and boda boda at the same time and introduce city and town publicly-owned buses in the model of the now virtually defunct KBS. Enough of such buses with designated routes and set pick up and drop off times and stations would decongest our roads, make them safe, and get everyone to work on time.

Publicly-owned city and town buses must again become the dominant mode of public transportation as the government pursues public-private partnerships to build light rail and subways where possible. There is a climate benefit to light rail and subways and electric powered buses that won’t add to our already terrible carbon footprint. Solar-powered buses, such as those used in some school districts in New York, would be a huge advantage for the country. As for the matatu, I would sharply limit their number, subject them to strict emission standards, strictly enforce traffic laws for them, and require them to be the same model with identical colors and designated stops.

I end where I started. I will not stop campaigning against boda boda, tuk-tuk, and matatu run amok. In fact, I will get louder until sanity and the rule of law are restored in our transportation sector. I have heard people tell me I should go back to New York if I don’t like the chaos. Good luck. Enough Kenyans support this fight which we will carry on until victory.

*Makau Mutua is SUNY Distinguished Professor and Margaret W. Wong Professor at Buffalo Law School, The State University of New York. He’s Senior Advisor on Constitutional Affairs to President William Ruto. On X: @makaumutua.*
