# The Executive in draft law is a stroke of genius

## What you need to know:

* Let us keep our eyes on the prize and not play into the hands of anti-reformers

Many pundits – some of them learned friends – have been insisting that the State President in the draft constitution is maridadi or ceremonial.

Nothing could be farther from the truth. One could be tempted to think that they either cannot read, or they know nothing about constitutional law and the structures of state power. But that is not true – they are simply doing the bidding of their malevolent paymasters.

These megaphones of the “filthy rich” are deliberately poisoning the public to defeat the reform of the state – once again. They hope, by their lies, to “manipulate” and “demagogue” the draft constitution so that it can be voted down.

I wrote last week – and will repeat it here – the Committee of Experts has given us the wisest structure of the Executive given the history of Kenya’s abusive presidency.

The bottom line is that the State President is not “imperial” and the Prime Minister is not “executive”. In other words, the Executive arm of the state is no longer an overlord over the people.

The two key offices in the Executive – the State President and the Prime Minister – are subject to stringent checks and balances that they have no chance of going “rogue”. Nor is there a loophole for them to directly compete against one another. The State President is still the top dog, but he cannot act alone.

The Executive is hemmed in by Parliament so tightly that both arms of the state must work together. In this arrangement, the Cabinet, which the Prime Minister will “compose” and “chair”, is vested with real power.

However, the Prime Minister lacks “executive” powers in the way the British, Indian or Israeli prime ministers do. The reason is that the State President exercises a large number of executive powers subject to the Parliament, the Cabinet, and the Prime Minister.

The key difference between the draft constitution and the Westminster model is that executive authority is dispersed to other democratic organs. That’s why the draft proposes a “semi-presidential” – not “parliamentary” – model.

Those who argue that the State President is maridadi are mischievous. In fact, the State President has a hand in most major national matters. The big difference is that he or she must “consult” and “act in concert” with other arms of the state and the Prime Minister.

His or her decision-making powers are highly circumscribed. Gone will be the days of unilateral decisions. But folks should be clear that the Prime Minister is not the “new imperial president”.

The Prime Minister will not act alone either – only the Cabinet can take decisions which the Prime Minister would have to sign for “prompt” communication to the State President. This is the essence of collective governance.

Those opposed to this structure of the Executive have no interest in democracy. They cannot imagine a state without an imperial and an unaccountable head of state or government. Nor can they think of state power without a Kenyatta, Moi, or Kibaki.

They are nostalgic about presidential despotism. They seek the power to oppress Kenyans and misappropriate the country’s resources with impunity. They are upset because accountability and transparency are the watchwords of the draft constitution.

But in a break with the past, the State President will not be a bully or despot. This is the best of both worlds. As I argued last week, this model is best analogised to the French or Russian executives.

I urge you to closely examine the political pedigrees of those opposed to the draft constitution. Most of them were Kanu diehards until 2002. Now they have regrouped as the KKK tribal alliance to “herd” the Kalenjin, Kikuyu, and the Kamba like cattle for their selfish ends.

Some of their leading lights are most likely in the crosshairs of Mr Luis Moreno-Ocampo and the International Criminal Court. Nor is it lost on anyone that the opposition to conserving the Mau Forest and the censure plot against Prime Minister Raila Odinga are pretexts for subterfuge against the pursuit of justice by the ICC.

This proposed “unholy alliance” wants to defeat the draft constitution and escape Mr Moreno Ocampo’s vice.

But we must not let them. As a country, we have come to a crossroads. Either we deliver now or we will be doomed forever. Let us not permit the forces of “reaction” and these “champions of impunity” to drive the debate on the draft constitution.

I know there are some good people who have problems with some provisions in the draft constitution. However, let us not be “single-issue” advocates. That is not how democracy works.

Let’s work with the Committee of Experts to impress upon it the importance of several revisions. But let us not hold the draft constitution hostage simply because our “pet” issues are not addressed.

The constitution is a project and a work in progress. There will be plenty of time for us to amend it after it is passed. Let us keep our eyes on the prize, and not play into the hands of anti-reformers. Let’s pass the draft constitution.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights Commission.*
