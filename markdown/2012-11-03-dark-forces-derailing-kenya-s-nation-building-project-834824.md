I don’t like alarmists, and I am not one. But I don’t like burying my head in the sand either. That’s because a spade can’t be a spoon. Why am I alarmed? I will be honest with you. There are “centrifugal” forces pulling Kenya apart.

It feels like a “black hole” is pulling the country to collapse into itself. In mid-December 2007, I predicted “civil war” at a public lecture in Nairobi.

My analysis – call it “crystal ball” – was that the election would precipitate a catastrophic paroxysm. Our “free press” refused to report my “alarm bell”. I was right – Kenya almost went to hell. Today, I almost feel the way I felt in December 2007.

This is why. When I grew up in the 1960s and 1970s, I was more “centred” as a Kenyan. Let me confess – I didn’t trust Mzee Jomo Kenyatta.

But the State meant something. Politicians spoke with moral weight. We listened with deference. That’s because public figures were generally more believable.

Today, there’s hardly a single politician who inspires hope, or enjoys universal respect. I know – there was lots of looting, land grabbing, and state brutality in the old “golden days”. But there was an intangible – a sense that no matter our flaws, we were “building” a nation, not “tearing” one apart.

Today, the “falcon cannot hear the falconer; things fall apart; the centre cannot hold; mere anarchy is loosed upon the world”.

William Butler Yates, the famous Irish poet, might as well have written *The Second Coming* for Kenya. That’s why the iconic Nigerian novelist Chinua Achebe “stole” it for the title *Things Fall Apart*. In the novel, Mr Achebe is lamenting the plunder and spiritual rape of the Igbo by the white man. Igbo society was “falling part”.

Okonkwo, the tragic protagonist, epitomises this epic collapse. That’s how Kenya feels like today. In essence, “they” have put a knife in the things that held us together. In Kenya today, as in *The Second Coming*, the sinister figure of the proverbial “Antichrist” may foretell the Apocalypse. Let me be clear – our fate isn’t cast in stone. We can escape catastrophe.

In 1995, I wrote a dense, but widely quoted, scholarly legal article entitled “Why Redraw the Map of Africa: A Moral and Legal Inquiry”. In it, I argued that the African state was a sociopath – an ogre bent on the consumption of its citizens.

Why – because the African state was “nonsense on stilts”. Virtually all African states are the haphazard creatures of psychotic European imperial cartographers.

During the Scramble for Africa, the colonialists carved up the continent without reference to ethnicity, culture, history, and even geography. White men sat somewhere in Europe and used ruler and pencil to “rape” Africa.

They were hungry beasts interested only in Africa’s wealth. The colonial states they made up were “completely fake”.

Think about this. Kenya, Tanganyika, and Uganda – as almost all other African colonial states – were fake. They were false. Perhaps the only exceptions were Ethiopia and Swaziland. So when does a state that’s unreal become real?

When, if I may ask, do the Akamba and the Somali start to see each other as Kenyans? It doesn’t work to simply “declare” them “Kenyans” on paper. When do they become part of a nation called “Kenya” whose nationals call themselves “Kenyans?” I am not talking about Kenyan “citizens”. “Citizenship” is a legal term – a paper term – that’s shallow.

By “Kenyan” I mean something more – a bone-deep identity that transcends paper identity. It’s a cultural and ideological submission to a polity.

There are sharp distinctions between three terms that most people conflate. These are – state, country, and nation. It helps to have all three rolled into one. But you can have a state and a country without a nation. You can also have a nation without a country or a state.

This is my point – at what point in history did Kenya become a nation? One theory is that the United States cohered into a nation because of the civil war. Nation building is an organic slow-burning process. It’s not like instant coffee. It requires the creation of a “national consciousness”.

Do the people who call themselves “Kenyans” have a “Kenyan national consciousness?” My answer to this question is negative.

I know my conclusion is alarming to some. But it’s the reality. Our leaders have spent nearly five decades of independence entrenching ethnic – not national – identities. The white colonialists lumped us together by force, but we’ve failed to create a “compact of the willing”.

Rather than “unite” us, our leaders survive on “dividing” us. If you doubt me, listen to most of the presidential candidates. They start by mobilising “their own” against “the others”.

Look at the unprincipled way in which they’ve dealt with the so-called Mombasa Republican Council, a group of nascent secessionists. The state has failed to understand that the MRC is clear evidence that Kenya is an incomplete “national” project. Talk to them, don’t kill them.

Many forces threaten the Kenyan project. Think of the extra-judicial killings, including those of Muslim clerics, religious extremists burning churches, tribal hate stoked against the International Criminal Court, grisly massacres of poor villagers over quarrels on land and other resources, false “peace conferences” by a hypocritical political class that thrives on hate mongering, the looting of the public purse to stock up “election war chests,” a state bureaucracy and commissions inhabited by self-seekers, and a feckless citizenry that loves looters.

Can the centre really hold? Does Kenya have the fortitude to conduct and survive the next General Election?

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at SUNY Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
