## What you need to know:

* Supremacist notions, or practices of exclusion, will put our country asunder.
* What President William Ruto has done is to try and re-balance the scales.

Last week, a politician with national visibility from Mt Kenya made an unfortunate nativist claim. In footage widely viewed on social media, the cocky MP looked straight into the camera and told Kenyans the Gema (Gikuyu, Embu, and Meru) communities had decided to a person to oppose President William Ruto’s re-election in 2027.

According to the MP, when Gema groups decide an issue, they move as one like a herd of sheep. The MP said they did so in 2022 to support then DP Ruto and will do so again in 2027 to oppose him. The MP spoke with the arrogance, entitlement, and confidence of a person who wields enormous power. I was flabbergasted by the MP’s contempt and supremacist countenance.

I’ve written times without number that Kenya isn’t a nation yet, but a nation in embryo. Meaning we are still baking in the womb and petri-dish of nationhood the political society we call Kenya. We don’t yet have a full-blown national consciousness. Our zeitgeist is a wobbly patchwork of incomplete emotions. We don’t yet regard each other as fellow citizens but members of disparate ethnicities and races with hostile and competing interests.

At best, we see each other as incomplete human beings. At worst, some of us regard “the other” as sub-human. As a state and country, we haven’t reached irreversibility. We disdain each other. Just look at the vastly incongruous levels of development, education, wealth, and other indicia.

## Supremacist notions

Kenya’s nadir as a nation is in full display for all to see. A child born in the so-called Northern Frontier District has an infinitesimal chance of survival and prosperity compared to one born, say, in Murang’a.

Once in 2003 when I was leading the task force under the Kibaki regime to establish a truth commission, a Somali woman asked me at Wagalla in Wajir “how is Kenya?” I didn’t understand the question and asked her to repeat it. To which she responded calmly that “Kenya starts at Mwingi.” I was stunned. Then I went to Narok and in the presence of the late Maasai supremo William Ntimama I was told over and over again that “Kenyans” should give the Maasai back their land.

Kenya has many communities that have been “othered.” Some stories are more severe than others. But we live in a country where some groups believe they “own” the country, and that others exist at their sufferance. This doesn’t make Kenya unique, but it makes our co-existence toxic. It’s difficult to live with people who believe that you are inferior to them, or that you exist to serve them.

If you look at the history of conflict in the world, you will notice that the most explosive cleavages are those of race, ethnicity, and religion and their manifestation in proximity to power and access to resources. Those who are excluded, discriminated against, and despised will rebel. It’s just a matter of time.

No country can cohere into a durable, permanent nation devoid of fragility if these cleavages and exclusions are allowed to fester. Those who believe themselves superior to others court disaster. That’s how countries fail. Look around Africa and elsewhere in the world. No further evidence is required. Either we all prosper, or no one prospers. Either we all have a country, or we all don’t. Supremacist notions, or practices of exclusion, will put our country asunder. These narratives must end.

## Demeaning stereotypes

I hear, for example, that such and such a community is “hardworking” or that another is “lazy.” These splitist and demeaning stereotypes must end, or we are all doomed. That’s what White Americans said about Black Africans and yet they held them in enslavement to work the plantations and build America. How ridiculous and ironic.

Let me return to the aforementioned MP who declared that a united Gema will make sure President Ruto isn’t re-elected. To suggest that one community holds the key to Kenya’s presidency is vacuous and contemptible. I am not aware of any scientific or empirical data that gives heft to such a preposterous idea.

First, I thought communities are made up of individual members who have their own agency. Each individual in a democracy is expected to make an individual rational choice. Otherwise democracy becomes a fraud. When I look around, I see that the Gema communities are over-represented in government. They get the lion’s share of national resources. What President Ruto has done is to try and re-balance the scales. Is it such a terrible idea that your neighbors are “eating” too?

I am not bitter that some Kenyans think like the MP. I am only sorry that such people aren’t self-aware. We shall not go anywhere as a country, let alone Singapore, if such bigots dominate our politics. Nations must be engineered for success. States don’t fly on autopilot. Let’s reject the MP’s vitriolic philosophy and build one Kenya.
