On October 10, I had a long and lovely conversation with Kenya’s political maestro [Raila Odinga](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/raila-amolo-odinga-3809512). He had called me as he checked out of hospital. Then his doctor walked in as we talked. Rather than ask to call back, he allowed me to eavesdrop in on his conversation with his doctor. The doctor was upbeat as he bid him goodbye.

He told Mr Odinga he had a clean bill of health and that he had a long and fulfilling life ahead of him. That sounded right to me because Mr Odinga’s voice was robust and happy. We agreed to talk in a few days. I had no idea that was the last time we would speak.

ODM leader Raila Odinga wiping his eye as he addressed journalists at Jaramogi Oginga Odinga Foundation in Nairobi on May 16, 2023

Photo credit: Evans Habil | Nation Media Group

On October 11, we agreed on text that he would visit Kitui Villa during Mashujaa Day on October 20, and that he would see if [President William Ruto](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/william-samoei-ruto-3809570) would come with him. But there was what is now an eerie caveat. He wrote that he wasn’t sure he would come to Kitui, but that if he did come, he would certainly visit my place. He’d been there many times, and on two officially opened wings of the premises. It had been a great honour for me and my family, as well as the community. On a normal week, I would speak with Baba two or three times. I never got a chance to speak or communicate with him again until I saw him at the Lee Funeral Home a week later.

## Key strategists

I got the call that [Mr Odinga had left us very early in the morning of October 15](https://nation.africa/kenya/news/politics/raila-odinga-dies-at-age-80-in-india--5230722). I became immediately numb and couldn’t feel or sense any part of my body. I believe a part of me died with him. I have known Mr Odinga closely for more than three decades.

In that period, our relationship evolved from a purely political one to a kinship, a deep friendship that was both social and confidential. We became family. I became one of his confidantes and key strategists. This is neither the time nor place to provide more detail. A time will come to tell the great man’s story in full. But suffice is to note that if I feel this way, I can’t fathom what Mama Ida, Rosemary, Winnie, and Junior have gone through.

I know family meant everything to the man we came to call Agwambo, Jakom, or Baba. Ida had been his rock even when the days were the darkest. His children were the apple of his eyes. May they know grace and peace in his physical absence. Mama Ida is a pillar and I believe she will rise up from this immeasurable loss and steer the family safely ashore.

Of that I am fully confident. I am also confident that the rest of the Odinga clan especially Senator Oburu Oginga and MP Ruth Odinga will rally around them. The largest tree in Kenya has fallen but it hasn’t done so in vain. Resilient and unbowed, Mr Odinga will live on in every Kenyan.

I have so many memorable vignettes with Mr Odinga. Let me tell a few. In 2009 Mr Odinga was the commencement speaker when I was dean of the Law School at the State University of New York. It was a momentous visit as he was Prime Minister then. Kenyans travelled from many parts of United States to come and fellowship with him.

## Africa’s renaissance

He took the Law School community by storm and in a deeply philosophical speech on Africa’s renaissance and the key importance of the youth – such as the graduands – in a democracy. His visit is spoken of even to this day. He formed lifelong American admirers from that visit. Many have since texted me to express their shock and deep condolences.

I’ve been privileged with a front row seat in Baba’s life. We ran his last most important races together. The first was his quest for the Chairperson of the African Union Commission. I was honoured to be a key strategist for him. We traversed the continent together in a very small tight-knit group which often included Winnie, Paul Achar, and Ambassador Mahboub Maalim, his key security men the late George Oduor and Maurice Ogeta, and Emmanuel Wanson. Usually we travelled with a senior state official since this was Kenya’s campaign. We were at times joined by Dr Caroline Karugu and Pauline Njoroge. Baba regaled us with deep historical insights. In every African capital, he was received with the protocols of a head of state.

I ran Baba’s last mile with him. In the deepest crisis to grip our country in years, he and President William Ruto, Kenya’s two towering political figures, put their ambitions aside and birthed the Broad-Based Government which has steadied the ship of state and restored sanity to Kenya’s politics. Baba left us a peacemaker. We must make sure his legacy endures.
