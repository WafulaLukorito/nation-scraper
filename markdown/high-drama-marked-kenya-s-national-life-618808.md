# High drama marked Kenya’s national life

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2009-12-26T15:37:02Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/high-drama-marked-kenya-s-national-life-618808  

---

It was the year that President Barack Hussein Obama of the United States unexpectedly won the Nobel Peace Prize, but paradoxically decided to escalate American military involvement in Afghanistan.

Euphemistically known as the “Graveyard of Empires,” the country in the grip of the Taliban, a medieval extremist Muslim syndicate, may very well hold the key to the success – or failure – of the Obama presidency. A quagmire there – and massive American casualties in body bags – could turn the most spectacular American president in history into a colossal failure.

On the other hand, success – defined as the establishment of a peaceful democracy in Afghanistan – would almost certainly guarantee Mr Obama’s re-election and cement his status as one of history’s greatest political leaders.

While President Obama made history, Kenya was either stuck in neutral, or took a few steps back. The country’s political class was engaged in endless small-minded games that lacked any purpose larger than their individual and myopic ambitions. They bounced from one rally to the next launching silly attacks against each other. In perpetual campaign mode, the most senior leaders criss-crossed the country hurling insults at each other before starving and impoverished crowds.

I could not help but think that the entire country was hostage to a few thugs who pass for political leaders. But I was disappointed that Kenyans continued to indulge these malevolent figures, many of them nothing but political charlatans. I yearn for the day that ordinary Kenyans will wisen up and refuse to go to abusive rallies.

But 2009 was a year of high drama, none more dramatic than when Mr Kofi Annan, the sage who saved Kenya from collapse last year, handed the vaunted Waki Envelope to Mr Luis Moreno-Ocampo. You could guess by deductive reasoning the names most likely to be on that list.

They denounced the ICC. They cried foul. They sued. They darkly talked about a repeat of last year’s killings. They threatened Armageddon. They did everything but confess to their crimes. But we all knew that they were spending sleepless nights not knowing when the ICC might swoop down and take them to The Hague for authoring last year’s post-election crimes against humanity. They know in their hearts that they have a date with lady justice.

But throughout 2009 President Mwai Kibaki struck his customary pose – serene and unbothered. I don’t know whether Mr Kibaki knows much about the Buddha, but he surely does a good impersonation. The man could be in a raging inferno and look totally unmoved. He really must know how to channel his inner energies.

Isn’t it interesting how President Kibaki – to paraphrase minister William ole Ntimama’s notoriously infamous statement – lies low like an envelope? Why, and how, such a man can be so successful politically should be the subject of serious study by social scientists. Either he is a political genius or he is the luckiest living Kenyan. The other possibility is that we could all be damned fools.

Then there was Prime Minister Raila Odinga, the hardest working politician in Kenya. He tried to reach for the skies, both literally and figuratively. Literally – because he tried to save the ozone and destruction of Kenya’s green lands. He understood the significance of destroying forest cover and water catchments.

He knows Kenya will join the unforgiving Sahara unless the state acts to prevent the looting of ground cover. Figuratively – because he spoke to the nobility of the national project. What was impressive is that he did so at his own political peril. Or perhaps not.

As a master political strategist, he may know that the future belongs to those who think big, even when it is politically risky. It’s a political gamble, but one for which Kenyans may handsomely reward him. Not everything was negative in 2009. The Committee of Experts unveiled a thoughtful draft constitution. Most Kenyans were impressed by the document and large segments of the society vowed to support it.

But much will depend on whether certain tribal barons – who have an eye on 2012 – will see the greater good and fold their malignant cards. To that end, it would be tremendously helpful if the two key principals – Prime Minister Odinga and President Kibaki – endorsed the draft constitution and whipped their allies to act in the national interest. For Mr Kibaki, he can claim the new constitution as “his legacy” to Kenya. For Mr Odinga, he would be the victor to whom the spoils would go.

2009 also saw one important taboo broken. For the first time in the country’s history, there was an open public debate on gay rights and sexual orientation. Some gay Kenyans came out and demanded recognition. This may have seemed like a tempest in a tea pot at the time, but history will prove that it was a pivotal moment in the journey of Kenyan democracy.

It was a small blow against bigotry, but a giant leap for Kenya’s soul. I want to predict that another giant leap will be taken next year when a democratic constitution is passed. Goodbye 2009.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights.*
