# KACC boss should have a record of fighting graft 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2009-10-09T13:53:39Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/kacc-boss-should-have-a-record-of-fighting-graft--610764  

---

## What you need to know:

* Is Kenya’s Parliament willing to go all the way and fulfill its national duty?

Mr Aaron Ringera, the former director of the Kenya Anti-Corruption Commission, was run out of town by popular will. Even though the motives of some of his detractors were suspect, Mr Ringera was decapitated mostly for the right reasons.

The public had come to believe that Mr Ringera was one of the pillars of impunity in Kenya. He, President Mwai Kibaki, Attorney-General Amos Wako, Ambassador Francis Muthaura, and Chief Justice Evan Gicheru were viewed as the pinnacle of official hubris.

But while Mr Ringera is gone, the others are still in place. Mr Ringera’s departure is significant, but it would be a mistake to see it as a game changer.

It is a fact that some folks went after Mr Ringera because of his ethnic identity. Others were plainly piqued that he made so much money for doing nothing. Yet some would like to kill the KACC altogether. But it is to the better angels of the Kenyan public that we must listen.

The vast majority wanted Mr Ringera out because they saw him as protecting looters. But the public must not stop there. A large share of the blame must fall on Parliament.

It is the people’s chamber that gave Mr Ringera a fat paycheck and denied him the powers to prosecute. Isn’t it hypocritical that the same legislators would quarter Mr Ringera like a chicken for failing to prosecute?

The truth is that neither Parliament, nor Mr Ringera, had any stomach for fighting the networks of corruption. The cesspool of corruption is too deep and wide – it is an integral of the body politic.

In fact, I bet there are very few senior public officials who do not swim in this cesspool. That’s why the failure to fight corruption is not Mr Ringera’s alone. It is a systemic failure.

The rot engulfs even some of those who shout the loudest against it in Parliament. Mr Ringera was simply one cog, albeit a significant one, in the massive state cover-up of grand corruption.

Those who designed his office meant it to deceive and hoodwink. Mr Ringera once confessed as much to me and Mr John Githongo, the former anti-corruption czar. So let’s not demonise Mr Ringera as though he was an exceptional monster.

As I see it, there is only one way for Parliament to redeem itself from the Ringera debacle. It gets a second bite at the apple to clean up its act. But first, Mr Danson Mungatana should drop his quest to place the KACC under the AG’s Office. What an awful idea!

Why would Mr Mungatana think that the KACC would be more effective under Mr Amos Wako, himself the master of the dodge? If you want to kill the KACC dead, then place it under Mr Wako.

Parliament should get serious and take a sharp knife to the Anti-Corruption and Economic Crimes Act. With surgical precision, it should excise and amend that law to give the KACC teeth.

Critically, the KACC must be given powers to prosecute. Parliament should amend the constitution if that is what is required.

Secondly, the KACC Advisory Board and Parliament must make sure that the next KACC director has a proven record of fighting corruption. He or she must be someone who has impeccable democratic credentials and is beyond reproach. In my view, the matter should not be left in President Kibaki’s hands.

Essentially, the President should be given a sealed envelope so that his only task would be to open it and announce the new director of the KACC.

There should be as little a margin of error as possible so that the forces of darkness do not put their agents in charge of the KACC again. By staring the President down – and driving Mr Ringera off to the sunset – Parliament has demonstrated that it can act with resolve.

But is it willing to go all the way and fulfill its national duty? Or will it be compromised and manipulated by the masters of illusion?

I know the names that Parliament should hand to Mr Kibaki for the directorship of the KACC. Any one of the three would be a superb appointment.

My choices to fill the directorship of a retooled KACC are Mr John Githongo, the former anti-corruption czar, Mr Pheroze Nowrojee, the Senior Counsel and a wise statesman, and Dr Willy Mutunga, the head of the Ford Foundation in Eastern Africa. The three have a demonstrated record of superior public service and an unbending desire to reform Kenya.

Mr Githongo paid a heavy price for his courage when he was forced into lonely exile over Anglo Leasing. Mr Nowrojee is an impeccable lawyer and academic who has steadfastly fought for democracy. Dr Mutunga, an erudite man, is the doyen of civil society. His humility and sacrifice for change have no parallel in Kenya.

Parliament has no choice but to act resolutely to redeem itself. Since it has failed to enact a special tribunal to try the perpetrators of post-election violence, it has one more shot at self-redemption.

We know that the presidency is utterly incapable of reform. Quite the contrary, the State House is the seat of anti-reform. That is why Parliament – with all its contradictions – must fill the void. My suspicion is that we are seeing the beginning of the end of the imperial presidency.

Parliament can hasten that process by boxing in the State House with a real anti-corruption agency headed by genuine reformers.

We know the judiciary will not come to the rescue. Two arms of the government see no evil, hear no evil, and speak no evil. But the legislature appears not to be blind. The only question is whether it will see clearly enough.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights Commission.*
