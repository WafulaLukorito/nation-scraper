Human rights are emerging as the malignant cancer of the William Ruto administration, and unfortunately, there is no cure.

The nomination of Dr Duncan Oburu Ojwang as chairperson of the Kenya National Commission on Human Rights, at the same time as the appointment of Prof Makau Mutua to head a coordinating framework for compensating victims of protests and riots is eloquent proof of frantic efforts to cut it out.

Until his defection to the dark side a few months ago, Prof Mutua was the scholarly pontiff of human rights. Now he applies embalming fluid to create a semblance of life where it long ceased to exist.

Dr Ojwang is unalloyed in his loyalty to President Ruto: He was not only a legal researcher on the government payroll when Mr Ruto was charged with crimes against humanity at the International Criminal Court, but was also on the legal team defending the 2022 presidential election result. He was promptly appointed to the board of directors of the Kenya Power & Lighting Company for his troubles.

Before rising to the top of the pile of individuals to take the place of the woman commissioner at KNCHR which fell vacant after the death of Chairperson Roselyne Odede, Dr Ojwang had pitched his hat in the ring for a commissioner’s position at the Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission.

## Public protests

In between, Dr Ojwang has not allowed the public to breathe. As a Ruto advocate, Dr Ojwang has been on television like white on rice. Every television show he has graced has given him an opportunity to push his odious views on human rights, including the error of public protests, the unviability of delivering the right to education or even affordable healthcare.

Cultivation theory, the communication framework propounded by George Gerbner, suggests that people’s social reality is shaped by the messages and depictions they encounter on television.

On social media, especially X where he is active, he once posted: “To hell with the form of human rights and reform which is more obsessed with the protection of the powerful against probe on Kemsa, State capture and its connection with huge fuel prices. They oppose the probe on State capture in the Kenya Kwanza manifesto as a mere witch-hunt.”

Barring a court order — Katiba Institute and the Kenya Human Rights Commission have filed suit opposing Dr Ojwang’s nomination on the grounds that it violates the principle of gender inclusion — and if Parliament refuses to approve him, the incoming human rights sheriff will arrive with the baggage of his past.

In August 2023, he cheered the appointment of two co-ethnics thus: “I have boundless gratitude for the appointment of these two professionals from our Lake: Gor Selina as manager [at] JKIA and Ogoye Henry as acting MD [of] KIA. This is all happening at a crucial juncture in our history. Our debt to the President for courage and vision is immeasurable.”

It is not unlikely that he will attempt to remake KNCHR in the image of his nominator, thus denying the country an important check on executive power. It is important to remember that the national human rights institution Dr Ojwang has been picked to head has a long history going back some 30 years.

At the height of international condemnation of Daniel arap Moi’s dastardly human rights record, he established the Standing Committee on Human Rights. It was partially cured of its congenital defects when the Kenya National Commission on Human Rights law was enacted in 2002, and went on to become a bulwark against State excesses and human rights violations under the tenure of the first chairman, Maina Kiai.

It is the KNCHR’s seminal report, On the Brink of the Precipice, that laid the ground for the Commission of Inquiry into the Post-Election Violence and the numerous reforms that followed.

Even at its lowest moments, such as when it upbraided victims of state violence during protests over the 2017 elections, the Kenya National Commission on Human Rights has always, at heart, been a people’s institution -- not the president’s laundry basket.

National human rights commissions, created under the Paris Principles, are expected to be robust, populated with commissioners committed to making human rights a reality and willing to stand firm in the face of inevitable resistance from other government departments.

When they are defanged through the systematic appointment of individuals with tenuous human rights credentials and questionable links with political powers, no amount of loquacious lawyering will absolve them of the responsibility to protect and defend human rights.

The nomination of a known lackey of the President to such an important institution at a time when there is yet to be any form of accountability for hundreds of lives lost through State violations is not an act of confidence. It signals a desire to overrun all institutions that make Kenya’s claim to being a constitutional democracy and turn the country into a choir, but more importantly, it is an invitation for Kenyans to look elsewhere for justice.
