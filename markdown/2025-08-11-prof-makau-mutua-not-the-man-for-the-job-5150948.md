President William Ruto’s [proclamation on compensation for victims of public protests and demonstrations](https://nation.africa/kenya/news/ruto-now-sets-up-team-to-compensate-protest-victims-since-2017-5147658) might have sounded, at first glance, like the greatest thing since sliced bread, at least in the life of this regime.

But the devil is in the details, or lack thereof. The first question one must ask is whether the government wants to compensate those it terms as terrorists, and on that basis justifies extreme measures including abduction, torture, illegal confinement, enforced disappearances and extra-judicial executions.

A glaring contradiction is that there has never been any acknowledgement of wrongdoing on the part of government. There is not the slightest sign of regret or apology for the brutality meted out on those simply expressing dissenting political opinion.

Neither was there even the slightest hint that those culpable for the most egregious crimes against citizens would be called to account.

That presidential proclamation appears to be no more than a public relations stunt. Or, maybe, just a piece of political posturing designed mainly to provide President Ruto’s coalition partner, ODM leader Raila Odinga, an escape route in the face of accusations that he has jumped into bed with a regime that murders its own people.

## **Trigger-happy police force**

Mr Odinga has been trying to placate a restive support base by distancing himself from the murderous actions of President Ruto’s trigger-happy police force and demanding justice for the victims.

Now he has it, and to cap it all, his confidante and adviser Prof Makau Mutua has been tapped to lead the State intervention, which will stretch back its work to the 2017 post-election protests.

Prof Makau might look like a good choice. He became one of the direct beneficiaries of the Ruto-Raila broad-based government arrangement.

In his capacity as President Ruto’s Senior Adviser on Constitutional Affairs and Human Rights, he might have the official heft to kick bureaucratic backsides that might impede his assignment.

The good professor also comes with an enviable record in the field of human rights and the unending campaign for social, economic and political justice.

I can disclose here a long association with Prof Mutua dating back to days in student activism at the University of Nairobi. That, of course, means the mother of all higher education institutions in this country, not all those other excuses for universities.

He was one of the Students Organisation of Nairobi University (Sonu) leaders expelled in 1981 by the Moi kleptocracy, thereafter finding his way to exile in Tanzania through an underground railroad organised by his lecturers at the university, notably Dr Willy Mutunga, Prof Kivutha Kibwana and Prof Shadrack Guto.

He went on to continue his law studies at the University of Dar es Salaam, and thereafter shifted his exile base to the United States. It was while in the US that he teamed up with five other Kenyan exiles to in 1991 establish the Kenya Human Rights Commission (KHRC), alongside future Chief Justice Mutunga, future Justice Minister Kiraitu Murungi, activist Maina Kiai and journalism scholar Peter Kareithi.

Prof Mutua was instrumental in the formation, leadership and growth of what is undoubtedly Kenya’s premier independent human rights organisation. He chaired the Commission for many years until he stepped down in 2022 to focus on his role in Mr Odinga’s presidential campaign.

The human rights credentials Prof Mutua brings to his new assignment tick all the rights boxes. However, my friend displays troubling signs of major somersaults since he joined the buffet table. It is during his time as President Ruto’s adviser on Constitutional Affairs and Human Rights that the government deported the Kenya Human Rights Commission's Senior Legal Advisor on Transitional Justice, Martin Mavenjina.

The institution Prof Makau helped birth and nurture described the action as an “extraordinary rendition—illegal, opaque, and deeply troubling”, and part of a general assault on human rights defenders and wider civil society. It might seem that, despite his lofty job title, either the President doesn’t take his advice, or he had no problems with the deportation.

Then there is the fact that Prof Mutua happily serves a regime that continues to insist protests are terrorist actions, or attributes all signs of political dissent to a supposedly ethnic anti-government movement.

One must wonder what an initiative headed by one with demonstrated bias will achieve. In any case, a compensation framework can only come after a serious, impartial and competent investigation.

That task can only be entrusted to an independent commission of inquiry, not a presidential declaration, which might have a pre-determined outcome intended to justify the terrorist tag on those who don’t toe the straight and narrow path of regime praise singers.
