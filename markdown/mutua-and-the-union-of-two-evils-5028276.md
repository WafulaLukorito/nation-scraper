Dr David Ndii, the chairman of the President’s council of economic advisers who unsuccessfully sought to persuade civil society to support William Ruto’s bid in 2022, is having the last laugh.

Last week’s [appointment of law professor Makau Mutua as a senior presidential adviser on constitutional affairs](https://nation.africa/kenya/news/politics/appointment-raila-man-prof-makau-mutua-joins-ruto-s-growing-sea-of-advisers-5023492) reunites two struggle compatriots with sizeable egos, exceptional brilliance and eloquence under the Ruto administration. It potentially recruits a critical opponent of the Ruto regime into its greatest defender.

Five years ago, Prof Mutua articulated the mainstream civil society rejection of Ruto’s candidacy on the basis of his anti-democratic record, unresolved rights violations and odious character references, famously vowing that he would never work for or with the man. The great schism between Dr Ndii and Prof Mutua, if it can be called that, came in the aftermath of the search for a means to stop the political juggernaut that was the Building Bridges Initiative (BBI) to protect the Constitution. A clique of legal scholars and researchers crafted the strategy that provided the backbone of the BBI case in the High Court, with Dr Ndii as lead petitioner.

Civil society’s political response to BBI was to consolidate a platform for the 2022 election. Dr Ndii had visibly run the Raila Odinga campaign in 2017 and was making a return to non-partisan civil society work. To his credit, he disclosed that he was negotiating with different formations. He then performed some moral summersaulting to land on his feet as Ruto’s election strategist.

When the rumour that Dr Ndii had been tasked with delivering civil society to the Ruto camp reached Prof Mutua, then chairman of the Kenya Human Rights Commission (KHRC), he came down heavily on the organisation’s leadership, scuttling the exploratory meetings Dr Ndii had been participating in to craft a platform for negotiations with the main election contenders.

## Two evils

A little-seen struggle over who to support for the election shadowed Prof Mutua’s public repudiation of Ruto’s overtures. In the end, he joined the Raila campaign as its spokesman. It was, in the words of the debate of the moment, the lesser of two evils. Former Chief Justice Willy Mutunga has famously retorted: “The narrative of the lesser of two evils is fraught with political opportunism, ethnicity and rationalisation. Which human being can accept such a choice?”

Prof Mutua’s gift to KHRC was to step down as chairman after a 30-year run, thus avoiding accusations of partisanship against the organisation. Although KHRC is non-partisan, it makes no pretensions to being apolitical. Its mission to defend human rights is political, and its very existence challenges the performance of power at the highest levels.

Anyone who attended the KHRC @30 event last year, or watched it on Youtube, would know that Prof Mutua had been one of the architects of the Coalition for Reforms and Democracy ticket that fronted Raila for President and Kalonzo Musyoka as running mate in the 2013 election.

Another founder member of KHRC, Kiraitu Murungi, resigned his board membership to contest elections in Imenti South. He would become not just Justice minister in the Mwai Kibaki government, but was a key member of the Kitchen Cabinet, and played a significant role in establishing the Kenya National Commission on Human Rights.

According to Rights and Fights: 30 years of the Kenya Human Rights Commission’s Impactful Legacy, the organisation’s leaders —notably its then Executive Director Willy Mutunga and Maina Kiai, among others —were responsible for knocking heads together to produce the elusive opposition unity that had twice cost them the presidency, and resulted in Kanu’s defeat in 2002.

In the present circumstances, the two evils have united and made the political choice that civil society must make much easier. Prof Mutua’s appointment, just like Dr Ndii’s, notwithstanding his frequent obtuse tweets, is backhanded recognition of the intellectual gravitas that the civil society enjoys. Although Dr Ndii is no longer a star attraction at civil society events, those who meet him acknowledge that he blocks a great deal of evil and foolishness from occurring in government. That can only be a good thing.

## Public intellectuals

Celebrated and honest public intellectuals such as these two would ordinarily explain the major changes in their posture and position on important issues. The world is still waiting. It would seem ridiculous to suggest that either of the two have been bought for personal financial gain—until one begins to examine claims that a family member to one of the advisors is at the centre of a controversial technology deal on social health insurance. Nonetheless they both are exemplars of the truism that hubris is a very loud price tag.

Civil society, which is neither a cult nor a religious order from which members cannot depart, must continue to do politics, but with moral clarity and eyes fixed on justice. Attempts to co-opt civil society members into government can open the way for negotiations on how government is perceived in return for incremental social change. It is not, however, a trumpet call to the barricades to retreat or surrender, but creates opportunities for dialogue that has been sorely lacking in the past decade.
