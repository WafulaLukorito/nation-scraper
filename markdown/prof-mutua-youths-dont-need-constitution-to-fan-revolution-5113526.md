## What you need to know:

* To claim that Gen Z's failure lies in constitutional illiteracy is to confuse radical spontaneity with legal deficiency.
* Progressive traditions have long warned against fetishising constitutions as engines of social progress.

Prof Makau Mutua’s *Sunday Nation* column strikes a familiar chord among liberal constitutionalists who romanticise legal texts as the foundation of political transformation. His central claim; that the Gen Z uprising lacks revolutionary potential due to its ignorance of the 2010 Constitution is not only misguided but betrays a deep liberal misunderstanding of how real social change occurs.

Constitutions are not neutral instruments of justice or democratic order; they are, at best, temporary ceasefire documents between antagonistic social/class forces. At worst, they are the juridical straightjackets by which dominant classes enshrine their power under the illusion of consent.

## Neo-colonial misrule

The 2010 Kenyan Constitution, progressive in form and tone, was nonetheless a negotiated compromise designed not to upend authoritarian class rule, but to stabilise it amid a legitimacy crisis. It offered concessions without disrupting the underlying architecture of neo-colonial misrule, including predatory accumulation, wanton expropriation, ethnic patronage, and imperialist subservience.

To claim, therefore, that Gen Z's failure lies in constitutional illiteracy is to confuse radical spontaneity with legal deficiency. The youth are not revolting against a misinterpretation of the law; they are revolting against the material violence the law now conceals. Their protests emerge not from a lack of civics education but from an intuitive, lived rejection of a system that offers nothing but debt, unemployment, police brutality, and moral hypocrisy.

## Permission to revolt

Progressive traditions have long warned against fetishising constitutions as engines of social progress. Lenin, in “State and Revolution”, dismantled the fantasy that bourgeois law could be wielded to abolish the very class system it was built to protect. Progressives politics, for its part, has always seen the state’s legalism as a ritual of containment, designed to disarm democratic fervor through procedural distraction.

What Gen Z understands, perhaps better than many of their elders, is that real change will not come from legal footnotes or policy clauses, but from organising outside and against the institutions that have failed them. Their refusal to be co-opted by NGOism, parliamentary opportunists, or liberal punditry is not a symptom of ignorance, but a sign of strategic instinct. They are not waiting for permission to revolt—they are creating new political languages beyond the sterile confines of constitutional discourse.
