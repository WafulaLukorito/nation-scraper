# Naikuni has what it takes to be Kenya’s next CEO 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-11-20T14:54:12Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/naikuni-has-what-it-takes-to-be-kenya-s-next-ceo--746326  

---

Opinion polls indicate that Prime Minister Raila Odinga would whitewash his opponents if the presidential contest were to be held today. No one comes close. But the election is far away.

A lot could happen between today and that fateful day in August 2012. I have a theory why polls give Mr Odinga such a commanding lead. Perhaps we are looking in the wrong places – and at uninspired choices – for the competition.

Which begs the question – are there some “sleeping dogs” who could give Mr Odinga a run for his money? Methinks so. One such “sleeping dog” is Kenya Airways Group Managing Director and Chief Executive Titus Naikuni.

Mr Naikuni is at the head of a class of younger Kenyan professionals who have transformed Kenya into an African economic powerhouse. Allergic to incompetence, Mr Naikuni is the quintessential model captain of industry.

To him and his band of brothers and sisters, there is no limit to Kenya’s economic potential. They are utterly committed to a greed-free, corruption-free Kenya where merit and performance are the watchwords.

In their world, a person is judged by the content of their character, not the sound of their name, or their accidental birth in a particular tribe. It’s all about what you are, not who you are, or who you know.

I know there is a raging debate about the “type” of leader that Kenya needs under the new dispensation. But the debate has focused more sharply on county governors.

Some have called for “non-politicians” to run the counties. Put differently, some Kenyans would like to see folks with corporate or private sector management experience run key units within the state. Kenya’s CEOs are being asked to consider politics.

I am afraid there won’t be many takers because public service doesn’t pay enough. Nor will there be many opportunities for unjust enrichment – corruption – under the new constitutional dispensation. Which means only the most selfless CEOs may choose to go into politics. I believe Mr Naikuni is one such public servant.

Why Mr Naikuni? First, his educational pedigree is golden. He is a graduate of both the University of Nairobi and the world-famous Harvard Business School. He has engineered the spectacular success of KQ, arguably the best airline in Africa.

He was responsible for turning around the Magadi Soda Company when he served as its CEO. Between 1999 and 2001, Mr Naikuni was a member of the World Bank “Dream Team” – Kenyan technocrats – who were tasked with saving Kenya’s economy. Their success was only prevented by the endemic corruption of the Kanu state.

Mr Naikuni has it all – smarts, experience, charisma and people skills. My crystal ball tells me he could be the person for whom Kenyans could have been waiting.

Besides, in the ethnically-charged atmosphere of Kenyan politics, Mr Naikuni offers the country an opportunity to break the “big-tribe” chokehold on power. He doesn’t come from any of the Big Five – Kikuyu, Luhya, Kalenjin, Luo and Kamba.

As a Maasai, he would break the perennial duel between who, from among the big tribes, would occupy the house on the hill. The impact on the psyche of the nation for such a choice would be incalculable, and may help us defang tribalism.

We just need to look at what choosing leaders from smaller ethnic groups has done for Tanzania. This is not a panacea for tribalism, but it might alleviate ethnic scheming.

In many established democracies, heads of state are chosen from a cadre of leaders who have “run something”. Being a life-long politician is not viewed as the best proving ground for national leadership. That’s partly why rarely Americans send senators or congressmen to the White House.

Before President Barack Obama, the last senator elected president was John F. Kennedy of Massachusetts in 1960. American presidents tend to be former governors of states.

The reason is that governors are regarded as CEOs of complex bureaucracies and gigantic economic units. This is important because the primary function of an American president is to oversee economic prosperity. That’s why American presidents can ill-afford for the economy to tank on their watch.

My argument is that in KQ, Mr Naikuni has run the equivalent of a mini-state. He has proved his worth as an economic steward of one of the “commanding heights” of the Kenyan economy. I suspect – and I know you agree with me – that the next stanza in Kenyan history will be written by economic entrepreneurs.

In the next 20 years, Kenya must become a middle income country, or better. That’s why it needs leaders who understand money, and how to grow the economy. It’s true we have a sparkling new Constitution.

Faithfully executed, that document will clean our politics and give us good government. But that’s only a start because we cannot “eat politics”.

One of the objections to Mr Naikuni could be that although he is a great CEO, he cannot survive in politics. That may be so. Not all CEOs understand politics. In fact, some CEOs think of democracy as the enemy of good business. To them, companies are not democracies. Many run their businesses as dictatorships.

That need not be so, but I have no reason to think that this is Mr Naikuni’s leadership philosophy. Everything I have heard points to his consultative and accessible style.

Do I think he would have a lot to learn should he throw his hat in the ring? Absolutely. But I believe he would be equal to the task. Go Titus.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
