## What you need to know:

* The right to be free is expensive – very, very expensive. People who want freedom are also ready to die for it
* My argument simply is this – freedom’s dear, and it will cost you if you want it
* I don’t mean to give a lesson in philosophy. However, I can’t help but remind Kenyans we can’t do the same thing – over and over – and expect different results

The word “masochist” can have a seedy and craven meaning. I don’t use it in that sense here.

I give it two other related meanings. The first means a person who “is gratified by pain and degradation that are either self-imposed, or imposed by others”. The second, and related to the first, means a person who “finds pleasure in self-denial and submissiveness”.

Don’t you think that describes many – if not most – Kenyans?

This is where I agree – which is rare – with the British imperialist Winston Churchill. He opined famously that the “best argument against democracy is a five-minute conversation with the average voter”. Methinks the nobleman was right. How else could we explain the masochism of Kenyans every five years?

Ever heard of the expression “in a democracy, the people get the government they deserve?” It’s usually incorrectly attributed to the French writer Alexis de Tocqueville. But it’s in fact a corruption of an earlier French writer, Joseph de Maistre, who wrote in 1811 that “every nation has the government it deserves”.

But you get the point – whether or not in a democracy – the type of government reflects the people. I know you think I am “blaming the victim”. Or worse, justifying dictatorship.

Let me assure you – it’s none of the above. I would rather we flipped the argument and asked this – what’s the cost of freedom? The first thing you’ve got to realise is that freedom isn’t cheap.

The right to be free is expensive – very, very expensive. People who want freedom are also ready to die for it. You can’t have freedom at someone else’s expense – you’ve got to be willing to pay the ultimate price for it. That’s why historically citizens of empires have been the freest people on the planet.

That’s because they – and their governments – purchase their freedom at any cost to themselves. You can argue whether this is moral or ethical, but that’s not the point. My argument simply is this – freedom’s dear, and it will cost you if you want it. But there’s more – the cost isn’t abstract or remote. It’s very personal. It’s not some other person’s burden, but yours.

The cost of citizenship – or belonging to a polity – isn’t cost-free. That’s why we treat individual human beings as “creatures of conscience”. Each one with an innate ability to make a rational choice and to exercise “sovereign autonomy”. That’s why we hold dear the legal fiction that all human beings are “formally equal”.

We would become barbarians if we vitiated these simple truths. Nothing would prevent you from being enslaved, tortured, or exploited by those who were stronger, wealthier, and ruthless. Life would be short and brutish for some – to quote Thomas Hobbes the philosopher – and full of bounty for others. This is the question – which life are most Kenyans willing to die for?

I don’t mean to give a lesson in philosophy. However, I can’t help but remind Kenyans we can’t do the same thing – over and over – and expect different results. Every five years – with perhaps one exception – we’ve gone to the polls only to do the same thing.

Three sure things happen at the polls every five years – people are killed, Kenyans vote along tribal lines, and the same crooks are returned to power. The lot of the majority stays the same – impoverished. Isn’t that totally moronic?

Why do Kenyans allow themselves to be manipulated by elites with whom they’ve nothing in common? Why is the average voter so gullible and easily tricked and bamboozled with the predictable tribal card?

**Voters are tribalists**

This is why. I think most voters are tribalists – pure and simple. They’ve drunk the Kool-Aid of their “tribal masters”. If not, why would a destitute Luo or Kikuyu express an unthinking embrace of “their own” and a rejection of “the other”? Don’t get me wrong – it’s not just the Kikuyu and Luo.

It’s everybody. Is the “call of blood” so intoxicating? It’s totally perplexing because the average voter will never get anything – or come near – their tribal overlord. Hell will freeze over before your tribal chief treats you like a human being. You can take that to the bank.

So, why does the average voter engage in this repetitive act of self-mutilation? It is self-hatred? Lack of will?  
I raise these questions because of what I’ve seen about the Ocampo Four. We don’t know whether they are guilty or innocent – that’s for the International Criminal Court to decide.

But we know this – the Waki Commission and the ICC found that they’ve a case to answer. Only time will tell whether there can be smoke without fire. What’s intriguing to me is the fanatical support that the Ocampo Four receive from “their kinsmen”.

You can go to rallies and read blogs – and I guarantee you that the support of Ocampo Four, especially those who’ve expressed an interest in State House, is largely tribal. Like deranged mobs, their supporters don’t want anything to do with justice for the victims.

This brings me back to where I started. It’s true that many Kenyans – not most – fought for democracy.

Many Kenyans still opposed the new Constitution. I truly believe that many Kenyans – perhaps most – hold some fascistic views. They may hate others based on ethnicity, religion, sexual orientation, gender, and political opinion.

When you think in these categories, it’s not out of the realm of probability to surmise that most Kenyans hold undemocratic views. If that’s the case – and I believe many Kenyans are masochists – then we’ve a long way to go. Let’s be honest with ourselves.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at SUNY Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
