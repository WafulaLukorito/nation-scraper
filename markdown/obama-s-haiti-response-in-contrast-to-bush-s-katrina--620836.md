# Obama’s Haiti response in contrast to Bush’s Katrina 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-01-16T14:59:10Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/obama-s-haiti-response-in-contrast-to-bush-s-katrina--620836  

---

On Wednesday, President Barack Obama of the United States called the Haitian earthquake disaster, in which hundreds of thousands may have died, a “cruel and incomprehensible tragedy”.

He ordered that American national assets be deployed to Haiti in “a swift, coordinated and aggressive” action to help Haiti.

Mr Obama said that American efforts must be “leaning forward” to rescue, recover and start rebuilding Haiti. His statement left no doubt that America would take the lead – and act decisively – to assist what is the most impoverished country in the Western hemisphere.

President Obama’s response to Haiti – a foreign country – is in stark contrast to that of President George W. Bush, his predecessor, to Hurricane Katrina which decimated historic New Orleans – an American city – in August 2005. The facts of Katrina are not in doubt.

One of the devastating hurricanes ever, Katrina collapsed the levees that protected New Orleans from storm surges.

The city went under water. Nearly 2,000 residents, mostly African-Americans, were killed. Over one million people, most of them black, were trapped in the most dire and abject conditions. The federal and state governments did nothing. New Orleans became a Third World city in America.

Last week – more than four years after Katrina – I visited New Orleans to see if the city had come back. The effects of that devastation are still visible – dilapidated buildings, ghost streets and a gloomy air.

More than one-third of the population has not returned. Crime rates are dizzyingly high in the city. Corruption is rife. To be sure, New Orleans had huge problems even before the hurricane.

However, even though it is trying hard to come back, the city remains mired in a swamp of despair and bureaucratic ineptitude.

It’s anyone’s guess whether the city known as The Big Easy for its many pleasures – jazz, great food and distinct culture – will ever fully recover.

But what was shocking to Americans and the world was the silence and inaction of the Bush Administration to the Katrina disaster.

Here was a government that could invade Iraq within days and topple the Taliban in Afghanistan in the blink of an eye but yet refused to mobilise its superior assets to rescue New Orleanians.

The total disregard of the plight of New Orleans by Mr Bush was chilling to most Americans. It shocked the conscience of the world that the most powerful nation on earth could treat its citizens like trash.

Why did President Bush not give a damn? Was it bureaucratic inertia and incompetence, as some claimed, or were dark motives at work?

It was not lost on anyone that the victims of Katrina were black and poor. The government’s apathy and the failure of the larger population to demand immediate action on the part of Mr Bush could only be explained by one word: Racism.

The whole sordid mess painted a grotesque picture of the cruelty of racism and the colour of poverty in the United States – black. Most shocking was the open exhibition of racism at the highest levels of the American Government. It was utterly inexcusable.

The legacy of Katrina, and the large blot that it left on the American conscience, will live forever. But it also now frames how the government reacts to disasters in the United States and abroad.

Katrina partly explains why President Obama has moved so quickly and decisively to respond to the tragedy in Haiti. It cannot be lost on anyone that Mr Obama – a black man – has ordered American military and other rescue assets to be deployed immediately in the Haitian theatre.

Contrast that to an inert and unconcerned Mr Bush – a white man – of his neglect of the mostly black Katrina victims. No pictorial could be more compelling.

It would be disingenuous for anyone to pretend that the racial ancestries of the two men – Mr Bush’s and Mr Obama’s – have nothing to do with the empathy, or lack thereof, with respect to disaster responses.

Their philosophies on the role of government would also play a part in how they understand state intervention.

My point is not that black men will always be empathetic with other blacks. If that was the case we would be unable to explain black-on-black violence, including the post-election killings in Kenya in 2008.

Nor would it be correct to say that whites are always indifferent to black plight. By the same token, it is a fact that there is white apathy to black plight and poverty in the United States.

Many analysts believe that white apathy was partially responsible for Mr Bush’s inaction during Katrina. In contrast, Mr Obama’s worldview, in which he sees humanity as one, compels him to respond to the Haitian tragedy as though it was an American catastrophe.

Mr Obama’s Haiti response is a clear sign of the Obama Doctrine in foreign policy. Simply put, the Obama Doctrine does not tether American interests solely to the rich and more strategic countries.

The doctrine is anchored in not just the traditional “hard” military and commercial interests. Human lives – even of poor Haitians – matter to President Obama. Change is coming to Washington.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
