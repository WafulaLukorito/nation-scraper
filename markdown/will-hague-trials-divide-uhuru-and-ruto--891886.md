# Will Hague trials divide Uhuru and Ruto?

## What you need to know:

* They believed the only way to beat the ICC was to capture the Kenyan state, and use it as a lever against the world court.
* It’s indisputable that the most barbaric attacks occurred in the Rift Valley, which is inhabited by the Kikuyu and the Kalenjin.

President Uhuru Kenyatta and Deputy President William Ruto are about to go down in the history books.

Never mind they did so already, but they’ll do it again – this time with perhaps more momentous consequences.

The pair that sits atop the Kenyan state was the first in the world to be elected while under indictment for crimes against humanity. The duo will make history again when its stands trial at The Hague while in office.

Mr Ruto will blaze the trail when he sits at the dock on September 10.

Barring any unforeseen surprises, Mr Kenyatta will follow suit on November 12.

But Mr Kenyatta and Mr Ruto are on opposite sides. Question – will the trials put them asunder?

Methinks the International Criminal Court may pose a unity test for the Kenyatta and Ruto camps.

There’s no doubt Mr Kenyatta and Mr Ruto came together for political convenience – and expediency. They are living proof of American founding father Ben Franklin’s famous quip – “we must all hang together, or assuredly we shall hang separately”.

They believed the only way to beat the ICC was to capture the Kenyan state, and use it as a lever against the world court.

But the ICC has proved more dogged than they anticipated. All efforts to derail the trials have come a cropper. The impending detailed evidence of the post-election violence will pose a great challenge to supporters of both men.

The violence in the wake of the 2007 elections erupted because of disputed presidential election results.

Mr Kenyatta supported former President Mwai Kibaki’s PNU over ODM’s leader Raila Odinga. Mr Ruto was Mr Odinga’s Kalenjin whip. It’s indisputable that the most barbaric attacks occurred in the Rift Valley, which is inhabited by the Kikuyu and the Kalenjin. The only questions – factual and legal – were who planned, orchestrated, ordered, and executed the attacks.

The ICC believes that culpability lies with the two men.

This much is clear – over 1,300 were murdered, 600,000 displaced, thousands raped and maimed, and millions worth of property pillaged. This is also clear – the victims have been forgotten.

But ICC Chief Prosecutor Fatou Bensouda says the victims aren’t forgotten. The indomitable Gambian has a steely resolve. She is an unbending champion of victims. It’s true that she’s publicly acknowledged that some witnesses against Mr Kenyatta and Mr Ruto have recanted. She’s openly charged that the witnesses are being sabotaged – bribed, intimidated, and disappeared.

I know that there’s jubilation by both Mr Kenyatta’s and Mr Ruto’s camps every time a witness pulls out.

But, if I were in their shoes, I wouldn’t celebrate – yet. It would be unwise to underestimate what’s in Ms Bensouda’s quiver. Let’s wait for the trials to play out.

Which brings me to the political risks that the trials pose for the marriage of convenience between Mr Ruto and Kenyatta.

It can’t be gainsaid that President Kenyatta and DP Ruto – who are political allies in office – will be legal adversaries at the ICC. It’s no secret that witnesses against Mr Ruto – and the evidence against him – will seek to prove how he committed crimes against humanity against them.

In return, the witnesses against Mr Kenyatta will adduce evidence that he organised attacks against Mr Odinga’s ODM supporters. Most were Kalenjin, Luo, Kisii, and Luhya.

The Kenyatta-Ruto axis has been built on the “reconciliation” between the Kikuyu and the Kalenjin.

But it’s not lost on students of Kenyan history that “reconciling” elites isn’t the same thing as healing the wounds of historical injustices against communities.

We know that at the fundament, the basic contradictions between the Kikuyu and the Kalenjin remain – no matter what elites say. That’s because the volatile question of land remains unresolved.

Make no mistake – cosmetic shows of unity can’t extinguish real grievances at the grassroots. I don’t know the answers to these questions, but peace activists need to swing into action.

Any astute analyst knows that Mr Ruto’s one – and only – ambition is to become President. He regards his sojourn in the Office of Deputy President as a temporary way station.

But my guess is that the man from Gatundu is acutely aware of Mr Ruto’s designs on his office. Which begs the question – does Mr Ruto have the patience to wait until 2022 when Mr Kenyatta would presumably finish his second, and final, term?

Or would Mr Ruto jump the Jubilee ship in 2017 and challenge Mr Kenyatta? I’d say all this depends on how the ICC trials go.

The best outcome for their political marriage is an acquittal. The worst is a conviction of one. My crystal ball tells me a conviction of one, or both, would collapse Jubilee like a house of cards.

I clearly don’t know who the key strategists for the two men are, but I bet the scenarios I intuit must be cause for worry.

Their allies in The National Alliance and the URP shouldn’t rest easy.
