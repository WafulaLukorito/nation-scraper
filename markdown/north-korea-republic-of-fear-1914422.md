## What you need to know:

* Even as Kenyans fight over the contours of democracy, they must thank their lucky stars that they don’t live in North Korea.
* Western intelligence services have spent gazillions trying to figure out North Korea, the most mysterious state on the planet.

“I was restless with yearning to write realist poetry based on what I saw, and not loyalist poetry based on what we were all told to see.”

Those words speak volumes about the vitality of human conscience — the predicate of a free society. They come from Dear Leader: My Escape from North Korea, a heart-stopping tale of misery and bravery of two privileged North Koreans who flee their country hoping to reach the green pastures of South Korea. Written by Jang Jin-Sung, the exile’s pen name, the book reveals a state and elite so cruel it makes your skin crawl. North Korea is the most closed and repressive state on the planet. The country is a republic of fear.

Even as Kenyans fight over the contours of democracy — which often seems non-existent — they must thank their lucky stars that they don’t live in North Korea. I thought it wasn’t possible to live in such a miserable country until I read and reflected on this bestseller from a “fugitive” insider of the North Korean gulag state.

Moreover, I speak as someone who has worked on some of the most desperate human rights situations around the world. North Korea is a warning to Kenyans. Elites can be ogres bent on the consumption of humans.

That’s why every citizen needs to have a healthy distrust of elites and the republic. Love your country, but be skeptical about the ruling elites.

## The suffering masses

I had read *Nineteen Eight-Four*, the dystopian novel by English writer George Orwell, and thought that such a state couldn’t exist in reality. I was wrong. That state is North Korea. In George Orwell’s stomach churner, the Party, through the Thought Police, rules the state of Oceania where Big Brother holds absolute power built on a mythic cult of personality. Nothing happens without his knowledge.

Through omnipotent surveillance, the “Orwellian” state disappears facts through propaganda, group-think, and manipulation. You are taught not to believe your “lying eyes”. What you see doesn’t really exist. The elite builds an alternate reality that justifies and cordons it from the suffering masses. Common people are resigned to their miserable fates.

Western intelligence services have spent gazillions trying to figure out North Korea, the most mysterious state on the planet. But it’s virtually impossible to get human intelligence because all North Koreans and foreigners are intensely watched. You can’t go to the bathroom without someone knowing about it. What you read, or hear, is strictly controlled. You need a written permit to go from one town, or region, to another. Defiance is punished without pity. “Deviants” are sent to camps of hard labour, and often ruined for life. Everybody spies on everybody.

Jang Jin-Sung lived as an insider in this world. His book is one of the most revealing exposes of a corrupt, demonic state. It is a chilling account.

Jang reveals the byzantine nature of the North Korean regime. Built on the cults of Kim Il-Sung and his son Kim Jong-Il — known as Dear Leader or the General — this family dynasty thrives through a claque of insiders. Their survival, and that of the system, depends on the continuation in power of the family dynasty.

Kim Jong-Un, the current dictator, inherited this state. The nerve centre of the state was originally the military, but Kim Jong-Il flipped the script and usurped power from his father — state founder Kim Il-Sung — by making the military subordinate to the Workers’ Party.

Kim Jong-Il took power from the aging patriarch while internationally creating the false impression that his father was still in charge.

## Foster sympathy

As they say, you can’t make this stuff up. The offices and departments of the North Korean state are eerily named. For a formulaic example, Jang Jin-Sung worked in Office 101 of the United Front Department (UFD). The Central Party offices are all located in a highly secure and secretive of part of Pyongyang, the capital city. The UFD was a propaganda department whose purpose was to oversee inter-Korean espionage, policy-making, and diplomacy.

Here, Jang and his colleagues created propaganda to foster sympathy for North Korea in South Korea. The idea was to finally triumph over the South and take it over.

Slowly, but progressively, as Jang rose in the hierarchy of the state, he came to see through the hollow lies of the North Korean regime. There was poverty all around him, even in Pyongyang. Power blackouts were the norm. Even food rations were given out according to rank and station.

When the state became bankrupt, the rations stopped nationwide, except for top party and regime cadres. For how long would Jang live these lies? He once saw an impoverished woman sell her ten-year-old daughter in the open market for 10 won, or ten US cents. It broke his heart.

On another occasion, when Jang visited his native town, he noticed a macabre scene. People were lying everywhere, either dead or at various stages of death due to starvation.

He noticed officials from the Corpse Department walking with sticks poking at bodies to see who was dead and who wasn’t. The contrast between this scene and his meeting with Dear Leader Kim Jong-Il can only be described as otherworldly. The Dear Leader had summoned him for recognition for a poem he had written in worship of the dictator. The dining was exquisite and the drinks world-class. Jang would become one of the “Admitted”, a tiny circle of elites that had met and dined with Kim Jong-Il. He was in.

However, matters took a turn for the worse. Jang makes the mistake of lending a forbidden book from UFD to his friend Young-min. Young-min was himself an insider musician for the band that played for the Dear Leader.

When Young-min loses the book and it is found in Jang’s back pack, the two know their geese are cooked. They flee North Korea undercover and cross the river into China.

## Fleeing from justice

North Korea informs the Chinese government that the two are fugitive murderers fleeing from justice. For weeks, the two live a life of penury on the run from Chinese authorities.

They spend many nights in the snowy outdoors without shelter or food. Eventually, they split up thinking strangers were more likely to help one, not two people.

The Chinese catch Young-min who commits suicide by jumping off a cliff rather than be returned to North Korea. Eventually, Jang makes contact with South Korean intelligence agencies in Beijing. They take him to Seoul, South Korea, where he has been a citizen since.

What Jang exposes to the world is a state built on deception and misdirection at home and abroad. He demonstrates how North Korea blackmails South Korea to get food and investments and deceives the United States about its intentions. In the process, the family dynasty has built a nuclear state amid starvation and abject poverty.

Perhaps the Confucian worldview, which can be exploited by states bent on abusing concepts of obedience, respect, and social stability, may partly explain why the people haven’t risen against the Kim Dynasty. It’s clear from Jang’s book that the state ruthlessness — public executions of senior military and party leaders, torture of common citizens, and exiles to gulags — plays a large role in silencing dissent.

Kim Jong-Un has a co-dependent relationship with the elites in the Workers’ Party and the military. However, ruthless though he may be, he is clearly not ruling alone. It is a syndicate that runs a criminal state. The survival of the elites depends on the survival of the Kim Dynasty. If the dynasty falls — and a Kim isn’t in charge — the whole deck of cards will crumble.
