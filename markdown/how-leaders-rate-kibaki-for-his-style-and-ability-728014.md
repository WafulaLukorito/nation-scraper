# How leaders rate Kibaki for his style and ability

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2004-12-25T21:00:00Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/news/how-leaders-rate-kibaki-for-his-style-and-ability-728014  

---

President Kibaki is in the spotlight at the end of his second year in office, with fellow politicians and leaders in business and civil society pitching with views on his performance.

Some of them are unhappy with what they term "his laid-back style", blaming it for "political drift of the government in the last two years".

Initially, this was explained away by the President's early indisposition and hospitalisation, something which critics complain allowed aides to take charge of things and alienate increasingly disgruntled coalition partners. Critics have criticised Kibaki's laid back style of leadership but political observers say that Kibaki is carving a style of his own.

Where some see a plodding and indecisive leader, others suspect there could be a more clever and calculating mind at work. Certainly he shows no stomach to confront his opponents frontally, but his silent way of operating has the effect of steadily tiring them off after they realise the commotion they are making has absolutely no visible effect on him.

Cracking the whip against his rivals and critics at this early stage when he has not completely consolidated himself seems out of the question. At any rate the President apppears keenly aware of this as he has so far resisted firing his opponents in the cabinet who belong to the LDP wing.

This has echoes of what Moi himself did immediately after taking over from Kenyatta; powerful ministers who were known to have been ambivalent to him like Mbiyu Koinange, Jackson Angaine, Paul Ngei and James Gichuru were left in place until they either died or lost their seats through the ballot.

While some have praised President Kibaki's style, terming it "democratic and fostering freedom of speech", there are concerns his henchmen may be capitalising on his "deep slumber" to propagate their own selfish agendas.

Factional warfare, says human rights activist Prof Makau Mutua, has destroyed Narc as a reformist force, a fate sealed by President Kibaki’s failure to offer bold and visionary leadership.

The *Sunday Nation* spoke to a number of leaders from the spheres of politics, religion, academia, trade unionism and civil society and the general feeling is that while he has allowed his ministers a much freer rein than either of his two predecessors, the president needs to be more active.

Some of the people we spoke to felt it was time for President Kibaki to crack his whip on those in his cabinet who don't toe the line.

**Kanu publicity secretary Shariff Nassir** said: **"**I just pity Kibaki. He has heaped his government with a lot of people with quite different ideas from his. His only problem is he has given his ministers a lot of power. He should never have done that. We leaders are power-hungry. We are like hyenas when it comes to power. Kenyatta and Moi had authority over their ministers. Kibaki must now face the issue head-on.''

Kanu's **William Ruto,** once an influential minister under Moi, says "there is something amiss" with Kibaki's leadership style.

"He needs to soil his hands and do the job. He must not just be seen to do the job but must do it. He should get much more involved."

He laments the President has given his ministers too much freedom. "The act of government is not together. The Cabinet needs to exercise democracy as a tool of governance. It should not create confusion."

He would like to see the President "get his act together".

"The country requires direction and leadership. As things stand now, there is a lot of guessing on what is going on. The President has a duty to delegate responsibilities. The ministers must in turn exercise their power in the best interest of the country."

However, cabinet ministers Mukhisa Kituyi and Amos Kimunya see matters quite differently.

Speaking to the *Sunday Nation,* Dr Kituyi explained that by delegating power and responsibility, the President had enabled his ministers to perform better than ever before. "His cabinet has achieved much more in two years (than any other in the past)."

Lands Minister **Amos Kimunya** explained: **"**There is difference between power and responsibility. The President has delegated a lot of responsibilities, like every good manager should do. We [cabinet ministers] are all accountable to him individually. That's the good thing he has done for Kenya. I don't think any of us is abusing power."

On allegations the President is indecisive and slow to act, Mr Kimunya said: "Good leaders are patient. They take time to act. President Kibaki is not slow; he is a fast thinker and actor. He may know your intentions even before you tell him anything. But he will not cut you short. He will give you time to explain yourself. He does not want to act from the podium as some people expect him to."

There are others still who feel it is not yet time to draw comparisons between President Kibaki and his predecessors, both of whom he served under.

**Constitution of Kenya Review Commission secretary Patrick Lumumba** describes the President as "laid-back", but says it is too early to compare his style with that of either President Moi or President Kenyatta.

"To be honest, we can only compare the first two years of Moi's presidency with two years of Kibaki presidency."

Mr Lumumba says the President has succeeded in delegating responsibilities to his ministers. "This has served to demystify the presidency to some extent, which is necessary. But one would have loved to see his style supplemented by a strengthening of his institutions. A new constitution by now would have done that."

On the President's strengths and weaknesses, Mr Lumumba says: "I believe there are emerging strengths in his style of leadership. One of them is the ability to delegate responsibilities. But delegation must never be confused with abdication. He must remain in control. The buck stop with him."

Mr Lumumba added that "the President should be more inspiring to create hope among the jobless and desperate youth. He must keep on telling Kenyans there is a promised land. I don't think Kibaki has been sufficiently present in Kenyans' lives."

He says the President should not give his ministers so much power. "Power is non-delegable. The President must always hold his ministers on a tight leash. The ministers are repositories of delegated authority which they must exercise with humility for the best interests of the country."

Former assistant ministers **Grace Ogot** and **Reuben Chesire** are satisfied President Kibaki is on the right track. Mrs Ogot, of Siaya, blames "some ministers letting the President down by quarrelling among themselves. They should stop this. President Kibaki does not travel a lot, but his presence is felt all over the country. He has created proper linkage with villagers, especially through the bursaries and free health care services. He may not be everywhere, but the gift he hands to the people reaches them through their MPs. Every village is now enjoying bursaries established by the government. That is Kibaki's extended hand.''

Former Eldoret North MP Chesire concurs: "People expect the President to be everywhere. Leadership does not mean being with the people all the time. Kenyans were used to iron-fist leadership but Kibaki has given us a lot of freedom. There is less interference in people's affairs. He has allowed democracy to flourish. But people tend to see this as a weakness.''

Two key church both said this was neither the time nor the occasion to speak out on the President's style because "Christmas is the season of goodwill".

**Anglican Archbishop Nzimbi** and **Nairobi Catholic Archbishop Ndingi Mwana a'Nzeki** told the *Sunday Nation* (in separate interviews) they would not comment on the President's style.

Archbishop Nzimbi did not want to get involved in what he termed a controversial issue. "I better give the President some rest this time of the year. We should be sending messages of goodwill and peace".

Archbishop Ndingi said: "I would not want to comment on this issue right now. Politicians have different styles of leadership.''

However, **Chief Kadhi Hamad Kassim, PCEA moderator David Githii, PCEA general secretary Samuel Muriguh, African Independent Pentecostal Churches of Kenya Archbishop John Mugecha, and Independent Churches of Kenya Archbishop John Kivuli** did not mind making their views on the President's style known.

Chief Kadhi **Hamad Kassim** said "it's too early to fairly judge President Kibaki's leadership style". However, he praised the President for "giving wananchi (the public) freedom of speech. Under Moi people lived in fear. Unlike Moi and Kenyatta, Kibaki seems to allow his own cabinet to express themselves even if what they say does not please him. I admire him for that."

**Archbishop Kivuli** commended the President for "giving his ministers a lot of freedom to prove themselves. It is unfortunate some do not know what they are doing. He should take them for training. Kenyatta was firm and tough. You could not bully him. Moi was a strong dictator. But he was out of touch with the people because he relied very much on his sycophants."

He continued: "During Moi's administration, people were not used to freedom. Kibaki has now changed all that."

The Right-Rev Githii admires President Kibaki's's ability to delegate power. "Kibaki is good in delegating power. That is part of a good leader. But his major weakness is that he takes too long to comment on urgent issues. This gives space for things to take the wrong direction. This is not to say that he should comment on absolutely everthing in the manner of his predecessor (Moi), but as soon as an important issue emerges he should come in to give guidance and direction.''

**The Right-Rev Muriguh said that** unlike Moi, "Kibaki's objectives are very clear. He is focused and consistent in what he does, despite what happens or what people say. His ability to distribute work among his ministers is admirable. But he should strengthen his supervisory role. Some of his ministers are not doing what they should."

The Right-Rev Muriguh said that "unlike Moi and Kenyatta, Kibaki is a bit tolerant. However, he seems very slow to act on certain issues. He should face issues head-on and give guidance. We don't want him to *payuka* (ramble) like Moi used to, but he should have a system to help him talk on certain issues in good time. We are missing him on that. We need to hear him speak on national issues, like the Carnivore tragedy."

Nyeri Catholic **Bishop John Njue,** former chairman of the **Kenya Episcopal Conference,** says Kenyans are now experiencing the freedom that should have existed during the Kenyatta and Moi regimes.

"We have now grown up. Kibaki's policy of delegation of power and his style of handling criticism is very positive. He is more attentive to reason than emotion. Kenyans were used to a presidency that was in the frontline . . . directing almost everything. We now have a presidency that has put people in the frontline."

He defends the President from accusations that he is not fully in charge: "Delegating power to his ministers is a sign that the President is in control. But the ministers are facing tremendous challenges because in the past no minister could make independent decisions."

But Archbishop Mugecha sees "total confusion in Kibaki leadership. He seems to have given people a lot of freedom. Kenyatta used to be tough. Moi was a dictator. But we don't know if he (President Kibaki) will act during the last year of his term."

He laments the President should not have given his ministers so much power. "Kibaki has given us a lot of freedom, which we lacked during the Moi administration. But absolute freedom is unproductive."

Cotu **secretary general Francis Atwoli** commends the President because his "non-interference policy on established institutions is admirable. He respects other people's opinions. He is accessible when required."

Said Mr Atwoli: "The President cannot be accused of being out of touch when he has delegated authority to his ministers. Moi and Kenyatta were politicians all their lives. Kibaki is an administrator. Our country can only prosper through application of modern administration ethics."

**Kenya National Union of Teachers secretary general Francis Ng'ang'a** praises PresidentKibaki for giving Kenyans freedom. "Under Kibaki's leadership people do not have much to complain about. I believe leadership is not a one-man business. The education sector is stable. Now teachers are not complaining."

**Suntra Investment Bank managing director James Murigu** thinksit is too early to judge the President but commends him for delegating power to his ministers. However, he says "some ministers do not appreciate what they have been given."

Mr Murigu urges the President to "delegate but don't lose control".

**Kenya Association of Manufacturers former chairman Manga Mugwe** says the President "needs to be open and more accessible to the people by going out to interact with them. Though he has officials who update him on what happens on the ground, I think he gets sieved information. He should meet people from various disciplines to get views of the common mwananchi."

**Kanu acting secretary general Julius Sunkuli** describedPresident Kibaki as "a very private person . . . he does not seem to enjoy appearing in public. His ideas and vision are a matter of conjecture. Nobody knows what they are or what he stands for, and the idea in which he lives. Unlike Moi, it is clear who Kibaki's friends are.

"The President has given his ministers so much power that it is not easy to refer to his administration as Kibaki's government because he seems to play a very small role in it."

However, Mr Sunkuli adds: "Kibaki is likeable person. I just admire his past. He was a lovely debator in Parliament. We don't see that any more. Perhaps as people get older they lose their sense of humour."

**Taveta MP Naomi Shaaban (Kanu**) laments that "some of the ministers make the President look ineffective and not in control. There is no collective responsibility. But the problem is the President. He has given them so much freedom. He should now take control and make sure they toe the line."

**Gatundu North MP Kariuki Muiruri (Kanu)** says:"Kenyatta would not entertain nonsense, rubbish, lies or reckless talk. Kibaki is so cool and does not panic. But he has to get rid of people who are messing his administration by making reckless statements. He should reshuffle the cabinet and bring in working rather than talking ministers."

**Nominated MP Franklin Bett (LDP)** urges the President to "drop his hands-off policy."

Said Mr Bett: "He ought to remove hostility among the ruling coalition leaders. He can only do this by being involved as the father of the nation. Kenyatta and Moi never allowed their ministers to quarrel among themselves. They were hands-on presidents."

He continued: "Kibaki should not interfere with everything like Moi used to. But he should now start touring the country to meet the people. He must be impartial and be seen with even LDP ministers going round urging Kenyans to work hard. He must not be seen to be working only with NAK ministers as this only adds to animosity between the coalition partners."

**Maendeleo ya Wanawake chairperson Zipporah Kitony** says: "Kibaki should know Kenyans love leaders who are closer to them. The President has overstretched his hand to his ministers. He has given them so much power. Some are now embarrassing him. He should now tame them."

**Prof Edward Oyugi,** a former detainee under Moi, says that "Kibaki's leadership style is the best at the current political dispensation. He inherited a country which had no freedom of expression. He is right in giving his ministers so much powers and freedom to learn. The fact that he has given them room to do what they want is no reason to say he is not in control."

**Architectural Association of Kenya chairman Gitau Mungai** contrasts Moi and Kibaki styles:

"Moi was a hands-on man. But his ministers would not make independent decisions. Kibaki is not a hands-on man but he inherited the same people. They now have freedom but they cannot move because they were not used to making independent decisions. Kibaki has not been a grassroots person. I don't think he quite understands what happens on the ground. He is just a lucky man; being at the right place at the right time."

**Baringo North MP William Boit** urges the President to take charge of his government. "It appears Kibaki is not in control of his ministers. They behave as if they are running a parallel government. There is no collective responsibility."
