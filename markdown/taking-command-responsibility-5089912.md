I’ve been provoked to write this column because of the [murder in police custody of blogger Albert Ojwang](https://nation.africa/kenya/news/albert-ojwang-arrested-at-home-found-dead-400km-away--5074146). The 2010 Constitution isn’t a piece of bathroom paper, it’s the legal foundation of the State. There’s no power, known or unknown, that’s not granted in the Constitution.

If a power, whether explicit or implicit, can’t be grounded in the Constitution, then such power doesn’t exist, including in ordinary laws. No official within the State, or a private individual, can claim a right or power that cannot be found in the Basic Law.

I say this knowing that the liberal constitution, or any law within the liberal milieu, permits all that which it doesn’t prohibit. Heads must roll for Mr Ojwang’s killing.

In April, [President William Ruto](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/william-samoei-ruto-3809570) appointed me his Senior Advisor on Constitutional Affairs. I gratefully accepted the appointment because it’s a great honour to serve my native Kenya. As Kenyans know, I’ve lived virtually all my adult life in the United States. I am a scholar of the law and human rights.

My commitment is to help President Ruto in his avowed commitment to create a legal and social environment where every Kenyan has the best opportunity to realize their potential. President Ruto has often said we don’t have a single human being to waste, or leave behind. In that context, I’ve been deeply upset by Mr Ojwang’s killing. His murder must be the last at the hands of our police.

Mr Ojwang’s killing has ignited a furor of rancorous national debate about impunity, big-manism, and command responsibility.

## Consumer spending

Let me state it clearly and without equivocation upfront: no society in human history has ever become great without an elite, which understands that a nation’s vitality is only possible when the littlest among us is given a chance to thrive. Even the greatest economy of the world is not driven by multinational corporations or the federal government of the United States.

No – the American economy is anchored by small businesses and consumer spending. Consumer spending itself is possible because common folk have money in their pockets. And they have that money in the pocket because they’ve ample opportunities to earn it.

I don’t want to preach, and that’s not my intention. But it would be a dereliction of my duty if I didn’t use my learning to point out a more enlightened path. This is the deal. First, I want to point to the notion present in all responsible societies of public shame — a concept that’s about self-ostracisation.

This is an act of proverbial self-beheading where a public official bows his head in shame and takes responsibility for negligence, criminality, or the failure to act with propriety. This is very common in Europe, the United States, and parts of Asia. It’s, in a manner of speaking, an official falling on their own sword. This concept is alien to Kenya.

In Kenya, we have many individuals who shouldn’t hold public office. Some of them have been convicted in a court of law for very serious crimes yet they cling to power. Others have been convicted in the court of opinion, but they remain tone deaf, squatting in public office. This is the culture that we must stamp out to become a great people.

In Mr Ojwang’s case, two legal doctrines are relevant. The first is command responsibility, also known as superior responsibility, developed in the context of war crimes and is a derivative of the older common and civil law doctrine of “respondeat superior”, or the master-servant rule, in which the employer is vicariously liable for the misdeeds of their agents or servants.

## Crime

In criminal law, command responsibility requires that a superior officer who orders a junior to commit a crime is guilty of the offence committed by the junior. For culpability, the senior officer doesn’t have to explicitly order his junior to commit a wrongful act. He’s guilty if he knew, or should have known, that the junior officer would commit the wrongful act either because he thought his superior wanted him to do so, or not.

Because of the power differential, the superior bears a heavy burden. This principle assures that junior officers won’t simply be fed to meat grinders to carry the crosses of their superiors while the latter go scot-free. This is what intelligent societies usually do.

The second doctrine is that of individual criminal responsibility, which really is a corollary of command responsibility. Junior officers aren’t automatons without a conscience or personal agency. If a junior officer knows, or should’ve known, that a superior’s order was illegal, then he should refuse to carry out that order.

If he does, then he’s guilty as well. Impunity can’t be stamped out unless individual criminal responsibility is exacted on he who pulls the trigger and kills another at the instruction of a superior. I rest my case.
