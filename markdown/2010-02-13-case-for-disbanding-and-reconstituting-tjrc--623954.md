# Case for disbanding and reconstituting TJRC 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-02-13T15:59:07Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/case-for-disbanding-and-reconstituting-tjrc--623954  

---

Both my mind and heart tell me that the Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission must be disbanded and reconstituted. It’s clear to all and sundry that the TJRC has gone over the cliff and cannot possibly be retrieved.

This is not easy for me to say because I have worked hard, including as chair of the truth commission task force, to make the TJRC a reality. But I have an empty feeling because this is not the TJRC I fought for, or expected.

With all due respect, the composition of the TJRC is utterly wanting.

Except for Betty Murungi, no other commissioner has the moral stature and political pedigree to sit on the TJRC. The credibility gap of the TJRC is so wide that we must start anew.

So, what to do – do we throw the baby out with the bath water? Are there corrective measures that can cure its crisis of public confidence? I do not think so.

A complete collapse of the entire exercise seems unavoidable at this point. Defiant utterances by Bethuel Kiplagat, the commission’s chair, only add fuel to the raging inferno.

The TJRC should be putting Kenya’s sordid history in the dock. But, paradoxically, it is the TJRC itself that has been in the dock by the public. It’s wrong for the truth commission to blame the public and civil society for its woes.

That’s called scapegoating. It doesn’t – and won’t – work. The most important asset of a truth commission is the legitimacy it derives from its moral standing and public confidence. Otherwise, it is a mockery of the public.

That’s why a truth commission that starts off by defending its credibility is DOA – dead on arrival. It’s akin to drinking from a poisoned chalice. I am not gloating, but my task force was enthusiastically received wherever we travelled across the country. No one ever walked out on us.

Kenyans wanted a truth commission, but a credible one, not an institution with suspect characters. I see four key problems with the TJRC.

First, the commission has an image problem. It has failed to capture the pain and suffering of the people.

The commission has not articulated a legitimate purpose for its existence. Mr Kiplagat, the white-haired former diplomat, only talks about “listening, reconciliation, and forgiveness” every time he opens his mouth.

This is the wrong message because it lacks “truth and justice”.

Mr Kiplagat doesn’t understand that “truth and justice” are conditions precedent for “reconciliation”.

He doesn’t manifest anger about past abuses and cannot convince Kenyans that he understands that nothing is more important than “justice.” This is why Mr Kiplagat is the wrong face for the TJRC.

Second, the commission has failed to show that it feels the pain of the victims of human rights violations, and that its main objective is to establish a moral account of Kenya’s abusive history so that perpetrators can be held accountable.

The truth commission has not underscored the fact that it is a victim-centred device. Right now it looks like a whitewash. It is a stinging indictment when members of the public repeatedly walk out on the truth commission.

I bet that the TJRC would be physically attacked were it to show up at Wagalla, the site of one of the most gruesome pogroms in the country’s history. The TJRC has been unable to publicly embrace victims.

Third, the composition of the commission must change. It is not enough for the commission to say that its members were vetted in a public process.

Obviously that argument does not wash with the public. Mr Kiplagat cannot be the face of the TJRC given the charges of complicity in the Moi regime that have been levelled against him.

He has become an albatross around the commission’s neck, and must step aside. The people have rejected him because they believe that he is tainted. He should not chair the TJRC. Members of a truth commission must be beyond suspicion and reproach.

Mr Kiplagat and a number of his commissioners fail this test hands down. Furthermore, most of the commissioners are virtual unknowns without stellar histories in the struggle for justice. That’s why a recomposition is unavoidable.

Lastly, the TJRC began on a non sequitur, a meaningless exercise at the Coast Province. It was unwise to go around “introducing” itself to the public and collecting their views.

My task force – and lately civil society – conducted public civic education necessary for a truth commission.

Rather than waste time chasing ghosts, the commission should have immediately embarked on public hearings starting with the most critical violations in the country’s history – the Wagalla Massacre, the murder of JM Kariuki, or the Robert Ouko assassination.

This would have glued Kenyans to their TVs and radios to catch the testimony of victims and perpetrators. The nation would have been riveted, earning the commission credibility in the eyes of the public.

I don’t offer these views lightly. But I see no other choice but to completely disband and reconstitute the TJRC. Let’s pull the plug now without any further delay.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
