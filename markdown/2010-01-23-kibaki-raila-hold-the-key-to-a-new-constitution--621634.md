# Kibaki, Raila hold the key to a new constitution 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-01-23T10:53:20Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/kibaki-raila-hold-the-key-to-a-new-constitution--621634  

---

The chance that Kenya will get a new constitution this year went from possible to probable when the PSC agreed this week on the presidential model for the executive. But we are not out of the woods yet. We should quit beating about the bush, and call a spade by its true name. Only a final agreement between two men can give Kenya a new constitution.

The two men – President Mwai Kibaki and Prime Minister Raila Odinga – have the power and leverage to snap their fingers and make the constitution a reality. The Naivasha meeting has value but I know enough about the nature of the political class in Kenya to conclude that neither the PSC, nor any group of MPs, can broker a lasting deal on the constitution.

This is not to say that the PSC is a fraud, although I am concerned about its cut and paste job on the CoE draft. God should save us all when the PSC mistakenly thinks that its job is to draft the constitution! Its “real” job is to “legitimise” the “process” by capturing the agreement between President Kibaki and Prime Minister Odinga.

Neither the PSC, nor Parliament, are the real decision-makers. They don’t – and can’t – call the final shots. That’s why folks should have taken Kalonzo Musyoka, the Vice-President, and his fellow PNU brigands, seriously when they “rejected” the “hybrid” executive. Mr Musyoka is a mouthpiece for Mr Kibaki’s wishes.

But why would Mr Kibaki and the PNU want to retain an imperial presidency, and oppose a parliamentary system? The PNU, through the emerging KKK Alliance, of which Mr Musyoka is the prima donna, thinks it has the ethnic numbers to beat Mr Odinga in a direct presidential election.

It is deathly afraid of the parliamentary system because it believes that ODM would win more seats in the legislature. This would make Mr Odinga the premier in a parliamentary system. This partly explains why until this week the PNU and ODM had been deadlocked on the structure of the executive. Now it appears both sides have come to believe that a presidential system would best serve their interests.

There is nothing inherently wrong with a presidential system so long as it is marked by strong checks and balances and stripped of imperium. This is Constitutional Law 101. But the bickering on the structure of the executive has had nothing to do with constitutional law, and everything to do with succession politics.

The disagreement was based on ethnic calculations, and what each side believed would deliver it the throne. It seems that Mr Odinga and ODM have called PNU’s bluff and taken the high ground. This is the right call by Mr Odinga, just as it was in the Mau Forest case. He has acted courageously as a nationalist to lead in securing Kenya’s most important environmental asset.

Some might think that the PM will be “eaten” alive by PNU in the ballot box now that he appears to have accepted the presidential system. Quite the contrary. Just like in the Mau case, the PM appears to have put the country first, and himself second.

If the agreement on the executive holds, and Kenya gets a new constitution, Mr Odinga will have high chances of being elected the chief executive in 2012. There are those who believe that Mr Musyoka and the rest of the PNU crowd have been constitutional obstructionists.

In 2012 Kenyans will remember what the PM did, and what Mr Musyoka did not do. That’s why Mr Odinga’s concession on the executive is a historic turning point. Has the PM committed political suicide? May be, although I don’t think so. Building a nation requires taking some huge risks.

It may also occasion failure. But look at the great leaders and the risks they took – Nelson Mandela, Mao Tse Tung, Vaclav Havel, Dedan Kimathi, Koitalel arap Samoei, and Mekatilili. The PM has already paid a steep price for opposing dictatorship – years in detention. He now has taken one more risk – by compromising with Mr Kibaki on the executive. It is a compromise that could make the state more, not less, democratic.

The PM should remember that it is he – and not Mr Kibaki – who has a future beyond 2012 in Kenyan politics. That is why it should be him, and not Mr Kibaki, who should take the lead – and the credit – in birthing a new constitution. This is my advice to the PM. Sir, own the Naivasha agreement and become its key salesman. Push for a structure that makes it clear that the President is *numero uno*, but stripped of imperial powers.

Tell Kenyans clearly and loudly why you believe this is the most democratic structure, and why you are making the compromise. Ask Mr Kibaki to openly support your position and whip his “boys” into line. I have no doubt that the CoE and Parliament would all fall in line. Then you can sit back and wait for your coronation in 2012.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
