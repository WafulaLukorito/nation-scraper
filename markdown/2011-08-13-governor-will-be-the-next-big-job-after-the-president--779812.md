# Governor will be the next big job after the President 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2011-08-13T14:18:00Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/governor-will-be-the-next-big-job-after-the-president--779812  

---

## What you need to know:

* **People:** The power of the county governor will come from ‘his people’ and not from Nairobi, or the President

I think the political class hasn’t fully digested how deeply the new Constitution has disrupted the status quo. Nothing will be the same again. Old assumptions have been stood on their head, and power has been grabbed by the people.

In the ashes of the old order, one particular office has emerged as a centre of gravity. That’s the county governor — the new sheriff of devolution — who will straddle the “periphery” like a colossus..

Which begs the question — will Kenya’s tribal kingpins commandeer the counties? My take is that the county governor — aside from the President and the Deputy President — will be the most coveted office in 2012 and beyond.

I have five reasons why folks will “kill” to get to the governor’s mansion.

First, county governors will be the new and “independent political kingpins” for 47 local governments. What does this mean? The power of the county governor will come from “his people” and not from Nairobi, or the President.

He/she is directly accountable to the citizens of the county. The President is empowered in Article 162 of the Constitution to suspend a county government, but he can only do so where there is “internal conflict or war” or in “exceptional circumstances”.

But a suspension cannot last more than 90 days, and can be terminated anytime by the Senate. The removal of the county governor is equally difficult.

Governors can only be removed for “gross violation” of the Constitution or other laws, the commission of a national or international crime, abuse of office or “gross misconduct,” and mental or physical incapacity.

This is a very high bar. The county assembly has no constitutional power to remove the governor. The county governor need not be a member of any registered party.

He/she can run as an independent and be free of party diktats and control. This means that the county governor doesn’t have to bow to the President of the Republic or party bosses during elections. Governors have two five-year term limits. This is a huge incentive to run a prosperous county if they want a political future.

Second, county governors will have access and control of enormous resources. County governments shall have revenue equitably allocated by the national government.

They’ll have the power to collect and keep taxes and can borrow money. The Fourth Schedule of the Constitution provides for the division of functions between counties and the central government.

The national government rightly controls foreign affairs, monetary policy, consumer protection, education, transport and communications, immigration and citizenship, defence, and other overarching matters. County governments control agriculture, health services, county transport, economic planning and development, public works, culture, drugs, and trade development.

These vest huge powers in the governor. A wise county CEO can ride the office to national prominence and influence.

Third, the county governor will have enormous political clout. Governors will have the opportunity to influence who is elected MP and Senator. As the CEO of the county, the governor gets to call the shots — most of the time.

Members of the National Assembly, previously MPs, of whom there will be many in a county, now become the subjects of the governor.

The Senator from the county will be important than the Assembly member, but not more than the governor. Why? Because the Senator has only one vote in the Senate, controls zero resources, has no direct power in the county, and must work through the National Assembly on most important matters.

Fourth, the counties are pivotal geopolitical maps in national elections. No one can be elected President of the Republic unless they win over 25 per cent of the votes in more than half of the 47 counties. This makes every county, with the exception of the few in the backyard of the candidate, key electoral maps.

This forces every presidential candidate to take every governor — and their county — very seriously. The drafters of the Constitution intended this to be a killer of tribal bloc voting, or cancerous ethnic balloting, which has hitherto only favoured the “leaders” of the five major communities — Kalenjin, Kamba, Luo, Luhya and Kikuyu. “Minority” candidates may have a chance under this formula.

Fifth, the county governor — as CEO — will have the opportunity to acquire and showcase executive experience required for the presidency. In the past, Kenyans have elected former vice-presidents to the office of the President.

One reason for this was their presumed “executive experience”.

Don’t snicker because under Kanu we had no choice. However, counties will give Kenyans the chance to judge candidates based upon their performance as CEOs. This will be a first in Kenya’s history — candidates being actually judged for their performance.

Except for the Deputy President and Cabinet Secretaries, no other public office will be better placed to train future presidents. Look for future candidates to have more substance than tribal oomph.

What does this mean for our national politics? It disperses power from the centre and distributes it to the “periphery”.

This will deconcentrate elites and resources from large urban areas, especially Nairobi. Better hotels, investments, and hangouts will spring up in the counties. Jobs will be created where most Kenyans live. County resources, including minerals, will be exploited to benefit locals.

This will slow the destructive urban migration to Nairobi. More elites will be produced, but not in the suffocating little circles in Nairobi.

This will put more power in the hands of the governor. That’s why I expect top brains and professionals to run for county governor.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
