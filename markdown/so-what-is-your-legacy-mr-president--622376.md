# So what is your legacy, Mr President? 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-01-30T14:29:07Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/so-what-is-your-legacy-mr-president--622376  

---

Mr President, this is an open letter to you. Sir, you are in the twilight of your long political career. I bet you didn’t know how exhausting it would be to rule Kenya when you were sworn into office in 2002. That’s because, unlike Daniel arap Moi, you took over a less despotic state, and a more irreverent populace. History books will record that Mr Moi was the last true dictator to lord it over Kenyans.

This is not to say that you have been free of the hubris of the imperial presidency. You tried – through senior aides in the inner sanctum – to dictate but “unruly” segments in the political class thwarted you. In an ordinary democracy, you would now be a lame duck because you cannot run for another term.

However, Kenya is a kleptocracy where political power is inherited through patronage, or by birth. Rarely is power earned in Kenya. That’s why, Mr President, you are still relevant even though your clock is winding down. Your political children cannot afford to be disinherited by you. Your power lies in your ability to make, or break them.

Whoever you anoint will inherit your political – and ethnic – mantle. Two key auditioners – Kalonzo Musyoka and Uhuru Kenyatta – are the apples of your eyes. This is where, Mr President, you must think – and think hard. In doing so, I ask you to look back, and then forward.

What have you accomplished since that inspiring day in December 2002 when you took the oath of office? I know you are scratching your head and wondering how time flies. Remember the many promises you made as an attentive nation and world hung on your every word? Swaying from side to side, the multitudes at Uhuru Park boisterously sang “*yote yanawezekana bila* Moi”.

But, alas, little did they know that you – the saviour – would bring Mr Moi back. Is there any particular reason you chose to rehabilitate Mr Moi, the very man you publicly vilified on that historic day? You swore to end corruption in public life and within the Judiciary. The worst part is that we wanted to believe you – and by God we did!

However, your internationally touted anti-corruption efforts – the anti-graft czar, the “radical surgery” of the Judiciary, exploring a truth commission, the Goldenberg Commission, and the Kenya National Commission on Human Rights – were either stymied, or ignored. Instead, corruption scandals erupted everywhere – the most famous being Anglo Leasing. Those efforts now seem misty in the distance, as though they took place in another century! Or am I dreaming?

Then the political landscape ruptured. Narc died. Remember Narc, Mr President, and what you said of it when you took the oath of office? You told naysayers that Narc would prosper and deliver Kenya to a new era of clean government without road side declarations. But little did we know that you would do just that – creating “political” districts and handing out title deeds through impromptu declarations. How, they say, the more things change, the more they remain the same!

Under your watch, reformers of yesteryear became the villains of today. Very soon we could not distinguish between those we had fought against to oust Kanu from power from those with whom we had fought. Villains and heroes became interchangeable. But the most troubling development, sir, has been the further tribalisation of the state and society under your watch. Tribal feelings have coarsened and ossified. That’s why the 2007 electoral campaigns were fought largely on ethnic lines.

The result was a near civil war. I believe that the same feelings undergird the push for “autonomous” regional governments. These tribal units would not be different from the “Native Authorities” invented by the colonial regime of yore. Their purpose and intent then, as now, is to conquer, divide and rule.

I have written before that Kenya has very few Kenyans in it – most of us are tribal bigots without a sense of the nation. That is truer today than it was at independence. Now, sir, let’s look forward. What would you like to accomplish before your term expires in 2012?

What, in a word, do you want to be known for? Will your legacy be the one I have sketched above, or do you wish for a more benign treatment by historians? Arguably, you have brought back some economic sense to Kenya. But those gains have not been felt by the masses of the people.

This was not all your fault. The drought and the great recession have taken a huge bite out of the GDP. But these are excuses, not reasons for the national malaise. I say so because dealt the same hand, other countries did not do as poorly as Kenya.

This is how you can salvage your legacy. The constitution is the single most important defining national obsession of our time. Forget about engineering either Mr Musyoka or Mr Kenyatta into the State House. Instead, give Kenya a new constitution. This is how you can cleanse your legacy and immortalise yourself.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
