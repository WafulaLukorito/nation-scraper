# The Constitution does not belong to MPs 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-03-27T16:03:37Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/the-constitution-does-not-belong-to-mps--628462  

---

I am sick to the pit of my stomach because of the people who call themselves MPs in Kenya. Except in Somalia, or some other god forsaken land, there possibly cannot be another set of dubious and vacuous legislators on this planet.

Parliament reached a nadir last week when it purported to agree on a 25-region devolution scheme for Kenya.

Why no one had ever mentioned that “magical” number before tells you how thoughtless the idea is. Apparently, Parliament does not think with its head.

If MPs were serious men and women – with a sense of national duty – they would not engage in such pitiable and worthless charades. We must tell them the Constitution does not belong to them.

There is no point in having the Committee of Experts if MPs can, without rhyme or reason, dream up meaningless numbers. MPs have taken the incorrect view that they “own” the Constitution, and that it’s their way or the highway.

They don’t give a damn about what’s in the CoE draft, or what Kenyans think. It’s the same arrogance they showed when they gave themselves sinful pay raises, or refused to pay taxes.

They are full of hubris and out of control. They have forgotten that they were elected to do the people’s work, not to advance their own selfish agendas.

With all due respect, MPs have gone rogue, and Kenyans should show them the exit come the next elections.

I do not deny that constitution making is inherently a political process. But it cannot be exclusively – or even primarily – a legislative process.

MPs have a role to play as an important institution, but the constitution is not theirs, nor is it being written for them. That’s why they must give primacy to the CoE and show deference to the thoughtful and rational draft by the CoE.

It makes a mockery of constitution making to take a unitary document and mutilate it piece by painful piece without any rationale.

I do not mean to be abusive, but I don’t know of too many MPs who fully comprehend the logical architecture of a constitution. Even fewer understand how the document hangs together.

The fact that one has been elected to Parliament, and has a national platform, does not make them an expert on the Constitution.

Their mandate is primarily “political,” not “legal.” It is the CoE that is invested with the “legal” mandate. If Kenyans thought otherwise, they would have asked MPs to write the entire document.

Every important sector has a stake in the Constitution. That’s why we trusted the CoE – as the neutral body – to play the key role. Sadly, MPs have conflated their role in the process and usurped the unique mandate of the CoE.

The process was not designed to give every MP or bloc of MPs the power to demand certain provisions, or hold the process hostage. The Constitution belongs to the nation, not to megalomaniacal legislators.

What’s clear is that those holding the review process hostage have devious agendas that have nothing to do with the integrity of the draft constitution.

Some political blocs believe that certain constitutional arrangements will favour them in the Kibaki succession sweepstakes.

That’s why they seek a particular devolution scheme. Others want particular arrangements to solidify their incumbency.

What’s disappointing is that all these machinations have nothing to do with the key reasons Kenyans have toiled for a new constitution in the last two decades. Instead, the draft constitution has become a battleground for temporary political alliances and tribal demagoguery.

In the meantime, Kenyans watch and wait, helplessly.

This is where President Mwai Kibaki and Prime Minister Raila Odinga come in. Mr Kibaki may be laid back, but he holds the key to his successor within PNU. He most likely will anoint his party’s nominee.

That’s why PNU presidential contenders cannot afford to brush his wishes on the Constitution aside.

On the ODM side, Mr Odinga is the eight hundred pound gorilla – he gets whatever he wants. He can snap his fingers and ODM match lock step to his wishes on the Constitution.

Kenya can only fail to get a new constitution if Mr Kibaki and Mr Odinga do not want to give Kenya one. It’s that simple — the “principals” can make it happen in a snap!

My point is that Mr Kibaki and Mr Odinga should save Kenyans from the myopia of MPs. But Mr Odinga has more to lose than Mr Kibaki if he is seen as an obstructionist. After 2012, Mr Kibaki will go back to Othaya to play with his grandchildren and watch the sunset.

Mr Odinga, on the other hand, hopes to ascend the political summit by claiming the keys to the State House.

The abortion of the draft constitution would hurt Mr Odinga more than Mr Kibaki. Mr Kibaki doubtlessly knows this fact. That’s why he will force Mr Odinga to make more compromises. Mr Odinga needs to play his cards wisely.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
