A High Court Judge has advised lawyers in the country to seek more knowledge on matters pertaining International Labour Laws to avoid being challenged in court by trade unionists.

Industrial Court Judge Lady Justice Hellen Wasilwa said that lawyers were losing most of their cases because they never travel abroad to learn more on the laws unlike trade Unionists.

She called upon the lawyers to make good use of the International Labour Laws terming them effective in the prosecution process.

“There is great need for lawyers to apply International Labour Laws whenever they are submitting cases in court. This is by virtue of Article 2 (6) of the Kenyan Constitution which allows the use of the laws,” Justice Wasilwa stated.

Speaking Friday at an Eldoret hotel in Uasin Gishu County during a one day seminar on Labour Resource based on the emerging jurisprudence and opportunities, Hon Wasilwa told the lawyers to also consider learning more about the international conventions covering the labour laws.

“Most trade unionists in the country travel abroad to Geneva in efforts to learn more about the 8 main Conventions that govern the International Labour laws. Such Unionists on most occasions take advantage of this to challenge the lawyers during prosecution,” Hon Wasilwa added on.

The main aims of the International Labour Organization are to promote rights at work, encourage decent employment opportunities, enhance social protection and strengthen dialogue on work-related issues.

However, speaking at the same function, Law Society of Kenya Chairman Eric Mutua called for the amendment of the Labour Relations Act 2007 stating that this is the only way many trade unions can be registered.

Mutua indicated that registering the trade unions, which have increased in number over the years, was the best way to protect many eligible employers from marauding fraudsters.

“There is dire need for the Labour Regulations Act which came into force in 2007 paving way for the formation of the Industrial Courts to be amended. This is because the Act consolidates the law relating to trade unions and trade disputes and also provides for the registration, regulation, management and democratization of trade unions and employers organizations or federations,” Mutua said.

The Act was enacted to promote sound labour relations through the protection and promotion of freedom of association, the encouragement of effective collective bargaining and promotion of orderly and expeditious dispute settlement, conducive to social justice and economic development and for connected purposes.

But according to Mutua, many people have failed to understand it effectively over the years so the need for its amendment.

Others present at the event included; Hon Justice Nzioki wa Makau of the Industrial Court, Dennis Magare a representative from the Center for Cognitive Liberty and Ethics (CCLE) and Rioba Omboto, Chairman of the Law Society of Kenya North Rift region among others.
