At the risk of appearing pollyannish, I posit that every nation has – or should have – a national soul. A zeitgeist, an identity, which is irreducible in the crucible of nation-building. That’s what separates and differentiates the 200-odd countries on the planet.

It’s the basis of national pride, patriotism, and even jingoism. It creates “apartness” – but not “apartheid” – among states. It’s for this same reason that individual human beings – even identical twins – are different from one another. No two human beings are exactly the same. That lack of exactitude is the same for nations as it is for individual natural persons. There must be a “there there” for each nation that makes it different from a gazillion others.

History is replete with artificial states. These are countries that are products of accidents of history, irrational colonial concoctions, or simply those that are made up for whatever reason. Perhaps the only common denominator is that countries and states are creatures of violence and are sustained by force, or the threat of force.

That’s why the police officer is never too far away from wherever you might be. For the police officer is the embodiment of violence or peace. Either way, the police officer is the symbol of law and order, or lack of it. But countries are more than instrumentalities of violence or coercion. They should be organic polities of high consensus about culture, governance, soul, and a unique identity.

States and countries should be living, dynamic organisms capable of both life and death. That’s why today I want to ask what the indicia of a nation, a country, a state, a government are? First, the basics.

## Defined borders

A nation must have a country with defined borders within a land mass, a population, an effective government that controls the national territory, and the ability to conduct international relations. These are the bare and outward physical manifestations of a state. Matters get complicated beyond these simple indexes. In the common Kenyan lexicon, these constitute the “hardware” of a state. The more elusive is what they call the “software.” Much of the software falls in the category of illusory intangibles, the soul.

The second index is things we can loosely group under “culture.” Here, we have language, music and cinema, food, and sometimes religious, racial, or ethnic identity. If we take Kenya’s regional neighbourhood, for example, it’s clear that Tanzania has developed a genre of national music that’s authentic and unique — usually sung in Kiswahili. Kenya has tried, but fallen short. Instead, Kenya has music genres cabined in ethnic silos. None are able to truly go “national.”

The most prominent of these are Luo, Akamba, and Agikuyu artistes of the gospel and secular hue. None have a truly national penetration unlike our cousins in Tanzania. Why? Probably because Kenya is an ethnically fragmented society unlike Tanzania. Kenya’s soul, if it’s there, is more restless than Tanzania’s.

If you want to know the soul of a country, look at these few things. The first, and most obvious, is the culture of traffic on the country’s roads and byways. Contrast two countries in the region, Rwanda and Kenya. Visit Rwanda and you will marvel at how orderly roads are. I know you will say Rwanda and capital Kigali are small compared to Kenya’s size and complex urban centres. But you would miss the point right there. I agree size matters in most things, but on traffic, size is irrelevant. Rules and their observance in the culture are everything. Order! Order! Ladies and gentlemen! Go to Rabat in Morocco, Ethiopia’s Addis Ababa or even Cairo, and you will see what I mean.

## Chaos

In Kenya, the chaos of our traffic culture is symptomatic of our national soul – turbulent, unorganised, undisciplined, and violent. Our roads are a total mess. I don’t even know why we have roads at all. We might as well just not have them. People driving against traffic, on the curb, the pathetic tuk-tuk croaking by, the matatu screaming past, threatening to decapitate one and all, and worst of all, the boda boda doing every bad thing except fly on the roads. It doesn’t matter whether we are talking about state vehicles or those driven by senior officials. The roads are a meat grinder. This is Kenya’s mind. This is Kenya’s soul. Our identity. We cannot go to Singapore this way.

Our traffic is a window into our identity. Do you know what? Our elites and government officials are the worst offenders. If they break rules with impunity, how can the little guy be expected to do differently? In the traffic, elites break every rule in the book, rushing to nowhere.

They break traffic rules just to show that they aren’t subject to them. I was in Copenhagen and saw the Prime Minister on a bicycle with only a few aides. In Kenya, a Permanent Secretary bogarts traffic as though he were Mufasa, the King of the Jungle. This mustn’t be our national soul.
