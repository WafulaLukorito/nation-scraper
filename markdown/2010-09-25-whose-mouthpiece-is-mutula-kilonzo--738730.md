# Whose mouthpiece is Mutula Kilonzo?

## What you need to know:

* **Ploy:** It’s a tactic to let the violaters go scot-free

The Honourable Mutula Kilonzo, the minister for Justice, is nobody’s fool. That’s why I listen carefully – and read between the lines – whenever he speaks.

The man is phenomenally brilliant, and an exceptionally able lawyer. His native smarts are off the charts.

Fathom this – he was the first East African to obtain a first class honours law degree at the University of Dar es Salaam. But I want to beg the minister’s pardon.

Being “brilliant” is not the same thing as being “moral” or “right”. Nor do brain smarts immunise one from “intellectual corruption”.

Or prevent one from being a “gun for hire”. “Political prostitution” is commonplace in Kenya. Which begs the question – has Mr Kilonzo’s mouth been “rented”?

For who is Mr Kilonzo a mouthpiece? He is simply too bright to shoot his mouth off without a strategic purpose. Two particular statements – bombshells, I should call them – come to mind. Both pertain to the new Constitution.

The first was the bizarre argument that the new Constitution recognises the Provincial Administration. The second – and more ominous – was the pronouncement that the International Criminal Court should end its investigations on Kenya.

Both outbursts are legally wrong, and politically explosive. Why would the minister, who is the “political guardian” of legality, champion impunity?

I submit that Mr Kilonzo knows something the rest of us don’t. What he knows – and you can take this to the bank – could blow your mind.

Let me tell you about Mr Kilonzo before revealing what he knows. That Mr Kilonzo’s biography is a textbook story of rags-to- riches is public knowledge. He was not born with a silver spoon in his mouth.

He has achieved unbelievable success by dint of sheer persistence, shrewdness, and the hawking of his brains to the highest bidders.

Like Gordon Gekko, the fictional character in Wall Street, the 1987 blockbuster movie, Mr Kilonzo believes that “greed is good” because “greed works”. Put differently, moral scruples should not detain one’s climb up the ladder of success.

That’s how Mr Kilonzo became a darling of the ancient regime under President Daniel arap Moi. He was a vicious defender of the one-party state.

No epithet was too low for Mr Kilonzo in his attacks against human rights defenders and multiparty advocates.

He would hiss on cue whenever Mr Moi blasted reform activists and opposition politicians. He believed, like Mr Moi, that democracy and human rights were foreign and alien ideologies.

That’s why it’s an out-of-body experience to hear Mr Kilonzo today speak the language of democracy and human rights. Does he really believe what he is saying, or is he simply speaking the political language of the day?

Is this not what political survivors do? Or has Mr Kilonzo undergone a true conversion? How do we know that he’s not a wolf in sheep’s clothing? Does the leopard ever change its spots?

But Mr Kilonzo was not only a Kanu anti-reformer. He was also Mr Moi’s private lawyer during Kanu’s heyday. Significantly, Mr Kilonzo was Mr Moi’s lawyer during the Goldenberg inquiry.

If there is anyone in Kenya who has gobs of information about the Moi family business dealings, it’s Mr Kilonzo. This comingling of business and politics is what inevitably leads to “intellectual corruption”.

Mr Kilonzo was rewarded with a nomination to Parliament in 2003 when Kanu lost the elections. In 2007, he was elected on an ODM-K ticket and joined the Cabinet when ODM-K leader Kalonzo Musyoka struck a deal with PNU.

Isn’t it ironic that Mr Kilonzo, a man with the deepest connections to Mr Moi, would anchor the Justice ministry?

Now let me tell you why Mr Kilonzo opposes the ICC and wants to retain the dreaded Provincial Administration. Mr Kilonzo knows that Kenya is bound by the Rome Statute of the ICC.

It’s Kenyan law. He knows that the government has legally and publicly committed to working with the ICC and Prosecutor Luis Moreno-Ocampo to bring those who bear the greatest responsibility for post-election violence to The Hague for trial.

But Mr Kilonzo says that the ICC is unnecessary because Kenya has a new Constitution to reform the judiciary and the police. Mr Kilonzo must think we are complete fools.

The new Constitution is a piece of paper – albeit a very important one – that will take years to implement. It will take at least five years to transform the judiciary and even longer to recreate the police force.

Does Mr Kilonzo believe that we should wait that long to bring the perpetrators of post-election violence to book? This is a tactic to let the violators go scot-free.

Why? I believe that Mr Kilonzo is part of a wider plot within PNU to scuttle the ICC so that the KKK alliance can win elections in 2012.

Mr Kilonzo is nothing but a mouthpiece for those within the PNU who believe that Mr Moreno-Ocampo poses a grave threat to some big names in the KKK alliance.

If one, or several, of the KKK bigwigs are nabbed by the ICC, the alliance will fall apart.  
The plot to resuscitate the Provincial Administration is connected to the scheme against the ICC.

The KKK alliance believes that the Provincial Administration will be a pivotal tool for mobilising people, “using” state resources to marshal support, and subverting opponents during the 2012 elections. In a close election, the provincial structures could prove crucial to a KKK victory.

Mr Kilonzo and Internal Security minister George Saitoti are on the same page. The Justice minister is living proof that you cannot teach an old dog new tricks.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
