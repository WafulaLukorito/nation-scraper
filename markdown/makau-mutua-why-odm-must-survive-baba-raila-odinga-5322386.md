Even a person with a middling noggin knows that most Kenyan political parties exist either in a briefcase or in the natural personage of the individual founder. In *sensu stricto*, such an entity isn’t, in the textbook sense, a political party.

It is a personal carriage for the political ambitions of its founding owner. It doesn’t matter what the Constitution prescribes; such a legal person is indistinct from the natural person leading it. It is a figment of the imagination in terms of survivability or irreversibility.

Real and substantive political parties are anchored in unyielding ideological commitments and mass movements. In Kenya’s history, the Orange Democratic Movement (ODM) stands as the most enduring exemplar of this definition. Today, I explore whether ODM can survive ‘Baba’ Raila Odinga’s exit.

The history of Kenya’s political parties is chequered. The Kenya African National Union (Kanu) was iconic as the independence party, but it decayed and collapsed in the hubris of the single-party State. The monopoly of State power by a party creates political mission creep, infertility, impotence, and finally death.

We can see some early signs of immobility and inertia in Tanzania’s Chama Cha Mapinduzi (CCM) and South Africa’s ANC, two of Africa’s most celebrated and historic parties. Unless a mass party re-engineers dynamism through robust internal democracy, its death is certain. Kanu, for example, held on to the State long after it had become a carcass, solely because of the monopoly of power.

## Ideological purity

Only Kanu, the Kenya People’s Union (briefly led by Jaramogi Oginga Odinga in the mid-1960s), ODM, and now [President William Ruto’s](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/william-samoei-ruto-3809570) United Democratic Alliance (UDA) have attempted ideological purity.

Among the four, ODM stands apart because of its longevity outside power and its principled commitment to social justice and left-of-centre politics. For a party that has never held power, ODM’s influence and national penetration have been remarkable and without peer. Its following across the country—except in the Mt Kenya communities and Ukambani—has been unmatched. Even so, Baba himself carried Ukambani every time he teamed up with Wiper Patriotic Front leader [Kalonzo Musyoka](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/kalonzo-musyoka-3810808). I remember a rally in Kitui Town in 2022 when Baba received a fanatical welcome that completely eclipsed his host, Mr Musyoka. It was a sight to behold. This was where the myth of the man converged with his pro-people politics to overwhelm even a native son.

The question now is whether ODM, as larger than life as Baba was, can survive his demise. CCM survived Mwalimu Julius Nyerere, the only leader who was larger than the party. The ANC survived Nelson Mandela, its saintly and unparalleled prince of peace. For ODM, the maths is more complicated. Barely before Baba’s body had been fully interred, intra-party conflicts burst into the open, threatening to take down the legacy of the political behemoth. Politicos who couldn’t utter a contrarian word in front of Baba suddenly grew cojones.

## Greed for power

That, in itself, is not negative. Whenever a *mugumo* tree falls, the smaller plants beneath it flourish. It only becomes nugatory when it is destructive, malicious and driven by unbridled greed for power.

In 2027, most of those who relied on Baba’s long coattails to gain public office will be orphaned. This includes many ODM bigwigs who have overstayed their political welcome and won only because Baba’s large umbrella gave them cover. This is one source of the conniptions within ODM. Without Baba, some of these figures are deer caught in headlights. Political incumbency without Baba will be hard.

Then there is the matter of party leadership. At the pinnacle sits Dr Oburu Oginga, the only senior ODM leader who was a plausible choice to succeed Baba. No one else—nada—has the clout, wisdom, or stature to hold the party together in a time of trauma. The naysayers fighting him are splitists who would kill the party.

The biggest elephant in the room arises from centripetal and centrifugal forces, within and without the party, that have designs on it. Most of these are animated by ODM’s relationship with UDA and the 10-point Memorandum of Understanding signed between the parties.

There is mortal fear that a formal UDA–ODM coalition for the 2027 elections would pulverise the opposition and sideline the refuseniks within ODM. That is why the ODM boat is being rocked from within and without. Those opposed to President William Ruto’s re-election want to kill any possibility of a coalition in 2027. The opposition has successfully recruited moles within ODM to torpedo this emerging alliance. My crystal ball tells me they will be crushed in the petri dish of politics.

The only way ODM survives Baba is if it keeps its promise to stay in the broad-based government that he authored with President Ruto, and which has brought untold benefits to ODM’s strongholds for the first time in history. A return to the political wilderness would be ODM’s death sentence. Many have said Baba told them “things”. He confided in me three pivotal ones, one of which I will reveal now: he told me he had decided to strike an agreement with President Ruto to support him in 2027. That is how ODM survives Baba’s death.
