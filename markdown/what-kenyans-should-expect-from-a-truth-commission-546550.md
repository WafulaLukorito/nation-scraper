# What Kenyans should expect from a Truth Commission

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2008-05-16T21:00:00Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/news/what-kenyans-should-expect-from-a-truth-commission-546550  

---

With the publication of the Truth Justice and Reconciliation Commission Bill last week, Kenya has begun the inevitable journey it has succeeded in avoiding for a long time.

A house set on fire by rioting youths at Ilula in Eldoret after the election results. Such scenes were witnessed all over the country. Photo/ JARED NYATAYA

For close to a century, Kenya has been ruled by regimes that have had little or no respect for human rights, the rule of law, social justice, transparency, accountability and other tenets of democracy.

The Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission proposed under Agenda item 4 of the Mediation Process is expected to transform and renew the country’s morality in politics, law, economy and social relations.

**Gross violations**

A Truth Commission is essentially a transition mechanism. Such mechanisms result from changing from an old repressive order to a new political dispensation that seeks to tackle the gross violations of the past as a deterrent.

In 2003 the Task Force on the Truth and Justice Commission, headed by Prof Makau Mutua, said in its report that transitional justice captures two notions:

(i) First, it acknowledges the temporary measures that must be put in place to build confidence for the construction of a state ravaged by human rights violations.

(ii) Secondly, it rejects the application of any rigid set of norms or criteria as a beachhead to the future. A society cannot fundamentally change if the transition from autocracy and plunder of public resources to democracy do not become a reality.

**Official sanction**

A truth commission is conceived for a country that is emerging from a period of gross human rights violations and economic crimes. It has to embrace internationally set standards while offering ways of responding to years of abuse, corruption, human rights abuses and other conflicts that arose over justice, power and control of economic resources.

Ms Pricilla Hayner of the Geneva-based International Transitional Justice Centre defines a Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission as a “non-judicial investigating body, granted some form of official sanction from the state but operating independently”.

It focuses generally on victims, listening to their stories to reclaim a denied or silenced history, and using the information and conclusions to craft recommendations for reforms in order to prevent repetition of such abuses in future.

The million dollar question among Kenyans is: What will the TJRC do that is different from the other commissions?

There is a danger of dumping every kind of violation on the TJRC without questioning the reasons for doing so.

We also need to define the specific mandate of the TJRC. For instance; what is the type of justice we want? Is it achievable only through TJRC or can it be achieved with other complementary processes? How do we create a link between TJRC and other judicial processes?

The challenge for the Kenyan situation is that we need to ask ourselves how we want to deal with our past. Is TJRC the sole institution and process for dealing with this past or should there be an integrated multi-faceted approach? What are the goals that we should pursue under the TJRC? How consultative and democratic is the process leading to the formation of the TJRC? Are victims’ or survivors’ voices reflected in the proposed draft TJRC law? Is Parliament ready and willing to listen and accommodate these voices when the TJRC Bill is enacted?

Looking at the Bill that has been published, it runs the risk of setting up a process that is bound to fail.

It is imperative to have the TJRC set up soonest possible but it is equally important not to participate in a process that is stage-managed by the State. The Truth Commission should not be a whitewash or a witch-hunt.

Often, talk about TJRC creates an impression that Kenyans look at it as a panacea. Hence, the tendency to dump everything on it. This is very dangerous as it can make the process easily fall into the hands of people intent on mischief.

The TJRC ought to be a step towards jump-starting an honest dialogue among citizens.

It is important to correctly establish the historical, socio-political, economic and institutional context under which the TJRC is being set up. For instance, the inclusion of economic crimes in the proposed Truth Commission has elicited some debate. Scholars like Ms Hayner argue that, “a question that would logically be raised in Kenya is, whether it makes sense for economic crimes to be involved in a commission’s mandate, along with human rights crimes”.

**Economic crimes**

In contrast, the Prof Makau Mutua-led task force report states that “…economic crimes such as the looting of the public purse, land grabbing, public corruption and the transfer of stolen public funds into personal bank accounts offshore must be investigated without delay”.

The 2003 TJRC Task Force justified the inclusion of economic crimes in a Truth Commission’s mandate thus: “It would represent such a yawning gap in the record of truth of Kenya should it be excluded. Despite the fine points of the debate it is clear that economic crimes are so closely intertwined with human rights violations that it is impossible to establish watertight compartments between the two types of violations”.

To ensure a credible and effective process, we msut clarify its mandate and ask questions on its capacity to deliver. How are people being consulted to establish what they want out of the TJRC? How does the TJRC relate to the other commissions’ reports?

The process needs to be assured of independence but still enjoy political will. Civil society and media have been consistent advocates for the establishment of such a commission and they must continue to push for a credible and effective one.

Vigilance, after TJRC submits its reports, is critical, given the fact that previous commissions have not addressed issues of impunity. Many such are still gathering dust in government offices.

How do you compel the government to ensure that the recommendations are followed to the letter? How do you ensure that victims get justice?

There are major questions and scepticism towards the roles of TJRCs in meeting their goals. Huge differences in understanding and expectations of the process exist between the elites and the communities. TJRCs across the world are notorious in their failure to implement their recommendations. This has led to rare attainment of the expected moral regeneration of the society. The ethnic and political divisions look even more set to make the work of TJRC more difficult in Kenya.

The government and its agencies as well as the citizens must behave in a manner to furthers the ideals of such a process. However, as long as previous injustices continue being replicated in Kenya, such as the gross human rights violations like extra-judicial killings and torture, then TJRC may not succeed. It would easily turn into another conflict resolution mechanism. The truth-seeking process is unfinished business that provides a foundation for the transformation of the state. It is only a step in the process of addressing long term and short term issues.

*Ndung’u Wainaina is Director, International Centre for Policy and Conflict, a member of the Kenyans for Peace with Truth and Justice coalition.*
