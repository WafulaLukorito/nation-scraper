# Nothing should derail this sound and solid draft

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2009-11-27T10:34:56Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/nothing-should-derail-this-sound-and-solid-draft-615850  

---

I have taken time to read the draft constitution front to back and back to front. I have done so not once, but three times. I have thought hard and long about it. Let me state the bottom line upfront – except for a few matters which are easily cured – the draft constitution is one of the most progressive and democratic documents of its kind in the world.

That’s why I am calling upon Kenyans to overwhelmingly approve it once the Committee of Experts makes several revisions. Our seemingly endless long dark night – in which we toiled in the vineyards for a democratic constitution – is now over.

Let me start by complimenting the Committee of Experts for a job well done. On what was the greatest roadblock to a new constitution – the structure of the Executive – the committee has provided the wisest arrangement possible given the history of the imperial presidency in Kenya.

The committee accomplished the near impossible by stripping the presidency of imperial and unaccountable powers while keeping the centrality of the office. The State President is still the first among equals, but the office has been fully democratised. In the draft, the State President is the physical embodiment of the sovereignty of the republic. The President is the “symbol of national unity” invested with the power to “safeguard the sovereignty of the republic”.

The President is the Commander-in-Chief of the armed forces but can only appoint – and dismiss – the Prime Minister, ministers, judges, diplomats, and holders of constitutional offices with the approval of the Legislature or other organs of state. The President can only commit Kenya to international treaties with the approval of the Legislature. He or she can only declare war or a state of emergency only with the approval of the Cabinet.

It is clear from the structure of the Executive that the Prime Minister is subordinate to the State President although he or she will be the head of government. The Prime Minister is a powerful figure because he or she “composes” the Cabinet for appointment by the President. The Prime Minister “chairs the Cabinet and literally ‘runs’ the government.

However, it is the Deputy State President, not the Prime Minister, who takes over as President in the event of a vacancy in the presidency. In a masterstroke, the drafters have divided executive authority between the State President and the Prime Minister in a way that avoids two centres of power, or conflict between the two offices.

Some uninformed or ill-intentioned pundits and politicians have argued that the president is ceremonial while the prime minister enjoys executive powers. Such demagoguery is shocking and is either driven by ignorance or mischief. The State President is directly elected – and above partisan politics – so that he or she can act without conflict of interest.

The Prime Minister, on the other hand, is only “indirectly elected” because he or she must be an MP who commands the largest following in Parliament. The President can be impeached while he or she can only remove the Prime Minister upon a vote of no confidence by Parliament. This structure of the executive is best analogised to the French model, and not the Indian or Israeli models, which have “executive” premiers.

The draft provides for a “strong semi-presidential” model, but with a “strong” – but not “executive” – prime minister. Now let me tabulate what I think ought to be revised. The devolution chapter needs to be revisited because it creates a top heavy political and bureaucratic structure. The sub-national governments are too tribalised. The draft should provide for small county and regional governments without giving every tribe a government.

There is no reason to expand the size of government under the guise of democracy without accounting for the cost of county and regional structures. Experience in the United States and other democracies shows a movement towards the consolidation of regional governments to reduce the cost to the taxpayer. Otherwise entrenched regional interests will use them to milk the people dry.

The chapter on the Judiciary is too cumbersome – there are too many unnecessary courts. Kenya does not need a Supreme Court, a Court of Appeal, a Constitutional Court, and the High Court! We can do without a Constitutional Court. The draft leaves murky the question of whether Kenya is a secular republic.

It’s contradictory to provide that there is no state religion and then acknowledge the “supremacy of the Almighty God”. It is violative of secularism to end the Preamble with “God Bless Kenya”. Kenya belongs to all – believers and non-believers alike. The draft’s language on family is homophobic and hateful. There is no reason to exclude sexual orientation from the protected minorities and marginalised communities.

Nothing should derail this draft constitution. It is a sound and solid document. Let’s remember that constitutions are imperfect living documents which can be amended with experience. It is impossible to please everyone on everything. The most important thing is whether this draft provides for a basically democratic polity. I believe it does. Let’s pass it.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights Commission*
