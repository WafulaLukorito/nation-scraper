Senior lawyers on Thursday said that the next Chief Justice should be an “angel” with the highest moral standing as well as an excellent legal mind.

Debate has been raging about who should take the mantle of running Kenya’s justice system after the Judicial Service Commission (JSC) revealed the names of those who had applied for the post on Tuesday.

Those wishing to apply for the post have only until this Friday, after the deadline was extended for a day to cover Thursday’s Idd-Ul-Fitr.

Senior Counsel Paul Muite told *Nation* that Dr Willy Mutunga’s successor should be beyond reproach and can come from any community or any part of the country.

“We should be looking for a Chief Justice with an impressive track record. An individual who will secure the independence of the Judiciary from all quarters,” said Mr Muite who ran for President in 2013.

“We need someone with an excellent legal mind who will give jurisprudence that other judges and lawyers will respect. We need a person with no blemish.”

**CONSTITUTIONAL REQUIREMENTS**

Constitutional lawyer Bobby Mkangi, who was a member of the Committee of Experts that drafted the Constitution, said that work was already cut out for the JSC.

“When we drafted the Constitution, we basically wanted an angel for a CJ. The requirements were deliberately set very high to get someone with the highest moral standing,” said Mr Mkangi.

Dr Mutunga retired last month after heading the Judiciary for five years as well as being the first President of the Supreme Court.

At the same time, the Law Society of Kenya (LSK) on Thursday said that the current debate on the next CJ was erroneous as it was based on individuals rather than qualities.

“We want a public pronouncement on the set of guidelines that will establish qualities expected of the next CJ and the LSK wants to be part of that discussion,” said LSK chair Isaac Okero.

Mr Okero said that the society will hold a council meeting on Monday to discuss the guidelines.

Political scientist Mutahi Ngunyi stirred debate online when he said that the next Chief Justice should not be an activist, in an apparent reference to Dr Mutunga’s previous life before being CJ.

“Ethnicity will not be a determinant for inclusion or exclusion in the recruitment process. Gender will not be a disqualifying factor. Age will only be relevant to the extent that the nominee is not above the retirement age limit,” the JSC said when it advertised the job.

JSC vice-chairperson Margaret Kobia last week told a local TV station that the commission wants a Chief Justice who “will work with other arms of government, but still retain their independence.”

But Mr Mkangi said that such a system can be abused.

“We want a Chief Justice who understands statehood, but this is not to mean one who will be subservient and lead an institution that is superintended (overseen) by the other arms,” said Mr Mkangi.

Among those who have applied for the top job include Supreme Court Judge Smokin Wanjala, US-based law scholar Makau Mutua, and Court of Appeal judges David Maraga and Alnashir Visram.
