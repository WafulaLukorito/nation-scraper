Most people knew [Baba Raila Amolo Odinga](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/raila-amolo-odinga-3809512) as the politician and the centre of gravity of Kenya’s politics. Others cryptically called him the Enigma. But most couldn’t define “enigma” or what it meant when used by thinkers about *Jakom* —as he was also known. In English, the word simply means “flummoxing,” “puzzling,” a “mystery.” The man from Bondo was all of the above, and then some.

The words, however, don’t even begin to describe Mr Odinga’s utter complexity and his seduction of the popular imaginary. Most people didn’t know that Mr Odinga was one of Kenya’s most accomplished thinkers. He wrestled with the most difficult ideas all his life, including on matters of the existence of Providence. Like most serious thought leaders, he was never comfortable with easy or lazy conclusions.

Mr Odinga was an academic in the finest traditions of the word. He was educated as a mechanical engineer in one of Germany’s best universities. Few people know that he started his academic career as a scholar in the Engineering Department at the University of Nairobi in the 1970s where he was only one of four African lecturers.

Even fewer are aware that Baba was the actual founder of the Kenya Bureau of Standards, and that he was the first indigenous manufacturer of gas cylinders. Even fewer know that Baba was an author of several books, including one on ethnography. He could regale you with the most detailed analyses of the migration patterns of African peoples. His description of Luo migration was so complex that you would wonder whether he was making it up!

## **Strategic thinker**

Once Baba left the university, he took the art of deep-think nuance to another level. At first, he was just a strategic thinker trying his hand in the murky waters of Kenyan politics. In the early years, he definitely lived in the shadows of the doyen of opposition politics. The maestro himself — Jaramogi Oginga Odinga.

Mzee was proof that the apple doesn’t fall far from the tree, especially where the progeny is willing to learn, work hard, stay hungry and remain humble. He understood a princely birth doesn’t automatically bestow one with greatness. That the best political careers are built by those who “carry the people along,” not those who want power on a silver platter because of birth order, or pedigree.

Those who knew him in the conclave known as Young Turks in the height of the Moi-Kanu kleptocracy understood how hard he worked to achieve the heights he did. Never one to rest on his laurels, Baba literally worked every second of his life until time ran out. I know because in our campaign for the Chair of the African Union Commission, he would never tire.

On long flights we would huddle and talk strategy. Then he would read, listen to music, usually rhumba, or simply walk back and forth in the small space to keep his legs fresh. He was always up at the crack of dawn although he would stay up every night watching a soccer match. The enigma. It was hard to believe he was 80.

Baba never ceased to be a strategic thinker. But he evolved into a nuanced strategist. In his older age, he became an African statesman recognisable everywhere. I remember going to a steakhouse with him in Pretoria. The waitpersons there were ecstatic upon seeing him and took many pictures with him. Even a senior African National Congress official seemed amazed.

## **Principles of social justice**

Baba was another Madiba, as South Africa’s Nelson Mandela was affectionately called. Unlike many in his generation, who became irrelevant and faded into the shadows, Baba retained currency and morphed from one generation to the next — never gathering moss, always grounded in the principles of social justice. He worked with various governments both in Kenya and abroad, but never allowed himself to be compromised, or silenced.

What I found most fascinating, however, was Baba’s turn of phrase to send multiple messages to competing factions. This way, he avoided “cancelling” most people because he truly believed in free speech. Most will remember his comment about ODM and the 2027 presidential elections.

Some in ODM were opposed to the broad-based Government and sought to disrupt the ODM-UDA pact even before it took hold. Baba was clear, as those of us close to him knew, that his détente with President William Ruto was sealed in the national interest, and that it was unshakable. But a small cabal within ODM were pushing for an ODM presidential candidate in 2027. In his uncanny style, Baba publicly asked the cabal, “who told you ODM won’t field a candidate?” In his nuance, he left open the possibility that the ODM candidate could be President Ruto.

Finally, a senior Azimio supremo went to Baba to complain about my critiques on their politics. The person wanted Baba to “silence” me, as if that was possible. Baba’s response was that “Prof is an academic!” Nuanced ambiguity.
