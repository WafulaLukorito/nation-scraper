## What you need to know:

* **Atrocities:** There’s no question the British must pay for their atrocities

They say it ain’t over ‘til the fat lady sings. It isn’t over yet, but my crystal ball tells me the fat lady is about to belt out a tune. The Mau Mau haven’t arrived, but they are on their way.

In early October, a high court in London held in a historic ruling that the British Government must answer for the torture of thousands of Kenyans during colonial rule. The ruling is a game changer that may rewrite the history of colonial brutality.

Mau Mau supremo Dedan Kimathi must be smiling from beyond the grave. But let the truth be told – this victory was made possible by a group of determined private citizens – not the State.

Just over a decade ago, a bunch of us thought we could vindicate the Mau Mau in British courts. The Kenya Human Rights Commission, then under Executive Director Willy Mutunga, now CJ, took the lead in seeking justice for the Mau Mau.

We gathered together a potent team – Senior Counsel Paul Muite, then Kabete MP, renown human rights lawyer Gibson Kamau Kuria, and ex-Mau Mau combatant Gitu Kahengeri – to map out the project.

We then raised some funds to engage lawyers Martin Day and Dan Leader of Leigh Day & Co to spearhead the litigation. On October 6, the London high court bent “the arc of history towards justice”. It legitimised the Mau Mau liberation movement in Britain.

How did we get here? Until 2002 – when President Mwai Kibaki came to power under Narc after vanquishing Kanu – the Mau Mau were viewed officially by the successive government as “terrorists”.

In fact, the Mau Mau was a banned organisation. It wasn’t until August 2003 that the Kenya Government lifted the ban on the Mau Mau.

Imagine that – for 40 years, the government of an “independent Kenya” kept the ban on the very organisation that liberated the country from the British. Mind-boggling. But obviously the ban begs many questions.

Why was a “Kenyan black government” afraid of the Mau Mau? Why was Mzee Jomo Kenyatta – the Burning Spear – and his successor Daniel arap Moi mortified by the Mau Mau?

Wasn’t Mzee Kenyatta jailed by the British at Kapenguria because of his “membership” in the Mau Mau? Or was the old man a fraud? Was he – or wasn’t he – the Mau Mau leader the British alleged? Or did he turn against the Mau Mau as a condition of his release to assume the country’s leadership?

How, and why, did Mzee Kenyatta fall under the spell of the “homeguards”– Africans who collaborated with the British to crush the Mau Mau – soon after his release?

Except for the late Paul Ngei, Mzee Kenyatta fell out with his fellow detainees – the so-called Kapenguria Six (Bildad Kaggia, Kung’u Karumba, Achieng Oneko, and Fred Kubai. He was particularly disdainful of Mr Kaggia).

My theory is that Mzee Kenyatta sold out to a right-of-centre ideology which the Mau Mau vehemently opposed. The Mau Mau – and left-of-centre ideologue Oginga Odinga – sought a more progressive redistributionist politics.

Land – that eternal Kenyan flashpoint – sat at the centre of the ideological dispute. Mzee Kenyatta’s government refused to return the land to the tillers, and instead adopted a “willing-buyer, willing seller” philosophy. However, many of his cronies simply grabbed the land without buying it.

That’s how the Mau Mau and their progeny were disinherited. President Moi followed Mzee Kenyatta’s “footsteps” to the letter. The State developed total amnesia toward the Mau Mau. The Kenya Government wouldn’t even press the British for Mr Kimathi’s burial place.

**Erected a monument**

I know the government has erected a monument for Mr Kimathi in downtown Nairobi. There have been half-hearted attempts to acknowledge his heroic spouse – the Mau Mau hero Mukami Kimathi. But the government hasn’t given her pride of place as Kenya’s “first” First Lady.

The state was very slow to support the efforts of the KHRC to sue the British. For years we sought – unsuccessfully – government financial support to pay the lawyers. I am encouraged that the state has been more forthcoming. It’s signalled support for the suit.

However, we need it to do more – Kenya must fully embrace the cause of the Mau Mau and fully fund the legal and other costs associated with the suit.

There’s no reason why the Kenyan state has allowed the Mau Mau heroes to live in squalor. The three named plaintiffs -- Paulo Muoka Nzili, Jane Muthoni Mara, and Wambuga wa Nyingi -- represent the desperate state of the aging heroes.

We’ve travelled to the homes of many Mau Mau survivors in Nyeri, Makueni, Meru, Embu, and other areas that were impacted – and we’ve seen nothing but abandonment. How can a nation turn its back on those who paid the ultimate price for its freedom?

There’s no question the British must pay for their atrocities, but the Kenyan Government should help us underwrite the legal costs of the case. The monies approved by the Cabinet should be released. This will recognise our liberators.

The Mau Mau have won this victory because of their own persistence and the unrelenting and professional work of the KHRC, the British lawyers, and other Kenyans. Mr Muite deserves special mention, among others. He never lost hope or faith that we would prevail. CJ Mutunga was our north star.

I take my hat off to the current leadership of the KHRC – Atsango Chesoni, Muthoni Wanyeki, Davis Malombe, and George Morara who’ve kept their eyes on the ball.

Lawyers Day and Leader have been tremendous. I can’t forget the board of directors of the KHRC. But let’s remember – the fight isn’t over.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at SUNY Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
