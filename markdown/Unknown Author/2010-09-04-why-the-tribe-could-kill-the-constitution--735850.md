# Why the tribe could kill the Constitution 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-09-04T15:31:08Z

## What you need to know:

* Paradox: The new law birthed by tribalism must be used to kill tribalism

Last week, I was giddy over the promulgation of the new Constitution. But, upon further reflection, I am now worried – very worried. I have come back down to earth, and the view from here is not very pretty.

Why am I so morose so soon after the most historic event in Kenya’s history since 1964? I have now carefully dissected the voting patterns in the referendum, and I don’t like what I see.

True, the ultimate result was great, but how and why “Kenyans” voted the way they did is proof that we are politically immature.  
The vote is illustrative of endemic pathologies and psychosis that stalk – and could derail – the implementation of the new Constitution.

I know that everyone is now lovey-dovey, and the last thing you want to hear is bad news. I don’t want to rain on your parade, but burying your head in the sand is not the answer. Dark and ominous clouds are gathering ahead.

That’s why the invitation of President Omar al-Bashir to attend the promulgation of the Constitution should give you pause. But that’s just one of our many problems. A closer analysis of the referendum results reveals that Kenya could be in big – big – trouble.

I have discovered that the ugly spectre of tribalism was a key driver of the vote. “Kenyans” voted largely along tribal lines. It’s fashionable to single out the Kalenjin for whipping because they voted – overwhelmingly – against the Constitution.

No single Kalenjin-dominated constituency voted for it. The dizzyingly high margins of the vote were simply astounding. Such margins call into question whether the choice of the voters was “thoughtful” and “deliberative.”

How could the Kalenjin, as a people, see the Constitution so differently from other groups? But the Kalenjin were hardly alone in voting as a tribal bloc.  
In Luo Nyanza, there was not a single constituency that voted against the Constitution.

The Luo simply flipped the Kalenjin script and voted in reverse. The margins of victory in each constituency were simply absurd. In Karachuonyo, for example, the “Yes” vote was 100 per cent. Such a high degree of unanimity is a manifestation of the lack of dissent, an essential feature of democracy.

Similar votes, though less emphatically unanimous, were recorded among the Kikuyu in Central Province where every constituency went “Yes”. Somalis voted in lock step too, as did the bulk of the Luhya, Kisii, Meru, and others.

They were as guilty as the Kalenjin for voting in unison. The pot cannot call the kettle black.  
The one exception to the pathology of tribal bloc voting was the Kamba.

The Akamba almost evenly split their vote – 51 per cent “Green” to 49 per cent “Red”. On the face of it, the Akamba appear to have rejected the “herd mentality”. But hold your horses before you declare the Kamba electorate more enlightened than other “Kenyans”.

**Quick analysis**

Did the Akamba vote largely based on “issues” in the draft constitution – as individuals – or did they vote as a tribe? More serious study down the road will tell us more.

But my quick analysis suggests that the Kamba voted on the issues but also with an eye to advancing the “tribal” presidential interests of Vice-President Kalonzo Musyoka.

Mr Musyoka confused the Kamba and, in the process, misled them. First, his identity among leaders who stood in the middleground. When he finally endorsed the document, he did so reluctantly and only after President Kibaki leaned heavily on him, and Christians defied the clergy.

By then it was too late for him to totally reverse the “No” tide in Ukambani because the churches, especially the Africa Inland Church, had scored points on the issues.

Initially, he was opposed to the Constitution for three reasons – the Christian clergy was opposed to it; Prime Minister Raila Odinga, his political nemesis, was for it; and his partners in the KKK alliance – Higher Education minister William Ruto and Finance minister Uhuru Kenyatta – were against it.

My point is that the Akamba, like other “Kenyans,” would have most likely voted as a bloc had Mr Musyoka clearly – consistently and forcefully – adopted a single position and stuck to it.

I have no doubt that the Kamba would have gone “Red” had he unequivocally joined Mr Ruto in the “No” camp.  
Folks should not underestimate the sentiment among the Kamba – like other groups – to see one of their own in the State House.

I believe a large part of the “No” vote in Ukambani was to deny Mr Odinga political mileage over their “favourite son”. Most communities followed “their” ethnic kingpins. The Kikuyu heeded President Kibaki. The Luo followed Mr Odinga.

**Tribal loyalties**

That’s why no one should be surprised that the Kalenjin followed Mr Ruto and former President Daniel arap Moi while the Luhya went with Deputy Prime Minister Musalia Mudavadi.

The “issues” raised by the churches were trumped by tribal loyalties and subservience to ethnic leaders.  
I believe only Muslims voted for the Constitution based on an issue – Kadhi courts; perhaps the issue most important to them as a group.

Otherwise, the referendum vote was no different from the 2007 General Election, or other previous polls in Kenya. The tribe still rules the ballot box.  
This portends a very serious problem for the implementation of the Constitution.

The enemies of democracy will exploit this deficit to try and kill reforms.  
Paradoxically, the Constitution birthed by tribalism must be used to kill tribalism.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
