# Graft on the spot at World Social Forum

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2007-01-21T21:00:00Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/news/graft-on-the-spot-at-world-social-forum-528686  

---

Kenyans attending the ongoing World Social Forum in Nairobi yesterday called for a special effort to fight corruption and punish those responsible for ethnic clashes.

Members of the Pastoralists Minority Group  arrive for World Social Forum talks at Kasarani yesterday.

The Kenyan delegates demanded the establishment of a truth and reconciliation commission to deal with past and current political, social and economic crimes.

The forum, which has brought together more than 50,000 delegates from developed and developing countries, has been convened to discuss the many problems afflicting the world’s poor and marginalised groups. The meeting is discussing insecurity, the Aids pandemic, climate change and other global challenges.

Former Constitution of Kenya Review Commission secretary PLO Lumumba set the ball rolling with a request for a home-grown initiative to identify and prosecute or forgive those responsible for the crimes that have hampered the country’s development.

Dr Lumumba, a law lecturer at the University of Nairobi, said those behind the tribal clashes in the run-up to the 1992 and 1997 multi-party elections, in which scores of lives were lost, hundreds displaced and property worth millions of shillings destroyed, should be pursued and held to account for their evil activities.

On the economic front, he called for the prosecution of those who printed money to finance the 1992 multi-party elections after nearly two decades of single-party dictatorship.

The effects of massive injection of cash into the economy for political reasons during the Kanu reign are still being felt today.

Dr Lumumba singled out YK’92 as one of those organisations whose activities must be thoroughly investigated and the masterminds punished for any wrongs they may have committed.

The former CKRC official also said Kenyans needed to know why civilians were shot by presidential guards in Kisumu in 1969, when the founding President, Mzee Jomo Kenyatta, visited the lakeside town.

His views were supported by other Kenyan delegates to the talks.

ODM-Kenya interim secretary-general Peter Anyang’ Nyong’o said the discarded Bomas Draft had a provision for the establishment of such a commission to help the country come to terms with its traumatic past.

**Political anxiety**

Dr Lumumba and Prof Nyong’o were speaking during the second day of the forum at Jeevanjee Gardens, which has come amid increasing political anxiety ahead of this year’s General Election.

The poll is shaping up as what has been described by some politicians as a two-horse race between Narc Kenya, which has proposed President Kibaki as its flagbearer, and ODM-K.

Yesterday, the Kenyan delegates said increased tribalism, political, economic and social crimes threatened to tear the country apart.

Had a truth and reconciliation commission been established, they said, it would have acted as a deterrent for present economic crimes, including Anglo Leasing, in which top personalities both inside and outside government were implicated.

Vice-President Moody Awori, Energy minister Kiraitu Murungi and former minister David Mwiraria were last week cleared by the Kenya Anti-Corruption Commission (KACC) of any wrong doing in the Anglo Leasing scandal.

The decision was promptly denounced by the opposition leaders as an attempt to cleanse those implicated ahead of the forthcoming elections.

The delegates, among them politicians, professionals, representatives of civil society, singled out tribalism as the greatest threat to the country’s social cohesion.

They were attending a parallel meeting dubbed Citizens Social Forum with other delegates from East Africa.

They also blamed the recurring ethnic clashes on the Government’s inability to deal with historical injustices

Tribal clashes have been reignited, since the Kibaki administration came to power in 2002, with the latest being in Mt Elgon, which has claimed 49 lives and left hundreds homeless. Politicians have been yet again accused of being behind the clashes with some funding a rag-tag group known as the Sabaot Lands Defence Forces (SLDF) to terrorise local residents.

Clashes have also occurred in Nakuru and Laikipia districts and North Eastern Province. Though competition for the limited natural resources is partly to blame for the conflicts, leaders have also had a share in the clashes by inciting their people against others. Dr Lumumba said: “The commission would act as a deterrent and hold those involved in the crimes responsible. And Kenyans must select criteria of those to be forgiven and prosecuted.” The World Social Forum is being held in Africa for the first time.

The delegates are expected to come up with recommendations in the five-day meeting to be shared with world leaders expected to converge in Davos, Switzerland, for the World Economic Forum.

**World’s economy**

The forum brings together top business leaders, national leaders and selected intellectuals to deliberate and shape the future of the world’s economy. This year’s meeting, whose theme is Shifting Power Equation, will be held from January 24 to 28.

Dr Lumumba told the delegates that Kenya must deal with its “dark past” the way South Africa and Latin American countries did.

“It is wrong for a country to have collective and convenient amnesia of deliberately forgetting its past,” said Dr Lumumba.

The establishment of a truth and reconciliation commission was one of the grand promises of the Narc Government.

It was supposed to deal with the historical injustices ranging from political to economic crimes since independence.

Some of the atrocities it was supposed to deal with include the infamous tribal clashes during the Moi era and various economic crimes ranging from land grabbing to outright theft from public coffers.

A task force chaired by law scholar Prof Makau Mutua recommended the establishment of a commission to enable Kenyans to reconcile with each other.

Yesterday, Dr Lumumba said such a commission must be enshrined in the constitution and legislation on its operations and functions formulated.

Prof Nyong’o said the discarded Bomas Draft had a provision for the establishment of a truth and reconciliation commission.

“The Bomas draft tried to deal with the politics of inclusion by collecting views from the public and enabling popular participation in Bomas but it was thrown out by the Kibaki administration.”

Dr Lumumba said Kenya’s greatest enemies were the perpetrators of economic crimes in the Kenyatta, Moi and Kibaki regimes.

“We must know why money was printed in a ginger group called YK 92 (Youth for Kanu) and under whose instructions,” he added. “Some of the culprits have now re-packaged themselves and think that time has cured them. They are even branding themselves liberators,” he said.
