# An open letter to the ICC chief prosecutor 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2011-10-01T14:33:24Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/an-open-letter-to-the-icc-chief-prosecutor--785200

## What you need to know:

* **Newborns:** An anonymous tipster told me the other day that villagers who can barely pronounce your name are naming newborns after you
* You have summoned the highest and mightiest to the corridors of justice. Such an action has no precedent in Kenya

Sir, today I write neither to glorify, nor to vilify, you. Rather, I have picked up my pen to reflect on your legacy on Kenya.

I am sure you know that your fame has spread far and wide in Kenya. You can’t imagine just how deeply you’ve penetrated the zeitgeist of Kenyans.

An anonymous tipster told me the other day that villagers who can barely pronounce your name are naming newborns after you.

In one village that shall remain nameless, a father named his son Okambo. The poor fellow doesn’t know he corrupted your name. In another, a mother christened her daughter Ocabo. But a family blessed with twins got it right — naming them Ocampo I and Ocampo II.

I can’t verify these stories. But I wouldn’t be shocked if they were true. It really wouldn’t matter whether they were fact or fiction. What’s important is that they exist. I know you trade in evidence of the legal kind.

But these tales are evidence of another kind. They exemplify the fascination of the Kenyan mind with your person. But it’s really not about you — it’s got to do with the seismic impact you’ve already had on Kenya.

To some, you would be massive if you were a “heart attack”. To others, you would be the most commanding mugumo [fig] tree, if you were a plant. Both friend and foe agree on one thing — you are titanic.

Opinion polls show that more Kenyans like than hate you. Those who like you think you are a godsend. Those who hate you think you are the devil incarnate.

I hope the haters don’t bother you. That’s because their fury is blinded by the basest of human emotions — tribal nativism and gross impunity. Pay them no heed.

I can tell you this — no one has ever come as close as you to slaying the dragon of impunity in Kenya.

When all is said and done, you may free Kenyans from a predatory political class. How strange would that be — an Argentine “liberating” Kenyans from themselves? It’s even stranger that “freedom” would come from a courtroom at The Hague.

Let me tell you why your name will live forever in the annals of Kenyan history. I know that you are a long way from your sworn goal — getting the International Criminal Court to convict the Ocampo Six for crimes against humanity. That may, or may not, happen. You’ve first got to convince the court they’ve a case to answer.

**Huge barriers**

Then you’ve got to prove their guilt “beyond a reasonable doubt”. These are huge barriers. Your swagger and legal skills will be put to the test. I know you believe you’ve got the goods on the Ocampo Six. But you’ve got to convince the judges.

However, in my view, you’ve already won. You’ve transformed Kenya’s political culture.

First, you have given the “little man” a voice. No Kenyan institution could — or would — stand up for the “little man”. You have defrocked Kenya’s malignant political class in front of the “little men.”

The emperor is now naked. You’ve summoned the highest and mightiest to the corridors of justice. Such an action has no precedent in Kenya.

Historians may record the ICC trials as the most important milestone of the so-called Second Liberation. In a literary prism, this may be Kenya’s “Mubarak moment”.

Symbolically, The Hague could be Kenya’s Tahrir Square. The “little man” — just like the average Egyptian — may be freed from the fear and spell of “Big Men”. This is nothing short of a political revolution.

Second, what you’ve done at The Hague will have a profound impact on Kenya’s legal culture. It could quicken the “judicial revolution” already under way. Kenyan courts have been utterly impotent. They’ve existed as a protection racket for the wealthy and powerful. But that’s changing under Dr Willy Mutunga, the new Chief Justice.

It’s because of Kenya’s feckless legal system that you instituted investigations. Dr Mutunga has sworn that the courts will be transformed to effectively try similar cases in the future. This is as it should be. Your work has shown the Kenyan courts what’s possible. You’ve shamed them.

They must now stand up to be counted. Unwittingly, you’ve paved the way for a judicial revolution in Kenya.

Third, your impact on the 2012 elections is undeniable. Political careers may either be terminated or rejuvenated. At least two of the Ocampo Six — Finance Minister Uhuru Kenyatta and Eldoret North MP William Ruto — are declared presidential candidates.

They will be out of the race if charges against them are confirmed. But they could bounce back stronger if you fail to convince the judges to bind them for trial. Your pursuit of the two will either elevate or decimate them.

**Next head of state**

Either prospect will determine who Kenya’s next head of state becomes. Bottom line — you already have a hand in choosing Kenya’s next leader. This could be either very good, or very bad, for Kenya. Your “intervention” is truly fateful.

Finally, you will cause Kenyans to re-examine their fixation with the tribe. The allegations you’ve already made are chilling.

Poor people — from different tribes — killed each other like animals for “Big Men”. This simple, but demonic, fact is undeniable. Kenyans know it, but they haven’t had an honest national conversation about it.

The proceedings at The Hague could force much soul-searching among Kenyans. I know many Kenyans who already “feel dirty” they took sides for “the tribe”. Tribal identity may truly never go away for most people, but it needn’t be abused for “Big Men”.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
