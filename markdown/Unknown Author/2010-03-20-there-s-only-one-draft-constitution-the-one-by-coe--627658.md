# There’s only one draft constitution, the one by CoE 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-03-20T14:15:45Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/there-s-only-one-draft-constitution-the-one-by-coe--627658  

---

Key political actors must stop behaving, and talking, as though there are two different draft constitutions – the Naivasha Draft and the CoE Draft. Only one single draft constitution is legally on offer – the CoE Draft.

Those conflating the Naivasha Consensus with a draft constitution have either failed to understand constitution making as a “process” or are engaged in political skullduggery with a filibuster in mind.

What happened in Naivasha was a small, albeit politically significant, part of a long process. It was not the end of the road, nor could the product of the PSC consensus be the final word.

That’s why it’s obstructionist for anyone to suggest that the CoE overstepped its bounds in “processing” the PSC consensus into the draft now before Parliament.

But no one should minimise the political breakthrough in Naivasha. Even though some in ODM are grumbling over the Naivasha accord, the review process would have been virtually dead had the PSC not come to agreement. Naivasha was a small step for the PSC, but a giant leap for the process.

But those in PNU – now christened PDM – should cease and desist from acting as though Naivasha was cast in stone. They want to use Naivasha as a pretext for denying Kenyans the long awaited constitution.

In any case, if truth were told, there is not much normative daylight between the Naivasha agreement and the CoE Draft.

That’s why it’s a red herring to manufacture fictional distinctions between the two to derail the process. In my view, the so-called Naivasha Draft was a “political” document informed by “law.” The CoE Draft, on the other hand, is a “legal” document informed by “politics.”

In reality, a constitution is a social contract that assumes a “legal” form although it is a “political” bargain. In other words, the constitution making process is the management of political hatreds through law.

A constitution is neither law, nor politics. It is both by nature and process. Only the CoE, by virtue of its central role in drafting the constitution, has the imprimatur to bestow “legal” legitimacy on the draft constitution.

The PSC and Parliament, on the other hand, have purely “political” roles to play. They can inform the CoE where the political centre lies, but they cannot usurp its unique “legal” mandate to translate the “political” will into a “legal” document.

A constitution is a moral document because it embodies within its normative edifice the values of a society. For example, a constitution that provides for the death penalty, or defines life as beginning at conception, makes a profound statement about the moral – or immoral – centre of a society.

A constitution that is hateful of the poor – by failing to provide for economic and social rights – speaks volumes about the nature of a society.

That’s why a constitution is a window into the “mind” of a country, and the character of its political elite. It is for this reason that constitutions must be aspirational – to capture the dynamic nature of society, and pragmatic – to reflect contemporary realities. It is a document for today and the ages.

I have no doubt that the moral centre of the most legitimate constitutions is a negotiation between law and politics. That’s why the most enduring modern constitutions avoid extremist provisions and reject sectarian or communal biases.

We must bear in mind that Kenya is a secular republic, not a theocracy like Iran or Saudi Arabia.

Religious leaders who are breathing fire and brimstone should show some humility and acknowledge that our constitution will not – and must not – be a religious document.

The republic and its basic law cannot be captive to – or an extension of – the Bible or the Koran. The clergy must stop threatening the review process with Armageddon because of their views on abortion and other wedge issues.

Neither John Cardinal Njue, nor the Chief Kadhi, is an ayatollah. They, and other clerics, must not hold the draft constitution hostage.

For over two decades, we have endured an excruciating and exorbitantly expensive quest for a constitution.

We are now within an eyelash of that dream. Let’s agree that the perfect constitution – one with which everyone completely agrees – is not possible. Let’s reflect on the fundamental reason for a new constitution.

There is little doubt that the drive for a new constitution has been to contain executive despotism and to empower the citizenry. This has been the core raison d’être of a new constitution. It was not a quest to fight “cultural” or “social” wars.

This is why I am appealing to the religious establishment to stop turning the struggle for a constitution into a referendum on faith. Kenya belongs to all those who live in it – the faithful, agnostics, unbelievers. All these groups must be equal in the eyes of the law.

Finally, I want to appeal to President Kibaki and Prime Minister Raila Odinga to speak out without equivocation in support of the CoE draft. Only the two of them have the political clout to push the draft constitution over the finish line.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
