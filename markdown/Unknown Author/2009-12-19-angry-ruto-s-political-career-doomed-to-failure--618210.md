# Angry Ruto’s political career doomed to failure

## What you need to know:

* Rift Valley politician must know that he cannot be the top dog in KKK alliance

Although I am not ready to write the political obituary of William Samoei Ruto, his days atop the political landscape are most likely numbered.

I say so because blinding ambition often leads one to political oblivion.

The minister for Agriculture, is an angry man these days, but he is in a fight for his political life with Prime Minister Raila Odinga.

The totality of Mr Ruto’s career suggests an individual who will do anything – in true Machiavellian tradition – to ascend to the top.

While it is Mr Ruto’s democratic prerogative to seek the State House, he is doomed to failure unless he abandons ethnic demagoguery and articulates a reformist agenda for Kenya.

Mr Ruto was not born with a silver spoon in his mouth. In fact, he owes his political career to Mr Moi. He burst onto the political scene at the tender age of 25 through the Youth for Kanu ’92 – the notorious State-supported outfit that was formed to defeat political opposition.

YK’92, designed to save Mr Moi’s political skin in the first multiparty elections since the end of the Cold War, was adversely mentioned in ethnic attacks against communities deemed opposed to Kanu – in the Rift Valley. Mr Moi triumphed in that violent election and that is how Mr Ruto earned Mr Moi’s ear and trust.

In 1997, five years later at the age of 30, Mr Ruto was elected MP for Eldoret North on a Kanu ticket.

He became one of the party’s fiercest hawks, a posture for which the President rewarded him with the post of Kanu Secretary-General and minister for Home Affairs in 2002.

He became one of the leading anti-reformers. He earned a reputation for political ruthlessness and came to be seen as a possible heir apparent to Mr Moi in the Rift Valley.

But the defeat of Kanu in 2002 complicated Mr Ruto’s path to the pinnacle of political power. He realised that Mr Moi, now out of power, was no longer useful to him.

But he knew that he had to politically “kill” his political father if he hoped to supplant him and take over the Rift Valley. That moment came in 2006.

Mr Ruto could smell political blood when Mr Moi decided to support President Mwai Kibaki against ODM, the opposition juggernaut formed by Mr Raila Odinga.

Capitalising on President Kibaki’s unpopularity among the Kalenjin for “sidelining” them, Mr Ruto successfully painted Mr Moi as a traitor to the Kalenjin.

Mr Ruto must have sensed, correctly, that the Kalenjin would support the Odinga-led ODM. He threw a token hat in the race for the ODM presidential candidacy knowing that he would be road kill for Mr Odinga.

But the move would elevate his status in the Rift Valley in ODM. That is how he claimed to have “led” the Kalenjin into ODM. By leaving Kanu and joining ODM, Mr Ruto had now become a “reformer”.

In the 2007 elections, the Kalenjin voted overwhelmingly for Mr Odinga. In a bid to end the post-election violence that followed the presidential election results, Mr Odinga became Prime Minister.

**Rival parties**

The bulk of post-election violence took place in the Rift Valley where youths attacked communities deemed supportive of rival parties – either PNU or ODM.

Fierce counter-attacks were mounted, a fact that analysts believed led to a checkmate, the eventual ceasefire, and the national accord.

But Mr Ruto came to believe that it was Kalenjin blood that had delivered Mr Odinga the premiership. That is why he has treated Mr Odinga as his political hostage.

For one, he expected to replace Mr Musalia Mudavadi as the unquestioned number two in ODM. He succeeded in sharing that slot with the Deputy Prime Minister.

Human rights groups have mentioned Mr Ruto in post-election violence. Mr Ruto himself has acted as though he knows that his name is in the Waki Envelope that Mr Kofi Annan handed over to Mr Luis Moreno-Ocampo, the Prosecutor of the International Criminal Court.

It is on this issue that relations between Mr Odinga and Mr Ruto have been put asunder.

While Mr Odinga has supported the prosecution of the suspects, Mr Ruto has threatened dire consequences should the suspects be arrested or prosecuted in the Hague.

Mr Ruto believes that Mr Odinga has hung him out to dry. That is why he has mobilised a sizable chunk of Rift Valley MPs to oppose Mr Odinga over the reclamation of the Mau Forest.

It is high noon in ODM. Either Mr Odinga will crush Mr Ruto and his band of rebels, or Mr Ruto will break away with his chunk of MPs and lead them into the KKK alliance to join Deputy Prime Minister Uhuru Kenyatta, Vice-President Kalonzo Musyoka, and Tourism minister Najib Balala to try and rally the Kalenjin, Kamba, Kikuyu and portions of the Coast against ODM and Mr Odinga.

But Mr Ruto must know that he cannot be the top dog in the KKK alliance. That is why his anger is unlikely to subside anytime soon.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights Commission*.
