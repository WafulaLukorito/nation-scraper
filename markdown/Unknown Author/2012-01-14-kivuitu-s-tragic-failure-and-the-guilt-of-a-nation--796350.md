# Kivuitu’s tragic failure and the guilt of a nation 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2012-01-14T13:21:18Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/kivuitu-s-tragic-failure-and-the-guilt-of-a-nation--796350  

---

Popular history loves a whipping boy. Except “popular history” isn’t real history.

It’s a quack’s version of the real thing. I only revisit this painful subject because of a recent interview the former chairman of the defunct Electoral Commission of Kenya (ECK) Samuel Kivuitu gave to the media.

In it, Mr Kivuitu is combative, unrepentant and loquacious. He’s even cantankerous. Is Mr Kivuitu simply unreflective, or is he on to something?

Should we dismiss him simply as a bitter old grandpa who will be remembered as the man who hurriedly – and cowardly – swore in President Mwai Kibaki at dusk?

Or was he a hapless victim set up by an evil system? Is he a malignant revisionist, someone who rewrites and bends history to suit his own purposes?

I am sad that Mr Kivuitu has been unwell. It’s incumbent upon every man and woman of goodwill to wish him a quick and complete recovery.

However, that shouldn’t stop us from asking the hard questions, especially when Mr Kivuitu seeks to absolve himself while apportioning blame for the 2008 polls violence.

But perhaps he should use his physical convalescence to seek a spiritual rebirth.

In the interview, I was shocked to see that Mr Kivuitu blamed everyone else – except himself – for the post-election mayhem.

His bravura betrayed no introspection about Kenya’s darkest hour. Which begs the question – should we blame him for the bungled election? The answer is yes and no. He can’t escape partial responsibility.

Mr Kivuitu was very profligate with the truth. Such profligacy with facts should be impermissible for a man his age.

His legacy will be better served by a candid admission of his faults. No man is perfect. Did he cause the polls violence?

In the interview, he quickly dug in, suggesting that he did nothing wrong. How can the verdict of history be wrong, and how can he be the only one who is right?

He should know that others – not he – will write the history of those fateful days after the 2007 elections sent Kenya into paroxysms.

I know that history won’t be kind to him. That’s why he must come clean for history to give him a fair shake. Several things Mr Kivuitu said struck me as outlandish.

First, he said that he “read” the election results given to him. Mr Kivuitu wants us to believe that he was a “mere reader” of numbers shoved in his face by mere functionaries.

This is simply astounding. Was Mr Kivuitu – as chair of the ECK – a mere mannequin? Wasn’t he the duly appointed executive holder of the lofty constitutional office?

What would he have done – let’s suppose – had he been given election results showing that Professor Makau Mutua had won the presidential election?

Would he have – as a “mere reader” – pronounced me the duly elected president? His logic is simply absurd and incredulous.

He makes a mockery of his office. Presumably, Mr Kivuitu was appointed chair of the ECK because of his competence in electoral matters, his integrity and sense of probity, and his sense of judgment.

The chair of ECK wasn’t a pedestrian’s job. Or perhaps it should have been if Mr Kivuitu is to be believed.

In that case, any member of the hoi polloi could have been chair. Perhaps that was the whole point the “appointing authority” meant by giving him the job.

Mr Kivuitu wants us to believe that he was a “mere mouthpiece”. We know that he acted like one. But did he have to?

My answer is a loud resounding “no”. He should have refused to announce the results. Perhaps that could have saved Kenya.

**Taken to a room**

Secondly, Mr Kivuitu says that he was “taken to a room” by security forces and forced to announce the results on K*BC.*

Is he admitting that he was “abducted by State agents” and forced to declare a winner even though he didn’t know that Mr Kibaki had won fairly?

Why would he have been abducted if Mr Kibaki had won fair and square? Wasn’t it Mr Kivuitu’s job to ascertain that the victor had won “fairly” before announcing the winner?

What is the purpose of an electoral body – and its chair – if it can’t ascertain the winner is elected in a free and fair election?

I cannot fathom what Mr Kivuitu was talking about. I am simply left speechless. Thirdly, Mr Kivuitu opines that the violence was premeditated, and was bound to happen anyway.

If Mr Kivuitu knew that as a fact, why didn’t he “ring the bell” and postpone the election?

When, how, and where did he know that the violence was pre-planned? Did he know the planners and the financiers of the violence?

If so, shouldn’t he be a witness for the prosecution at the International Criminal Court? What kind of a patriot would have such vital information and not divulge it to the nation?

Finally, Mr Kivuitu says that he knew the composition of the ECK was fatally flawed because Mr Kibaki refused to consult the opposition.

Why, then, did he agree to chair it? Mr Kivuitu was a brilliant lawyer, politician, and judge in his heyday.

I’m at a loss to explain what happened to him along the way. At some point, he became part of the kit and caboodle of impunity.

It’s true that PNU, ODM and ODM-K engaged in rigging the elections. So did the Executive through the security forces and the Provincial Administration.

So did many ordinary citizens. The whole country is guilty for conducting a flawed election. But several individuals – Mr Kivuitu key among them – tragically failed Kenya.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
