# MUTUA: I am convinced that Uhuru is his own worst enemy

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2011-09-24T10:32:17Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/mutua-i-am-convinced-that-uhuru-is-his-own-worst-enemy-784470  

---

They say that he who represents himself has a fool for a client.

Finance minister Uhuru Kenyatta, the most famous of the Ocampo Six, could fry himself at The Hague. Mr Kenyatta, Kenya’s fallen “prince,” must contain his emotions if he’s to be saved.

I know — unless the world is upside down — that no lawyer worth his salt would willingly let a suspect for crimes against humanity testify at a pre-trial hearing. It’s the equivalent of putting a bull’s eye on your client.

International Criminal Court (ICC) Prosecutor Luis Moreno-Ocampo will play target practice with Mr Kenyatta.

Take this to the bank — Mr Kenyatta will be nothing but road kill for Mr Moreno-Ocampo. It’s a stunning miscalculation and a fool’s errand.

By nature, “princes” are full of bravado and braggadocio. That’s because they believe they are better than the hoi polloi.

They don’t play by the rules that govern mere mortals. They act with impunity. They thumb their noses at society. They are “untouchable”.

**Mere earthlings**

Guess what? That may be true — but not at the ICC. Before the ICC, the mightiest are but mere earthlings.

This is a lesson that all the Ocampo Six need to internalise. Why? Because the outcome of a trial could send them to prison for 30 years in a foreign country.

It’s no time for tomfoolery. Which begs the question — why has Mr Kenyatta decided to play kamikaze at such an early stage in the game?

I have four reasons why Mr Kenyatta shouldn’t offer his head on a silver platter to Mr Moreno-Ocampo. First — and most important — criminal trials are not about the truth.

Criminal proceedings in modern courts don’t seek to find out the truth. I know this sounds like treason to the layman. But get this — criminal trials are determined by the weight of the evidence.

A guilty person — like a murderer who is as culpable as sin — will go scot-free if the prosecution cannot adduce enough evidence to prove guilt “beyond a reasonable doubt”.

Conversely, an innocent person can be convicted of a crime if the prosecution can overcome this high bar through evidence. Remember that evidence can be manufactured.

All that matters is that the “evidence” be presented in such a compelling manner that it’s believable by the court. Courts don’t seek to establish innocence or guilt.

**Weigh the evidence**

Their job is to weigh the evidence and decide whether the prosecution has proved its case. The court’s verdict has zero to do with the truth — or justice.

It’s all about who’s got the best evidence, and who can cast the best doubt on the evidence. This is why criminals can get off on legal technicalities.

That’s why guilty suspects with great lawyers often go scot-free. Mr Kenyatta makes a fatal mistake if he thinks that it will serve him well to take the stand and tell the “truth”.

The “truth” that Mr Kenyatta tells the court will instead be used against him by the prosecution.

Mr Moreno-Ocampo will seek to show through cross-examination that Mr Kenyatta is telling untruths. Mr Kenyatta’s “truth” will be turned into “lies” by Mr Moreno-Ocampo.

I can bet on it that Mr Moreno-Ocampo is salivating. He will seek to show that Mr Kenyatta can’t be believed — he contradicts himself, and makes up facts.

I would be very surprised if there isn’t a “deer-caught-in-the-headlights” moment for Mr Kenyatta.

By taking the stand, he will have given Mr Moreno-Ocampo the biggest gift of the confirmation hearings. It will be like shooting fish in a barrel. I won’t relish the sight.

Secondly, a trial is like a game of chess. One player cannot let the other know what he’s thinking, or what his next move will be.

Kenyatta will be fair game when he takes the stand as a witness. Mr Moreno-Ocampo can ask him anything as long as he lays a foundation.

Here’s the catch. Mr Kenyatta will not have time to consult with his lawyers while on the stand. Thus he won’t know what questions he shouldn’t answer, or what answers he should give.

Mr Moreno-Ocampo will attempt to slap him repeatedly to get him “punch-drunk”. He’ll try to confuse him thoroughly to elicit “admissions” from Mr Kenyatta. I don’t know why Mr Kenyatta wants to show his cards.

Thirdly, confirmation hearings are not the place to “empty your gun”. Mr Moreno-Ocampo hasn’t revealed much of the evidence that he would rely on in the actual trial.

The hurdle that he must clear at this stage to get the Ocampo Six committed to trial is so low that virtually any lawyer can meet it.

Why, then, would Mr Kenyatta’s lawyers allow him to “walk naked” into the “lion’s den” at this stage? It’s almost a sacrificial move.

It looks to me like either a death wish, or a Hail Mary. It’s a move that’s so desperate that one wonders whether Mr Kenyatta’s lawyers have given up on him. Why throw him to the wolves, or allow him to self-destruct?

**Clear benefit**

Finally, the accused should generally avoid taking the stand unless there is a clear benefit, or it’s the last arrow in the quiver. We are not there yet.

The main dance hasn’t even begun. The defence should only consider using Mr Kenyatta as a witness after seeing the entirety of the prosecution’s case.

Anything else is close to malpractice. I am convinced that Mr Kenyatta is his own worst enemy. He probably insisted on taking the stand against the advice of his lawyers.

This know-it-all-princely-problem could boomerang on Mr Kenyatta and sink him.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
