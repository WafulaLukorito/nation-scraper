# Why our ‘national’ eulogies are hypocritical 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2012-03-24T15:33:05Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/why-our-national-eulogies-are-hypocritical--805552  

---

They say “thou shalt not speak ill of the dead”. Let me confess – I have no such intention.

But lying about the dead is equally reprehensible. The measure of a society is its ability to tell the truth, even when the truth is inconvenient.

But some lies about the dead are shocking. Imagine the lie that the late North Korean dictator Kim Jong Il invented the hamburger.

The “dear leader” may have been a great man, but I doubt he even knew how to cook a hamburger. We shouldn’t bury the truth with the dead, especially if they were statesmen.

Unless we are a country of blatant liars, our nation’s eulogy to a departed leader should be compassionate but truthful.

**Gema leader**

This brings me to Kenya’s handling of the deaths of former Gema leader Njenga Karume and politician-businessman John Michuki.

Each man was eerily iconic. Somehow, we can’t imagine the making of modern Kenya without the two men. Their signatures – and DNA – run deep within the Kenyan state.

They fundamentally shaped the culture of politics, bureaucracy, and business in a way that few have. Yet both epitomised the contradictions of the Kenyan elite of a certain age.

Both personified the inner sanctum of power and wealth, even during the height of the anti-Kikuyu Kanu regime under former President Daniel arap Moi.

Were they Kenyans, or Kikuyus? Could they have been both? Did they foster, or undermine, the idea of Kenya?

I listened carefully to the “national” eulogies for the two men. Particularly perplexing were tributes in Parliament and at memorial services.

I swear I didn’t recognise the men who were being eulogised. I don’t want to pick a fight, but it’s my job as a social critic to say what others can’t, and won’t, say.

There’s a potent word in the English language – revisionism. Used in the study of history, it means “doctoring” or “rewriting” a narrative to suit the desired “facts”.

In plain English, to “revise history” is to “lie about history”. We lied about Mzee Jomo Kenyatta when he died.

We’ve lied about every major politician who has died. I am afraid this includes Mr Michuki and Mr Karume.

How have we lied about Mr Michuki and Mr Karume? The key potentates of our state – from President Mwai Kibaki down – would have us believe that their two departed colleagues were the epitome of virtue.

According to them, the English language has a shortage of words to describe the greatness of the two men.

Words like “brilliant businessman,” “model public servant,” great mentor and counsellor,” “consistent and unbending,” “principled,” “irreplaceable,” “benign,” “inclusive,” and “patriotic” have been thrown around like sweet candy.

None of these words were uttered in irony. Grown men and women – not relatives of the two – have wept in broad daylight.

The “nation” was steeped in “deep mourning”. Really? Or is this mere revisionism? Let me tell the truth, and what the deaths of the two “important men” don’t tell us.

There’s no denying the tribal kleptocracy around Mzee Kenyatta designed the structure and norms of modern Kenya. Mr Kenyatta’s regime spawned a huge tribal political class, business elite, and bureaucracy.

You can’t understand the history of the Kenyan state without comprehending the nature of this tribal elite.

Within the state bureaucracy, Mr Michuki was a guiding light. He acquired his considerable skill under the colonial regime.

Within business, Mr Karume made Gema the linchpin for Kikuyu hegemony.

Gema was the umbilical cord that fused the state to the Kikuyu elite. Gema was the instrument for the full tribalisation of the State.

**Average peasant**

My view is that Mr Karume would have died an average peasant were it not for his tribal ancestry. He cunningly used his tribal pedigree to become a tribal godfather.

He was an exclusivist chauvinist who – with the late MP Kihika Kimani and minister Paul Ngei – banded in an unholy alliance in the “Change the Constitution Group” to deny Mr Moi the presidency upon Mzee Kenyatta’s death.

Mr Karume was determined that the State House stays within the house of Mumbi.

I find it mildly interesting that, in his twilight, Mr Karume was gravitating towards Prime Minister Raila Odinga and away from former Finance minister Uhuru Kenyatta.

But that may be due to Mr Kenyatta’s woes at The Hague. Mr Michuki was more urbane, but equally committed to the project of tribal hegemony.

Perhaps more than anyone else, he was the personification of the vaunted bureaucratic and business elite that emerged at independence.

He was a powerful man who didn’t suffer fools kindly. He believed in the rule of the iron fist.

The horrible raid at the *Standard* newspaper and his “shoot-to-kill” Mungiki policy will live in infamy. He was an unapologetic curmudgeon with a dictator’s mettle.

His true tribal colours came out in his twilight when he declared Mr Kenyatta the “king” of the Kikuyu and asked related groups to line up behind Jomo’s son.

To many Kenyans, he symbolised the so-called big tribe arrogance and superiority complex. My point is that our “national” eulogies are hypocritical.

While I mourn with everyone – as I do for every fallen soul – the deaths of Mr Karume and Mr Michuki, I won’t allow myself to suffer from amnesia. Nor should you.

That’s because how we understand the Karume-Michuki legacy has a lot to do with whether we can build a new Kenya.

I hope their passing has drawn a curtain on an era that we must overcome.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at SUNY Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
