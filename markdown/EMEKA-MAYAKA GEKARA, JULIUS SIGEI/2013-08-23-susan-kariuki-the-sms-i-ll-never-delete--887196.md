## What you need to know:

* The chief executive of Youth Agenda talks about the last text message she received from her mother and the books she is reading
* At 28, she became one of the youngest chief executives in country

She is as tough as nails. An admirer of Narc Kenya leader Martha Karua, Susan Kariuki-Mwongera became chief executive of the Youth Agenda at 28.

This thrust her into the responsibility of running a non-governmental organisation that not only serves as the nerve-centre of the youth movement in Kenya but also presides over multi-million shilling donor-funded programmes.

Unlike former MPs Wanyiri Kihoro and Koigi Wamwere, who have placed little faith in the Jubilee government, Mrs Mwongera thinks the dynamic duo at State House carries the hope of the youth of Kenya.

At 32, the Daystar University graduate who is now eyeing a post in the gender commission reckons that women should read the Bible to remain sane — but must be tough and rough.

She lost her mother in August 2012 in circumstances that still remain etched indelibly in her mind. The activist also told us why she was dying to marry a pastor. And she did.

**Q: You have consumed millions, perhaps billions, of shillings at the Youth Agenda being trained to be Kenyans first, your tribes second. How come Kikuyu youth still voted with Uhuru and their Luo counterparts with Raila?**

A. Kenyans should invest in their children and educate them to believe in nationhood, not their tribes. Tribalism is a manifestation of the way people are socialised as they grow up. It is difficult to change certain attitudes at a certain stage in one’s life.

Maybe young people should be encouraged to inter-marry across communities to create blood linkages. That will reduce tribal animosity and intolerance.

**Q. Talking of intolerance, human rights crusader Makau Mutua and literary scholar Evan Mwangi have staked out their all for gay relationships. When will time be ripe for Becky to marry Betty?**

A. The idea of same-sex marriage is an abomination. It has no religious or biological foundation. Our Constitution is clear that marriage is between members of the opposite sex. God created Adam and Eve. Gays should be affirmed in God’s teaching. Engaging in such activities is putting our treasures in the wrong place.

**Q: Kenyan youth depict consumption of alcohol, drug abuse and illicit sex as marks of heroism. When will the rain stop beating them?**

A. Blame it on unemployment, poor education and ignorance. It starts with the family. Girls these days flash cleavages before their fathers. If the grandmother complains, the girl’s mother would tell her ‘let her dress the way she wants. Ni time yake (it her time).’ Parents have left their job to teachers. We are victims of a total break-up of family values.

**Q: The media has lately been awash with youths using sexual enhancers. Why can’t they rise to the occasion?**

A: It is because of this culture of copy-and-paste in schools. Youngsters are copying cultures from the west. There is too much sex going round. It also goes back to parenting. Parents should impose sanctions on children that can make them fear and tremble.

I see a slump into blind secularism and experimentation. The shocking conversations I hear on FM stations about prostitution, incest, homosexuality and adultery are true. The other day a lady stood up in church and said ‘I am shocked my husband is sleeping with another man. I need help’. The filth in social media is a reflection of a rotten moral fabric.

**Q. And when will youths stop being used as doormats by politicians, embrace a transformative agenda to secure their future?**

A. We have stopped being used. A lot of young people aspired for positions of leadership in the last election and they were elected. Three senators and one governor are below 35. The National Assembly has 23 MPs who are also below 35 and 481 county representatives.

We have placed our hope and faith in them to deliver the youth agenda. But I am sad that I have not heard any of them speak about the Sh6 billion Uwezo fund. They first had to negotiate their salaries.

**Q: There is this notion that youthful leadership is the answer to the leadership question in Kenya, yet if it is corruption youthful leaders are as corrupt as they come.**

A. Like anybody else, young people can be corrupt but majority have integrity. Kenya requires servant leaders committed to serve with integrity.

**Q. A recent survey in the social media revealed that most young Kenyans interviewed have never heard of Bildad Kaggia. Is the country raising what someone has described as the Dumb Generation?**

A. Those concerns are true. Youths these days don’t want to get into anything intellectually engaging. Their discourse is defined by lack of intellect. Nobody wants to analyse complex issues.

They find comfort in celebrity gossip, music and sports. That is what is trending in the social media. Most of them have no time for history, economics, agriculture and literature and even debates and developments in the world of science.

I visited a university during the 2110 referendum. Not one student had read the Constitution, including Article 55 which anchors their issues.

**Q. But last year you declared that youths should not study anthropology**

A. No. I was saying that training in our institutions should be aligned to job market needs. For instance, the country should know how many archeologists and gynaecologists we require.

We are training too many lawyers to defend ourselves. What have we done to require so much defence? Anthropology is critical. How do you equip future generations without any knowledge of human societies, their developments and cultures?

**Q. What is the most transformative book you have read?**

A. The Power of a Praying Woman by Stormie Omartian. When I am down I shut up and pray. It has shaped and helped me manage my life as a Christian woman and wife. I have learnt not to fight back in certain situations.

But this does no mean I just sit. I am a fighter. I work very hard. I am tough. I can be rough. I seek wisdom and authority from God. I have seen God and I will never be ashamed to say so. But the most inspiring book is the Bible.

It is the best resource material on all subjects. I carry it wherever I go. It was inspired by God and all answers come from Him.

**Q. What are you reading now?**

A. Ha..ha..ha websites on the management of pregnancy and on parenting. I am looking forward to being one soon. I am also re-reading Who Moved My Cheese my by Spencer Johnson. It is the other book that has helped me deal with inevitable changes. I use it to encourage people to do their best and leave the rest to God.

I always make reference to David Kadalie’s Leader’s Resource Kit and John Maxwell’s Leadership. I want to be a good leader and mentor. My job involves a lot of reading when doing research and preparing papers.

I have been reading on socio-economics, devolution, the Middle East conflict, organisational behaviour, gender, the rise of the Asian tigers and public policy. I read on a need basis. But I read the Bible every day.

**Q. What book would you buy for Kenyan politicians?**

A. The writings of Mathew, John, Luke and Mark. They would learn something about Jesus’ humility and search for wisdom to provide solutions for mankind. He was servant leader. Kenyan politicians are too busy chasing money, power and luxury at the expense of service to the public.

Q: Media reports for the past week have been a sad commentary on exploitation of sheep by their shepherds. To whom should the vulnerable run?

A. What is happening is not new. Acts 20 cautions that “grievous wolves” would come and distort God’s message. They will speak perverse things, to draw away disciples after them.

They should be whipped out of church the way Jesus did to those who had turned His temple into a market. The remedy is for Christians to read and know the right gospel so that they can tell false prophets.

**Q. Living in the shadow of a pastor is certainly not every lady’s cup of coffee. Why a pastor?**

A. I always wanted to get married to a pastor and God blessed me with one. All the guys I interacted with closely in college were either Christian Union or worship leaders.

I draw a lot of inspiration from Matthew 6:33 which says: Seek you first the kingdom of God, and his righteousness; and all these things shall be added unto you. I was keen to build and be part of a relationship committed to create a treasure in Heaven.

**Q. You encouraged your mother and her team of Christian women to travel to Tanzania for evangelism. She did not come back alive.**

A. She came to Nairobi and had lunch with us on a Sunday. She was the leader of the Woman’s Guild at her church in Thika. She had come to process passports for her team which was travelling to Tanzania to evangelize.

The idea of them travelling to Dar es Salaam was mine. They initially wanted to visit Uganda but they could not after reports of an Ebola outbreak. I asked them ‘why don’t you go Arusha?. Mama said Arusha is too near. ‘Lets go to Dar es Salaam instead because it is much further and we will be able to see more of Tanzania.’

They left on Thursday, August 9, 2012 in two buses. All 80 of them. For some strange reason I had read a lot from Ecclesiastes that week. It teaches a lot about life and death.

I spoke to her more than twice on that day while at work. She told me they had got to Moshi. At around 10pm I called her to find out whether they had reached Dar. She took the call but we could not talk because there was so much noise in the background.

I realised they were singing. They sounded happy. I let them sing. I knew she will call. Then she text me. “I love you. Say hi to Sammy.” I slept. That is the last time we spoke. I got a call the following morning from my younger brother that mum and her team had been involved in a road accident. My world turned upside down. I will never delete that message.

**Q. Do you at times wish the trip never took place?**

God knows why He let it happen. She had finished her task. I have never gone back to her unmarked grave because I know her soul is not there. Her death came a year after that of my father and three months after my wedding which she had wanted held the same August. She was my role model. Life has not been easy without her.

**Q. Does your husband cook for you?**

Ha ha ha ha… before we got married he promised that he will be cooking ugali, nyama and rice for me. It has never happened. But he wakes up to prepare breakfast three times a week. He knows how to measure milk, cereals and I don’t know who taught him to buy the long bread.
