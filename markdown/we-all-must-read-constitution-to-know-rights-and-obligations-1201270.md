## What you need to know:

* Key politicians and public servants supportive of the Jubilee administration have neither read nor understood the Constitution of Kenya.
* The function of the security organs in the on-going anti-IEBC protests should be to provide protection and ensure peaceful and orderly demonstrations.
* The police must not act like youth-wingers at the service of politicians who do not understand and appreciate the gains enshrined under the Constitution.

It is obvious now that key politicians and public servants supportive of the Jubilee administration have never read, or at least have never understood, the Constitution of Kenya.

The 2010 Constitution was achieved through blood, sweat, and tears. It was supposed to be the final piece in two decades of the struggle for the establishment of a true democracy and ouster of a tyrannical, kleptocratic dictatorship.

It was not by accident that the progressive supreme law came with an extensive Bill of Rights, which, among other things, entrenched the constitutional right to demonstrate picket, march, assemble, and other expressions of democratic dissent.

These inalienable rights are not a gift or favour from the government of the day. The function of the government in situations where citizens want to express themselves in such fashion is to protect and provide an enabling environment.

It follows that the function of the security organs in the on-going protests against the discredited Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission should be to provide protection and ensure peaceful and orderly demonstrations.

The police must not act like youth-wingers at the service of politicians who do not understand and appreciate the gains enshrined under the Constitution.

**BETRAY OATH OF OFFICE**

They must not betray their oath of office nor offend the Constitution by employing violence, thuggery, and criminal tendencies to break legal processions.

By the same token, however, those organising the demonstrations also have to understand the Constitution. If they cared to peruse the document, they would learn that the right to demonstrate, picket, march, assemble, shout, sing, and dance is not absolute.

Demonstrations must be peaceful and orderly. They must not hinder the free movement of other persons. They do not have to be violent and characterised by looting, mugging, robbery, and the launching of crude missiles at the innocents.

So while the leadership of President Uhuru Kenyatta’s Jubilee alliance must read the Constitution and educate their political thugs and the Kenya Police Service that enjoyment of fundamental freedoms must not be unlawfully hindered, Mr Raila Odinga’s opposition Cord coalition must likewise educate its own bands of politicians and thugs that the demonstrations must be peaceful.

Jubilee must denounce MPs and other leaders claiming to act under the auspices of Gema (The Gikuyu, Embu, Meru Association), threatening to violently disrupt the Cord protests.

That one stinks, for policing and security is not an ethnic militia function, and neither are those hurt by the Cord protests exclusive to an ethnic group.

And I would want to believe that the government and its security organs are not an ethnic association. Any such militia formations must face the full force of the law.

Of course it also behoves the Cord leadership to police its own demonstrations. In civilised society groups given licence to march also deploy their own marshals to ensure that everyone is on their best behaviour. They also guard against crooked elements that might want to take advantage of the situation to rob, loot, rape, and plunder.

Cord was early to raise the alarm that Jubilee was intending to infiltrate the demonstrations and cause mayhem, but it cannot run away from the responsibility to police itself and isolate troublemakers.

I do not know whether it is the bushy visage, but many strangers are accosting me in the streets and pubs, addressing me as doctor, professor, engineer, and other highfalutin titles.

I wish to set the record straight: I am neither of the above. I have not read enough to earn such monikers and I will not bestow on myself false academic credentials.

But even if I had read that much, I would not make a big fuss about showing off my credentials and challenging those I reckon are not as learned as I. Such vanity I will leave to the likes of Makau Mutua, Peter Kagwanja, Mutahi Ngunyi, and Godwin Murunga.
