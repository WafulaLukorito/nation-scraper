# How to give effect to Uhuru’s reconciliation pledge

**Date:** 2014-01-04T20:29:29Z

## What you need to know:

* From the perspective of reconciliation, the government responded to the shock of the 2007/2008 violence in three main ways.
* Under political pressure, the contents of the report of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission were altered by some Kenyan commissioners without reference to their international counterparts.
* Kenyatta, after him, has been in direct conflict with some of them, and it remains unclear how he will achieve the reconciliation he seeks for the country under the circumstances.

President Uhuru Kenyatta used his New Year address to spell out the agenda that his government intends for the country in 2014, indicating that his administration will seek “reconciliation and unity towards inclusive growth” during the year.

I would like to suggest ways in which the President’s wishes can be given practical effect in the year.

Reconciliation became an important national issue following the violence experienced in Kenya, which was driven by hate-filled campaigns that preceded the fateful elections of 2007. The term “hate speech”, the use of which has become a little banal, entered Kenya’s popular consciousness in the wake of recriminations over what had caused the violence.

From the perspective of reconciliation, the government responded to the shock of the 2007/2008 violence in three main ways. First, through the formation of bureaucracies that would take responsibility for peace building and reconciliation in the country.

In this regard, the National Steering Committee on Peace building and Conflict Management was formed under the ministry of Special Programmes, while the National Cohesion and Integration Commission (NCIC) was established under the ministry of Justice and Constitutional Affairs, whose mandate now incorporated “national cohesion”.

Secondly, the government gave effect to the recommendations of the Makau Mutua Task Force, establishing a truth and reconciliation commission for Kenya. The commission, started by the grand coalition government, lingered in office long enough for the Jubilee government to come to power, and only handed its final report to President Kenyatta in May 2013.

Thirdly, the government appointed the Waki Commission whose recommendations were formally accepted by the government in late 2008. The commission had been mandated to suggest how accountability for post-election violence could be achieved.

In the period after the post-election violence, both the National Steering Committee and the NCIC have struggled to impose their mandates on the country. The Steering Committee claims success in preventing localised conflict in several places around the country including the Isiolo triangle, the Rift Valley and Nairobi.

In the lead-up to the constitutional referendum of 2010, the two bodies teamed up to form the Uwiano Platform which campaigned for peaceful voting, and in the period before the 2013 elections, the two also campaigned for peaceful polls.

One issue which the NCIC sought to address with little success was accountability for hate crimes. The commission commenced a criminal case against a senior member of the Kibaki government but, amid strong political resistance, the case eventually collapsed. Thereafter, the commission developed its own internal challenges which it had been unable to overcome by the time its term expired. As a tool for accountability against hate crimes, the cohesion commission had become hopeless.

Under political pressure, the contents of the report of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission were altered by some Kenyan commissioners without reference to their international counterparts. The subsequent protestations by the international commissioners received no sympathy from the government.

After Kenya failed to establish accountability measures as proposed by the Waki Commission, itself an internal accountability mechanism, the International Criminal Court moved in and is now prosecuting two Kenyan cases, one of them against the President. As a mechanism for accountability, the report of the Waki Commission has never been assessed independently of the ICC politics in which it is now engulfed.

The status of the President as an accused person before the ICC does not provide an ideal context for evaluating what is possible in relation to the recommendations of the Waki Commission.

The foregoing account leads to the conclusion that the tools for giving effect to the President’s wishes as to reconciliation are not in a good condition. The President would need to update and refresh these tools in a manner that might make them more effective than they are at the moment.

Secondly, the model of reconciliation that has been pursued in Kenya suffers from one fatal problem. It lacks a connection with everyday politics.

While the grand coalition government established the Waki Commission, the truth commission and the ministries that deal with cohesion and peace-building, there was no political reference to their work.

For example, while in office, President Kibaki never publicly affirmed or spoke about these bodies. Far from affirming any of these bodies, President Kenyatta, after him, has been in direct conflict with some of them, and it remains unclear how he will achieve the reconciliation he seeks for the country under the circumstances.

Thirdly, political practice is at variance with the stated objective of reconciling the country. The recent tiff within Jubilee over jobs and public contracts made it clear that the State is now the private enterprise of a section of the country — the one that supported Jubilee in the last elections.

Also, in keeping with this new status of the State, all the important positions in the public service are currently held by only the core ethnic communities that supported Jubilee, undermining the seriousness of the message by the President.

Nothing polarises as much as political exclusion. Several parts of the country are now totally out of power. The President could become credible in his stated objective of reconciling the country if he started with the redistribution of core public positions in a manner that reflects the face of Kenya.

Also in everyday political life, the President needs to affirm the truth commission, the implementation of whose report he should promise and prioritise.
