Nations, pretty much like humans, are living organisms, albeit of a political anatomy. That means because they are born, they can also die, and several have done just that – been wiped off the face of the map. One of the mightiest was the Soviet Union. The one-time giant of communism is no more.

Just like people, nations must take advantage of favourable winds to steer them ashore, or perish in the storm. Some, like Singapore, were fortunate to make use of a great leader’s brilliance to escape the vagaries of the Third World.

Others in this league include South Korea, Malaysia, and several Asian tigresses. In Africa, we are yet to see such a breakthrough in spite of our enormous natural and human resources.

In Africa, ours isn’t the curse of fate, or a cruel god. Rather, it’s the myopia of our governing elites and a scandalous international system that keeps us in bondage. But the Asian tigresses, and now China, have escaped the poverty trap laid by the West. William Shakespeare in Julius Caesar, the celebrated play, wrote these words, “The is a tide in the affairs of men, which taken at the flood, leads on to fortune; Omitted, all the voyage of their life is bound in the shallows and in miseries.” This is what I call the inflection point. It happens in the life of almost all of us — as indeed it does, the life of all nations, sometimes more than once.

It's up to the nation, or the human person, to recognise that moment of inflection, of historic opportunity, and seize it. As Shakespeare concludes in the same stanza, “On such a full sea are we now afloat; And we must take the current when it serves, or lose our ventures.” That’s to say, in the English expression, “opportunity seen, opportunity lost.” If we don’t seize our opportunities, then we squander them. For hunters know this – the animal will only be in your crosshairs only once with a perfect shot; a bullseye. You blink once and when you blink again, the animal is gone. Kenya has had a couple of such moments. At independence, the onset of the Kibaki Era, and now.

## Self-laceration

Why do I think that now is another inflection point for Kenya? I know we live in the Age of Discontent. Not just in Kenya, but the world over. Many great powers are imploding right before our eyes. It’s the season of self-cannibalisation by states. Instead of steering clear of the gathering storms, many nations are deliberately flying into them, tempting fate and everything they treasure.

In this sea of chaos and self-laceration, I see opportunities aplenty. Those who seize these opportunities will be leading lights in the new world order. Kenya is perfectly positioned to leap into this future. But only if we seize that opportunity. This is the big question – do we understand the Big Idea when it hits us in the face?

In my view, this is the Big Idea. We have a restless country that is ripe for huge successes. The ingredients for the stew of greatness are all there. We have a youth population that wants – and deserves – greatness. This youth has told us so and even gone to the streets in its innocent iteration to demonstrate their commitment for a greater version of ourselves before goons and other malignant forces intervened to steal their thunder. In my view, this youth movement was a force for good, not ill.

Left to mature on its own without disruption and weaponisation by failed and toxic politicians, it would have yielded great results in a dialogic engagement with the state and society. I believe it’s not too late.

But the youth alone cannot do what’s required. They are a key constituency like several others. That’s why the effort must be dialogic. I am convinced that part of the Big Idea is President William Ruto.

## Performed miserably

I know that some have demonised and demagogued many of his most vital ideas and projects. While that’s part of politics in a democracy, it’s clear that most of the attacks on President Ruto are driven by toxic politicians, who simply want to replace him at State House.

This bunch, in my view, has zero credibility because it’s been there before – for decades – and performed miserably. So, they lack street creds to credibly attack him. The country should give him a chance to carry out his vision.

President Ruto is part of the Big Idea that together with the aspirations of the youth can germinate greatness. He has a brilliant and strategic mind. Even at the height of our fight against him in Azimio, we realised this fact. He is a big thinker and dreamer. He’s determined to leave a great legacy. He’s an extraordinarily hard worker. He, and the youth, are Kenya’s inflection point. Let’s marry the two.
