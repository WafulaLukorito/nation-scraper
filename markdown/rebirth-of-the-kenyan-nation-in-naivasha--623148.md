# Rebirth of the Kenyan nation in Naivasha 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-02-06T15:24:17Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/rebirth-of-the-kenyan-nation-in-naivasha--623148  

---

One fundamental misconception is that the constitution is only a legal document, a piece of paper that is the source of all power within a state. That’s partly true, but the constitution is much more than a legal contract.

In its holistic view, the constitution is also a political and moral document. That’s why it’s possible to have the most elegant constitution and yet have a deeply roguish state. This is why so many states have constitutions, but lack constitutionalism.

Constitutionalism is the “spirit” of the constitution whereas the constitution is the “letter of the law”. A state without constitutionalism, the “esprit de corps” of the constitution, is like a body without a heart.

Could the Naivasha Consensus be the start of the “spiritual” growth of the political class, the renaissance of constitutionalism? Is the political class finally coming of age?

We know that no political society can develop without a political class that internalises the national interest, and works to maximise the public interest. Or is there a less generous reading of what happened in Naivasha?

Is it possible that our elected leaders finally comprehend the solemn duty of leadership as opposed to the narrow and destructive politics of skullduggery? If so, why the change of heart? Does the near civil war of 2008 weigh heavily on them?

Although the jury is still out, I want to think of the Naivasha Consensus as the rebirth of Kenya.

Let’s take Naivasha apart so that we can understand it. Ambitious is the one word that best describes what happened there. There was some greed, too, but I will address that later. It was the dogged determination of the PSC to resolve – by complete consensus – the vexing matters that was most impressive.

It is not lost on me that the two principals – President Mwai Kibaki and Prime Minister Raila Odinga – had freed the PSC wings of ODM and PNU to come to an agreement.

Even so, it was remarkable how quickly the sticky issues that have derailed a new constitution for two decades were resolved in just under two weeks. This may prove that the disagreements of the past were either disingenuous or driven by myopia.

If that is the case, then I think we have reason to hope that a new chapter may be in the offing.

Constitutions are political and moral pacts. If we get a new constitution, we must christen it the Naivasha Constitution in honour of the Naivasha Consensus. It will be in Naivasha where the “political” and “moral” centre of the constitution was forged.

The PSC took the “legal” draft constitution that the Committee of Experts produced and “legitimised” it as a “political” and “moral” document. The CoE, as the legal mechanics, lacked the grant of “sovereign” power to imbue the draft with popular legitimacy.

But the PSC, as an organ of the elected body of the people, could – and did – bequeath the draft with the veneer of popular sovereignty.

At the end of the day, it will be up to Parliament and the national referendum to bestow ultimate legitimacy on the draft constitution.

But not everything in Naivasha was golden. There were some missteps by the PSC that the CoE should – and must – correct.

The most serious was the unwarranted and unsupportable increase of MPs to 349. In a greedy mood, the PSC picked a number out of a hat!

Why would Kenya – with a population of a mere 40 million – need 349 MPs while the United States – with a population of over 300 million – has only 535 legislators?

To put this in context, the US GDP is 15 trillion dollars to Kenya’s 30 billion! I don’t know how the PSC thought that the Kenyan taxpayers – many of whom live on less than a dollar a day – could afford all these fat-cats.

The CoE must drastically trim the number of MPs based on the logic of the quality of representation, and not the quantity of representatives.

The CoE must clear up the matter of devolution. The decision to adopt two tiers – national and 47 counties – is the right one.

The oppressive provincial administration and the much-talked about regional governments must be killed dead. Kenya does not need tribal blocs of votes that will be used by demagogues and charlatans to retard nation building.

The CoE should vividly capture economic, social and cultural rights and lay to rest the incorrect arguments by some in the PSC that such rights are unenforceable.

I hope that the Committee of Experts has the courage to reject the repugnant notion that life begins at conception. This benighted view, just like the assertion that marriage is between a man and a woman, has no place in a modern constitution.

The Naivasha Consensus may very well go down in history as the start of a seismic shift in Kenyan politics.

It may portend the maturation of the political class. Perhaps Kenya can once again become the beacon of hope in the region.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
