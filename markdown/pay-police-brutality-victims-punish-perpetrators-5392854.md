On the surface, it might seem a positive development that the Kenya National Commission on Human Rights (KNCHR) is moving full steam ahead on a task that should lead to compensation for victims of human rights violations, particularly those related to the Gen Z protests of 2024, and the 2017 and 2022 post-election disturbances.

Full disclosure before proceeding: I am an interested party, having been a victim of illegal abduction in July 2024 by officers from the Directorate of Criminal Investigations. The Independent Policing Oversight Authority (Ipoa) has been unable or unwilling to conclude investigations into the crime despite numerous witness statements and the provision of photographs and videos that clearly captured the faces of some of the perpetrators. More on that later.

The compensation and reparations framework under the aegis of the 10-point Agenda signed by [President William Ruto](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/william-samoei-ruto-3809570) and late opposition doyen [Raila Odinga](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/raila-amolo-odinga-3809512) should hopefully lead to healing and closure, but financial payouts alone can never be enough if simply seen as blood money or attempts to evade justice and accountability.

The national human rights watchdog seems to recognise this. It emphasises that a just and effective reparations programme includes restitution (restoration of lost rights and status), rehabilitation (medical and psycho-social support), satisfaction (acknowledgment, apology, memorialisation and truth-telling) and guarantees of non-repetition (institutional reforms to prevent recurrence of State violations).

## Gross human rights abuses

Sounds nice on paper, but it is loudly silent on explicit guarantees on penalties for those who must be held accountable for gross human rights abuses, including murder, abduction, torture, illegal confinement and enforced disappearances.

The proposed programme seems designed to commit taxpayer funds to monetary compensation for the victims, while sparing any penalties for the perpetrators. That is not right. We cannot seek guarantees of non-repetition while leaving scot-free those responsible for horrendous crimes against citizens.

It would seem that under the cover of a compensation proposal, we are being told to leave alone those who ran criminal Deep State operations against presumed foes of the Ruto regime.

KNCHR was handed the onerous responsibility of leading the compensation process after President Ruto’s appointment of a panel of experts dominated by “regime-friendly” individuals was annulled by the courts. It is disturbing, however, that the panel chaired by Dr Ruto’s advisor on constitutional affairs and human rights, Prof Makau Mutua, has been retained to oversee operations once the human rights body presents its findings.

It remains unclear at this stage whether the supposedly independent KNCHR will therefore be subjected to the indignity of its reports and recommendations being subjected to further review by a group beholden to the president.

The role of an intermediary is an issue that KNCHR Chairperson Claris Ogangah and Secretary/CEO Bernard Mogesa must review and come clean on. All the work it does will be in vain if the implementation of recommendations will ultimately be at the mercy of a nebulous bunch of State gatekeepers.

The commission must also review its timetable to deal with the perception that it wants to fast-track submission of compensation claims, while sweeping under the carpet serious issues of justice.

The process cannot be complete without the prosecution and removal from office of all those in the State security networks, as well as their political overlords, who must bear individual culpability for crimes against the people.

## Police excesses

The ongoing process will be an acid test for the KNCHR, which in recent times seems to have become a passive bystander on human rights issues, in contrast to a glorious past under luminaries such as Maina Kiai and Florence Simbiri-Jaoko.

Although it regularly releases statements on various issues under its purview, it seems to do so hesitantly, almost as if afraid of ruffling feathers within the political establishment.

As an independent body funded by taxpayers, the official human rights watchdog must always be at the forefront, fiercely and without fear, defending the rights of all citizens. It must be seen and heard confronting State overreach and, where necessary, leading legal challenges against police excesses, roles it seems to have surrendered to independent civil society lobbies.

Finally, as mentioned earlier, this victim of State-sponsored human rights violations demands an inquiry into the refusal or inability of Ipoa to ensure that criminals within the police and other security organs are made to face the law.

Compensation means nothing where criminals within the security and political establishment are made to believe that they operate above the law, and that they remain free, at the service of the regime, to kill, abduct, torture, harass and generally eliminate real and imagined political foes.
