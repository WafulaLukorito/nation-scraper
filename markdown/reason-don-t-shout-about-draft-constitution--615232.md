# Reason – don’t shout – about draft constitution

## What you need to know:

* We should beware the paid megaphones of the ‘filthy rich’ and ethnic warlords

An empty vessel makes the most noise.” I hope you recall the Kiswahili iteration of that famous proverb. It is stunning that some prominent Kenyans dismissed the draft constitution before it was released by the Committee of Experts.

This chorus of refuseniks has picked up since the release on Tuesday even though they have not actually read the document.

Good people, chill and take time to digest the draft constitution before venturing an opinion. You should not read the constitution the way you do a newspaper. It is a complex and sophisticated document that requires careful study and analysis before one can issue an informed verdict.

Even though the constitution is the people’s document, not every person can understand its internal logic and complicated architecture.

I liken the constitution to an air plane. We can all fly in a plane, but only a few of us can pilot it and even fewer know how it is manufactured. Many of us might have an opinion on the shape of a plane, or how smoothly it flies. But this knowledge is aesthetic, not mechanical or technical.

The analogy is that the Committee of Experts are the technicians, the intelligentsia and the political class are the pilots, and the masses are the passengers.

We all have opinions, but not all the opinions are driven by noble purposes or have the same value.

You might accuse me of elitism, but you would be wrong. I submit that the masses – the passengers – are not the problem. The culprits in this debate are the intelligentsia and the political class.

But the masses can be a problem if they allow themselves to be “confused” and “manipulated” by the intelligentsia and the political class. That is precisely what the pundits have been doing. They are already whipping up hysteria so that the masses can vote against the draft constitution.

This is “irresponsible citizenship” on the part of the political and elite classes. In fact, this is treason against the Kenyan people because it seeks to steal their sovereignty to make an informed judgement.

“Responsible citizenship” – the only citizenship that patriots exhibit – seeks to create a common polity that nurtures the best in us and suppresses the worst in us. There is no better document to chart this destiny than a democratic constitution. This is what acting in the “best interests of the nation” means.

The reverse – acting for personal or tribal agendas – is “irresponsible citizenship”. The problem with Kenya has been its domination by “irresponsible citizens”. I want to urge all the “responsible citizens” to stand up and take back their country from charlatans. If they don’t, the draft constitution will be defeated by demagogues and ethnic warlords.

Don’t get me wrong. I am not saying that we should suppress dissenting views, or not point out flaws with the draft constitution. We should – and must – debate the normative content of the draft constitution robustly. But the key word is “debate”. Debate refers to a reasoned argument that is conducted respectfully.

The “national dialogue” over the draft constitution must be civil and sober. There must be no room to demagogue the document. Let’s ignore those who would rather shout and yell than think and argue their position. The marketplace of ideas is a battlefield, but a civil one. There is no room in it for the morally corrupt or the “filthy” rich – emphasis on “filthy”.

We should beware the paid megaphones of the “filthy rich” and the ethnic warlords. These intellectual pretenders should be marginalised because they are cheap profiteers. They would say anything for a buck. Such “experts” on the constitution are just guns for hire.

Constitutions are not documents for the immediate, or the personal. They should be immortal documents written for the ages. That’s why the glib and dismissive comments by some in the political class ought to be treated with the contempt they deserve.

The masses – the passengers – must insist that the pilots and the architects work in harmony to deliver a basic law that lifts everyone.

You’ve noticed that I have not offered an opinion on the draft constitution. Why? Because I am still reading it! When I am done, I will go into my study and think hard. Only then will I voice an opinion. But my opinions will not be intended to destroy or shoot down the document. Rather I will be offering my views to improve it. After all, the document is a draft!

Out of the debate there should be areas of convergence and “nuggets of wisdom” which should drive revisions. I expect revisions because I know no draft document can be perfect. I am sure the Committee of Experts would agree with me on this.

Finally, let me say that Kenya is a poor country. We have already spent too much money chasing after a constitution. The time for that chase should come to an end with this cycle. That will happen only if we allow reason and common sense to prevail over the ignoble machinations of “irresponsible citizens”.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights Commission.*
