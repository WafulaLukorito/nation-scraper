Last week, comrade Kwamchetsi Makokha wrote a provocative piece, “[Balance Safety, accountability in spy agency](https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/balance-safety-accountability-in-national-intelligence-service-4939110),” (Daily Nation, February 24, 2025). Let me state upfront that I am glad scribes and citizens in general are freely expressing their views about the state as never before. The official slogan of the *Washington Post*, one of America’s most storied papers, is “Democracy Dies in Darkness.” I couldn’t agree more.

I wrote last week in this column that we also live in the “[Age of the Big Lie](https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/we-live-in-the-age-of-the-big-lie-4937150)”. That’s why it’s imperative, especially in emerging democracies such as Kenya’s, to debate public policy with cogent and unimpeachable data, information, and empirical clarity. Mr Makokha got many things right, but was dead wrong on some critical ones.

First, I think it’s unarguable that national security sits at the centre of the survival of the state, and therefore, the nation. It’s not the province, or fiat, of the average citizen to guarantee the security of the state. Citizens are too busy performing the miracle of “five fishes and two loaves.” That unenviable task falls on the broad shoulders of the Kenya’s armed and security services. Key among these agencies is the National Intelligence Service (NIS) under its Director General Noordin Haji. No country can survive in today’s world without a fully-fledged first-class intelligence agency.

Second, it’s true the public purse must be used by the state accountably. It’s after all the people’s money. However, in every democracy, the exact allotments to spy agencies and other secret services is always a closely guarded national secret. There’s nothing nefarious about such secrecy, as long as a closed number of public officials in the legislature and executive are in the know.

It’s these officials who’ve a fiduciary duty to make sure resources aren’t squandered, or used to subvert democracy. Publicly broadcasting such allocations could clearly pose national security risks. It’s the equivalent of showing your mortal enemy the knife in a critical phase of combat. A state can’t blow the bullhorn on the work of the spy agency.

## Opacity

Third, it’s true most intelligence agencies around the globe have historically operated with unjustifiable opacity. But that’s changing with new techniques and technologies that have transformed how intelligence is collected and deployed. A lot of previously hidden information is now available in plain view. So a lot of the cloak and dagger of yesteryear is obsolete.

Many spy agencies, including in the most advanced democracies, are now more accessible to the public. I am aware that Mr Haji has taken unheralded steps to make the NIS more public facing. Citizens need not fear their intelligence agencies. And they won’t if those agencies are more open to the public the way Mr. Haji is doing. They will even attract better talent.

Fourth, the intelligence services are the key to guaranteeing the permanent existence of the state and safeguarding the independence and sovereignty of the republic. By themselves, citizens or other public officials cannot know what Al-Shabaab and other malignant non-state actors, let alone what both friendly and hostile states, are planning against the republic. Nor can any other arm of government do counter-intelligence, or thwart economic espionage and violent threats to the republic, both from within and from without. If NIS isn’t fully well-resourced, and a terror attack takes place, such as occurred at Garissa University a while back, there could be incalculable losses. Then citizens would bay rightly for the blood of the security agencies and their heads.

Fifth, a lot of garbage has been spewed on TV and print and other electronic media about Kenya’s African Union Commission (AUC) campaign in which ODM’s [Raila Odinga](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/raila-amolo-odinga-3809512) was the candidate. We were not unsuccessful for reasons that will be debated for years to come. As a key strategist in the campaign, I can assure everyone we did everything possible to win. [President William Ruto’s](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/william-samoei-ruto-3809570) support was total and unstinting. I’ve seen unwarranted and ignorant attacks on NIS and Mr Haji. Mr Makokha has fallen for this drivel, even questioning why the spy chief was in Addis Ababa. Mr Haji wasn’t the only spy chief there – virtually every country in Africa and the world had their their top intelligence officers present.

## External threats

Finally, the NIS defends against both domestic and external threats. I am sure in addition to the AUC campaign, Mr Haji had other engagements with his counterparts in Addis Ababa. I am saddened and stupefied by unwarranted attacks on Mr Haji, the first NIS Director General of Somali extraction. These bilious attacks, such as those vitriolic, unfounded, and bigoted remarks by then then-Deputy President [Rigathi Gachagua](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/rigathi-gachagua-3815200) in June 2024, are poisonous to our democracy. The office of NIS Director General isn’t the preserve of certain ethnic communities. I think Mr Makokha fell into this trap too, though I hope unwittingly. Mr Haji is a compassionate, thoughtful, erudite man. A patriot par excellence. The unwarranted attacks on him must cease. He’s a full-blooded Kenyan who’s served his country with valour and dedication.
