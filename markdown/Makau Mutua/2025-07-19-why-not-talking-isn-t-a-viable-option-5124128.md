In this column last Sunday, I made a national plea for an [inter-generational conclave](https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/kenyans-we-must-talk-now--5115686) to resolve the fever that has gripped the nation since last year. Kenyans gave their views on social media sites X and Facebook where I posted the piece.

The views ranged from the thoughtful to the inane. Most engaged with an insightful sincerity while others were vituperative. That’s democracy, and I took it in my stride. I noticed one thread that ran through a lot of the comments. Many commenters posited that Kenyans know what ails the country and, therefore, do not need to talk.

All that is needed, they wrote, is to correct the governance of the country and create economic opportunities.

As a prognosis, I can’t argue with them. That is because those two complaints confront every government on our planet. Americans complain about the same thing, even though they live in the wealthiest and one of the most open societies on Earth. This isn’t to pooh pooh their comments. In fact, Kenya has a long way to go.

Even so, the fact of the matter is a lot is happening. What appears to be a big issue is the trust gap between the state and the people so that even when good things happen, well-meaning folks and demagogues can gainsay, or minimise, them. But again, that is all part of the democratic process. People think whatever they wish in a democracy.

## Trust gap

Let me say one more thing about the trust gap. This is fact and fiction. Fiction manufactured by the opponents of the regime and fact sometimes fuelled by the excesses of the state such as the murder of Albert Ojwang in police custody.

The trust gap is resultant from some of our brethren from Mt Kenya who have trouble accepting President William Ruto because they failed to control and instrumentalise him. Many supported him with a view to politically enslaving him. He refused to be their figurehead.

Rigathi Gachagua, the impudent deputy and the chief architect of this Mt Kenya entitlement, was impeached and thrown out of office. He’s trying to foment a revolt against President Ruto.

I want to offer an anecdote about Tanzania and the crisis born out of the demands for multi-partism in the early 1990s. Furious winds of change were blowing throughout the world, and especially Africa.

Single party, military and opaque regimes and dictatorships were swept away by that political renaissance. Kenya’s Daniel arap Moi capitulated and agreed to return the country to democracy. In Tanzania, after a wide and deep national consultation, the majority rejected multiparty democracy. Mwalimu Julius Nyerere, the sage of African politics, stunned the nation when he announced that he would accede to the demands of the minority who wanted open political competition. I believe that Kenyans – the power elite and the people – should do a Mwalimu.

Not talking isn’t an option. In fact, I believe we will fight if we don’t talk. Those who say we shouldn’t talk aren’t telling us how to tackle the impasse. We have seen the devastating impacts of the street demonstrations called by the Gen Z and weaponised by political demagogues and goons.

Lives have been lost and billions of shillings lost in looted and burnt property. Law enforcement infrastructure, including courts, have been razed. Small businesses, the backbone of any economy, have been destroyed. The livelihoods of the most vulnerable have gone up in smoke. Most importantly, precious young lives have been snuffed out. This isn’t sustainable and that is why we must talk to unlock and unfreeze the stalemate.

## ‘Ruto Must Go’

Those who say “Ruto Must Go” simply won’t have their way. Kenya is a democracy and they must await their turn at the ballot in 2027, unless they want to collapse Kenya, as we’ve seen elsewhere in Africa. No one will stand by and let them burn the only country Kenyans call home. I was a refugee for a decade and I know the vagaries of exile.

If we collapse Kenya, do those who are fomenting chaos think they will be welcome with flowers by any of our neighbours in the region? Don’t say I am selling you fear. Been there, and done that, and it’s very, very hard. Take it from me. Talk now, or invite Armageddon.

Kenya is yet to fully recover from the spasms of Covid 19. The borrowing of the last decade has taken a toll on recovery as we spend a lot on debt repayment. The youth bulge is a huge challenge that Kenya must address. We must fully implement the Constitution or revise parts that we believe are either impracticable or unwise. This will help us deal with our governance deficit.

*Makau Mutua is SUNY Distinguished Professor and Margaret W. Wong Professor at Buffalo Law School, The State University of New York. He’s Senior Advisor on Constitutional Affairs to President William Ruto. @makaumutua.*
