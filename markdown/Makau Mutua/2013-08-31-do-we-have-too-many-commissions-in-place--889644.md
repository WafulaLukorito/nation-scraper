# Do we have too many commissions in place?

## What you need to know:

* It was neither a witch-hunt, nor a whitewash. Justice Philip Waki made sure that his commission would strike a blow against impunity when he gave the historic “Envelope” to former UN Secretary- General Kofi Annan.
* Why do we need two bodies to do essentially the same thing? I know the excuse is that the former is independent while the latter isn’t. So what? To make this ridiculous, throw in the Attorney-General and the relevant Cabinet Secretary. That’s right – these two are also involved in the implementation of the Constitution.

When there is a public furore, and the state doesn’t want to do anything, it forms a commission to study the matter. This is the most cynical use of the device of the commission — to hoodwink the gullible public.

Another tactic is to use a commission to kick the can down the road. Perhaps the matter at hand is too hot a potato, and the state wants to buy time so that tempers can cool. May be the problem is really insoluble, and the only way out is to “put a commission in motion” to appear to be doing something. That’s called governing. It’s often about lying to citizens. Which begs the question – does Kenya have too many useless commissions?

You may call me cynical. But how many commissions – except the Commission of Inquiry on Post-Election Violence – has ever amounted to anything? The so-called Waki Commission is arguably the first, and only, commission to give us something concrete.

It was neither a witch-hunt, nor a whitewash. Justice Philip Waki made sure that his commission would strike a blow against impunity when he gave the historic “Envelope” to former UN Secretary- General Kofi Annan.

Justice Waki’s report and the “Envelope” led to the investigations by the International Criminal Court. Now the Sword of Damocles dangles over the heads of the Bensouda Three. I bet you can’t name another commission that’s been as effective. That’s because there isn’t.

Most commissions have been a black hole for taxpayer funds. A few come to mind. Perhaps the most infamous is the Commission of Inquiry into the Goldenberg Affair.

After fleecing Kenyans of billions, the Bosire Commission came up with a mealy-mouthed report and the matter died on the vine.

Recently, we were treated to a charade of a truth commission. The Truth, Justice and Reconciliation commission was yet another exercise to empty our pockets. The TJRC lost its credibility because Ambassador Bethuel Kiplagat, its chair, would not resign over allegations of wrongdoing.

However, Vice-Chair Betty Murungi quit, and left the TJRC to list in rough seas. Take my word for it – its report will probably never amount to anything.

I had hoped constitutional commissions in the 2010 Constitution would prove to be different. It’s early days, but I am already disappointed by most of them.

They have either gone into hiding – like a witness protection programme – or they are grandstanding.

Most commissioners have no clue what a constitutional commission is supposed to do. They’ve no idea that it’s a tool of “democratic governance,” not a witch-hunt.

But I am afraid the commissioners are making terrible masters, and not the servants they were designed to be.

Not to say that they see the commissions as personal goldmines. Some are run like insane asylums. Others are comatose. But the worst of all are those packed with political hacks and tribal incompetents.

I am concerned about the duplicative and zillion commissions. For one, I am not sure we need the Commission on the Implementation of the Constitution in addition to the parliamentary Constitutional Implementation Oversight Committee.

Why do we need two bodies to do essentially the same thing? I know the excuse is that the former is independent while the latter isn’t. So what? To make this ridiculous, throw in the Attorney-General and the relevant Cabinet Secretary. That’s right – these two are also involved in the implementation of the Constitution.

Talk about too many cooks spoiling the broth. Mind you that none of these bodies and offices have a final say on constitutional matters if there’s conflict.

Only the High Court, and finally the Supreme Court, can rescue us from problems of the implementation and interpretation of the Constitution.

That’s why I think the CIC must be redefined, and given unambiguous primary over the AG and the Executive. Chair Charles Nyachae and the CIC can – as mandated – weigh in on implementation. But neither he, nor the CIC, can give a legally binding opinion or interpretation of the Constitution. That should change – give the CIC some teeth, and make it subject only to the courts. Otherwise, let’s not call a cat a tiger.

This is one more issue that must be put on the table as we discuss possible constitutional referenda. We can also scrap some commissions.

For decades now, public commissions and task forces have been a gravy train for the elite, especially those related to, and connected, to the political class. Someone should add up the total bill that all the commissions and task forces put together have cost Kenya since independence.

I bet the amount will be staggering, as will the absence of any tangible results. Instead, wages paid out to commissioners have only gone to enrich them. That’s one reason elites fight and lobby so hard to be appointed to commissions. Every one of them thinks it’s their “turn to eat”. This is where the tribe comes in handy as a lobbying tool. Every tribal baron must reward cronies or be damned.

I worry about the cost of government under the new Constitution. County governments alone will be a huge drain on taxpayers’ cash. All these new institutions and officials will have to be paid – most handsomely. Can Kenyans afford it? I ask because the cost of government everywhere in the world is under scrutiny.

That’s because taxpayer money should go to citizens and social services – and not fat-cat bureaucrats.

That’s why we must rethink the trigger-happy approach to commissions and the cost of government bureaucracies. Democracy is expensive, but it need not be impoverishing.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at SUNY Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC. @makaumutua.*
