# Activists challenge Ruto’s protest victims compensation panel 

**Author:** Joseph Wangui  
**Date:** 2025-08-28T09:34:22Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/news/activists-challenge-ruto-s-protest-victims-compensation-panel--5170396

By 
[Joseph Wangui](/kenya/joseph-wangui-395672)

Correspondent

Nation Media Group

Four activists have petitioned the High Court to invalidate President William Ruto’s decision to form a panel of experts to oversee the compensation of victims of demonstrations and public protests.

The petitioners allege unlawful exercise of presidential powers, arguing that the Head of State lacks the legal authority to establish such a panel.

They also question the criteria used to select members of the team.

Further, they claim the panel duplicates the mandates of existing State institutions such as the Kenya National Commission on Human Rights (KNCHR), the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) and the Inspector-General of Police (IG).

“Considering that the executive propagated the injury of its citizens, it beats logic for the same executive to purport to compensate the said citizens, yet there are institutions like courts that handle compensation in situations of human rights violations,” the petitioners argue.

The 18-member Compensation and Reparation Panel, chaired by President Ruto’s constitutional advisor Prof Makau Mutua, was established through a gazette notice dated August 25.

Its mandate includes designing a framework to verify, categorise and compensate eligible victims, engaging stakeholders to ensure fairness and authenticating data on victims.

The panel is also tasked with proposing legislative and institutional reforms related to protests and policing, before submitting its report to the President.

The petitioners Dr Magare Gikenyi, Eliud Karanja Matindi, Philemon Abuga and Dishon Keroti—maintain that the President’s decision is unconstitutional.

They say the move amounts to usurpation of the constitutional powers of independent bodies such as KNCHR, the ODPP, IG, the Independent Police Oversight Authority (IPOA), the Victims Protection Agency (VPA) and the Judiciary.

Since the panel has been empowered to summon individuals and entities to provide information or documents, the activists insist this would be unconstitutional.

They argue such authority is reserved for courts, independent commissions and Parliament.

“Purporting to bestow these powers to an ad hoc illegal panel is contrary to Article 94(5) of the Constitution as read with Chapter 10 on the Judiciary. Only Parliament has powers to make provisions having the force of law in Kenya,” the petition states.

Article 94(5) expressly provides that “no person or body, other than Parliament, has the power to make provision having the force of law in Kenya except under authority conferred by this Constitution or legislation.”

“The President of the Republic is not the Parliament of Kenya. He cannot therefore usurp legislative powers donated by the people to their Members of Parliament,” the activists add.

The petitioners also allege improper use of public funds in financing the task force.

Since the team was not included in the government’s budget cycle, they argue its expenses will require reallocation of funds without parliamentary approval, contrary to constitutional provisions on public participation and appropriation of money.

They further raise concerns about the lack of transparency regarding members’ remuneration.

“Other than the President, nobody knows the allowances and remuneration of the team members, contrary to Articles 10, 201 and 232(1)(e) of the Constitution on transparency, accountability and good governance,” the petition reads.

Another contention is that the panel has been granted powers to access personal data, including that of victims and their families, from State agencies and public offices.

The petitioners argue this violates Article 31(3) of the Constitution and the Data Protection Act, as well as the independence of constitutional commissions under Article 249.

“This blanket power is unconstitutional. The President of Kenya cannot lawfully direct State organs on how to carry out their functions,” they argue.

The activists also challenge the membership of the panel, claiming members were appointed without transparency, merit or competitive selection.

“Other than the president nobody knows the allowances and remuneration of the team members contrary to principles of transparency, accountability and good governance, article 10, 201, and 232(1)(e) of the constitution,” they contend.

The petitioners have applied for interim orders suspending the President’s decision pending the case’s determination. They want conservatory orders stopping the panel from compiling reports, making recommendations, or taking any action.

The application is awaiting hearing directions.

The respondents including the Attorney-General, the panel nd its chairperson Prof Mutua are yet to file their responses.
