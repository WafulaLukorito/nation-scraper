# The three possible scenarios from the ICC judges 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2011-12-17T15:22:33Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/the-three-possible-scenarios-from-the-icc-judges--793350  

---

It won’t be long before the International Criminal Court (ICC) brings the hammer down on Kenya. Doomsday isn’t far off. On that fateful day, the skies will open, and fates will be sealed. One thing is for sure — we will cross the Rubicon. There will be a “before” and an “after”. Dark and ominous clouds will gather for some, and a soothing light will appear for others.

For victims of post-election mayhem, justice or heartbreak will be nigh. The Ocampo Six will either have comeuppance or vindication. Either way, nothing will ever be the same. Logic tells me doomsday will unleash three scenarios — all the Ocampo Six go scot free; all go on trial; or only some go on trial.

Let’s take one at a time. The first maximalist scenario is where all the Ocampo Six go scot free. Finance minister Uhuru Kenyatta, Eldoret North MP William Ruto, Head of Civil Service Francis Muthaura, Tinderet MP Henry Kosgey, former Police Commissioner Hussein Ali, and journalist Joshua arap Sang don’t believe they “will walk”.

But it’s possible — though not probable — that the ICC will refuse to confirm their charges against humanity. The judges may decide that Prosecutor Luis Moreno-Ocampo didn’t discharge his burden of proof.

Remember, Mr Moreno-Ocampo has to show — as required by Article 61 of the Rome Statute — that “substantial grounds” exist for charges to be confirmed. It’s possible Mr Moreno-Ocampo failed to do so.

How’s this possible? Remember the ICC has evidentiary standards for every stage of the criminal process — summons, confirmation, and trial. Those standards ratchet up as the process goes deeper towards culpability or vindication.

The prosecutor’s burden gets heavier and more complicated. Which begs the question — did Mr Moreno-Ocampo let all the air out of his case at the confirmation hearings? Did he present sufficient evidence — and raise enough questions — to convince the judges that all of the Ocampo Six must be put on trial?

Does the theory of his two cases — against ODM (Mr Ruto, Mr Kosgey and Mr Sang) and PNU (Mr Kenyatta, Mr Muthaura and Mr Ali) — hang together? If not, all six walk to freedom.

The second maximalist scenario is where all the Ocampo Six are bundled off for a full blown trial. In this scenario, Mr Moreno-Ocampo would be deemed to have easily met his burden of proof by establishing the existence of “substantial grounds” to try the Ocampo Six.

He would have revealed sufficient evidence to convince the judges that the Ocampo Six had “motive, opportunity and intent” to warrant a full trial. He would have had to show that Mr Kenyatta, Mr Muthaura and Mr Ali were connected in a plan to carry out the crimes alleged.

Likewise, Mr Ruto, Mr Kosgey and Mr Sang must be tied together in a nefarious plot to commit crimes against humanity. But remember that “confirmation” isn’t conviction. At this stage, the Ocampo Six are still innocent until proved guilty.

However, the confirmation of charges tightens the noose around their necks. At this point, the suspects are looking at a narrowing window of freedom. Faces grow long and anxiety rises. Blood pressures go up.

For victims, this is the most promising outcome. Never before have they come this close to “justice.” In contrast, the first scenario— where all go scot free — would be a crushing blow to victims.

The second scenario, even before a conviction, would be the biggest blow to impunity in the history of Kenya.

**Substantial grounds**

The third, and last, scenario would have some of the Ocampo Six go to trial, while others go scot free. Here, the judges would figure that either the prosecutor didn’t provide “substantial grounds” for some, or that several of the suspects weren’t essential to the case.

I have a strong feeling that Mr Sang — whether innocent or guilty — may not be essential to the case against Mr Ruto and Mr Kosgey. The judges may also think that he doesn’t bear the “greatest responsibility” for the mayhem.

For PNU, I have a very strong feeling that the case against Mr Kenyatta, Mr Muthaura and Mr Ali cannot stand if either one or two of them are severed from it.

Where does this leave us on doomsday? That there will both be celebration and heartbreak on that day isn’t in doubt. Victims will ululate in every village and hamlet if several, or all, of the Ocampo Six are ordered to stand trial.

But they will be crestfallen if the suspects go scot free. Justice will have been denied once again, as usual in Kenya. The perception will be that “little men” cannot get justice against Big Men.

Then it will be left to Chief Justice Willy Mutunga to see what the new-look judiciary can do to bring justice to the thousands who were killed, raped, maimed. But, as the saying goes, can one fingernail kill a flea by itself?

They say that you better kill the king if you shoot at him. Or you are dead. My view is that the ICC will have done immeasurable damage to Kenya if it doesn’t confirm the charges against the Ocampo Six. Why?

That’s because the Ocampo Six and their supporters will exhibit a belligerence — and elephantine hubris — heretofore unseen. Mind you I am not saying charges must be confirmed if the evidentiary standard hasn’t been met.

My point simply is that the ICC would have had no business issuing summons in the first place without confidence that charges would be confirmed.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC*
