## What you need to know:

* Both the Government Chemist and the Kenya Bureau of Standards have proved that the said soft drink lacks alcohol.
* The soft drink investigation risks destroying that brand, some consumers will begin to associate it with alcohol.
* Today, activists are becoming abstracted from reality, they seem to forget that there are more important things than fighting the Establishment

The typical image of mother-in-law is that of frumpy meddlers in windscreen glasses getting down on arthritic knees to check for dust under the bed and sending the breakfast tea to the Government Chemist to confirm that it is drinkable.

According to sources who requested anonymity, they are irrational, quick to criticise, see nothing right in their virtuous daughters-in-law and nothing wrong with their useless, drunkard sons.

We have, I fear, a new, super mother-in-law in town. It is Parliament. A nominated MP went to the House to complain that a popular soft drink is actually alcoholic and demanded a ministerial statement.

The Industrialisation minister, a university-trained chemist, no less, went to great lengths to explain that the soft drink had no alcohol whatsoever, and both the Government Chemist and the Kenya Bureau of Standards have proved as much.

But the MP, if I recall well, wanted to provide evidence of her own. I was puzzled when the Speaker ordered a parliamentary committee to investigate the matter and report back.

This committee, having no competence in chemical matters, will have to rely on the same Government Chemist and KBS that the minister did. So what’s the point?

Now, as far as I am concerned, Parliament can form a committee to investigate smelly socks, or the population of cockroaches in Muthaiga kitchens, or indeed the availability, distribution and affordability of large nappies for fat, incontinent old men. Parliament can do that — probably should do that — to keep itself employed.

My concern is that these humorous activities are not totally benign. The soft drink investigation risks destroying that brand, some consumers will begin to associate it with alcohol, which defeats its very purpose of being non-alcoholic.

How will Parliament compensate the manufacturer when it is finally proved that the drink is as non-alcoholic as claimed?

The justice dispensed by MPs appears to me to be nothing but a political charade. MPs come down like a ton of bricks on unpopular ministers against whom there is no smoking-gun evidence, but let off the hook crooks whose activities could be dangerous to the interests of the Republic.

Even the very notion of parliamentary independence is a con. Nearly half the MPs — ministers and their assistants — work for the Executive!

It calls to mind a mother-in-law shuffling through the apartment, her thick woollen socks pulled up to her armpits, her stuffy sinuses making a racket, making an ass of herself by trying too hard to be helpful.

(Disclaimer: It is not mathematically impossible to find a sexy, sophisticated mother-in-law who smokes, knocks back Jack Daniels and couldn’t be bothered to meddle. There, how is that for fairness?)

When I expressed my views a couple of months ago about a report by the Kenyan National Commission on Human Rights which I found quite objectionable, I incurred the wrath of certain parties in the human rights movement.

Subsequently, I had the very good fortune of sitting down with the gurus of activism, among them Prof Makau Mutua, the Dean of Buffalo Law School, State University of New York and chairman of Kenya Human Rights Commission and Dr Davidar Lamba, a very sincere and public-spirited individual who walks around with a whistle hanging from his neck.

I took the opportunity to voice a matter that is increasingly bothering me. In the past, I used to know that I could trust the folks in the human rights movement, they were with us, they led us in the fight against the Kanu dictatorship.

Today, activists are becoming abstracted from reality, they seem to forget that there are more important things than fighting the Establishment.

And I put my case in the simple, common sense manner of the peasants among whom I was raised: Whose rights must I, as a journalist, stand for first, the rights of our children who are in danger of being blown to smithereens by terrorists or those of the alleged terrorist?

If a person from a neighbouring country is accused of associating with terrorists and the authorities have requested that individual to please return to his country because they do not consider his presence to be in the interest of Kenyans, should I stand with the Kenyans in whose interest the authorities say they are acting or should I stand shoulder-to-shoulder with the alleged terrorist?

I have heard the complaint in some countries that the authorities seem to care more for the rights of the offenders than those of the victims of crime.

I have certainly heard more noise about the military whipping villagers in Mt Elgon than I have about the poor people who have had their tongues and ears chopped off by the Sabaot Land Defence Force, or those who have had to live under virtual slavery, daily victims of extortion, intimidation and quite often harm.

On Friday, the KNCHR released its report on the post-election violence and went so far as to name individuals it said organised and or financed the chaos.

Kenyans expected an allegedly professional body such as this one to explain what happened in Eldoret, Naivasha, Nakuru, Koibatek and so on.

Looking at the names named and shamed, it occurs to me that Kenyans may have something to say about the completeness of this report.

Others may question the motives of the commission, its fairness and impartiality.

I encourage Kenyans to read the report and make up their own minds. But I get the sense that if the commission continues in this vein, it will be dead and without credibility by the end of the year.

As we say where I come from: I have spoken, now you can kill me.
