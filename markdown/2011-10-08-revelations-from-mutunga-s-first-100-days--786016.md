# Revelations from Mutunga’s first 100 days 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2011-10-08T09:06:26Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/revelations-from-mutunga-s-first-100-days--786016  

---

A central maxim of the law is that justice must not only be done, but must be seen to be done.

Form matters as much as substance. That’s why I am flummoxed by critics of Chief Justice Willy Mutunga. They charge — without interrogating themselves — that Dr Mutunga is only concerned with form.

First, that’s completely untrue. Second, it wouldn’t be an indictment even if it were true. The reason is that in law, form and substance are different sides of the same coin.

One is impossible without the other. This is the chicken and egg quandary. You can’t tell which one came first. I will tell you why the CJ scores highly in his first 100 days in office.

First, the most successful revolutions start in the head. You can’t do it unless you can conceptualise it. Strategic revolutionaries shape public opinion and prepare the political landscape for change.

The context will determine whether a situation requires “shock therapy” or “gradualist reforms”. Maybe a clever combination of the two is necessary.

Methinks that Dr Mutunga is proceeding on case-by-case basis. This is as it should be because a “revolutionary moment” doesn’t exist in Kenya.

Kenya today is akin to post-Apartheid South Africa. “Reformers” have to share power with “reactionaries”.

That’s why reformers must “out-think” reactionaries. I’d say CJ Mutunga wrote the book on this strategy. He must make fundamental changes, but bring the elite along.

Even more importantly, the CJ must have the hoi polloi on his side. Why? That’s because they’ve been “recipients of injustice” who must become “consumers of justice”.

The legitimacy of Dr Mutunga’s tenure — and the Judiciary as a whole — hinges on whether the hoi polloi believe him.

It’s true the CJ needs to bring the elite along, but he absolutely must have Wanjiku with him. The reason is simple — the country actually belongs to the latter, not the former.

The elite may rule, but the common man is the foundation of the State. That’s why Dr Mutunga must psychologically prepare the peasant, the market woman, the labourer, and the hawker for the changes in the Judiciary.

I think Dr Mutunga has done a masterful job of setting the table for the masses. He first “defrocked” the “priests of injustice”.

You know what I mean — he disrobed the judges and took away their scary wigs. I can’t overemphasise the importance of this move.

**Professional dress**

The “judicial priesthood” thrived on the fear of the masses. Part of that fear was orchestrated by “oppressive regalia” in the name of “professional dress”.

Tell it to the birds. Not even the justices of the United States Supreme Court dress in such comically unfashionable dress.

Kenyan judicial regalia would make great Halloween costumes in the US. Remember Halloween is a satanic ritual.

Changes in judicial dress level the playing field, and make the courts a less threatening environment where the “little people” feel they can get justice.

Dr Mutunga has also changed salutations in court. Judges shouldn’t be addressed as though they were gods. This emasculates everyone else and belittles those who have come to seek justice.

Salutations ought to be respectful, but not “feudal” and calculated to demean others. Titles like “your lordship” or “my lord” conjure the image of a supplicant before God.

It elevates the judge into a deity and smacks of sacrilege. Judges and lawyers are professionals who shouldn’t assume godly airs. This is truly ironic given the history of incompetence and corruption in the Kenyan Judiciary.

Second, CJ Mutunga has undertaken substantive reforms since taking office. He’s moved to restore ethics in the Judiciary.

He’s rationalised judicial postings and transfers to stave off rigidity, laziness, and the temptations of corruption. Judges are being grouped in bench clusters where their strengths can be leveraged, and their weaknesses minimised.

The CJ is in the process of breathing life into the judicial institute to foster a “thinking Judiciary”.

This will introduce judges to emerging legal trends and jurisprudence in other jurisdictions. This should help make judges truly “learned”.

Gender parity, access to courts in “forgotten” counties, and the rights of the marginalised are taking centre stage. The CJ won’t hesitate to “retire” judges stuck in the past.

Third, the independence of the courts is a key priority of CJ Mutunga. I am sure that the Executive will dare not encroach on judicial independence during Dr Mutunga’s reign.

Any judges “taking orders from above” will have a short career. This means that Director of Public Prosecutions Keriako Tobiko, who was installed in office under a cloud, had better earn the trust of the new Judiciary and the Kenyan people.

Criminal investigations and prosecutions of the high and mighty can’t be perfunctory or fake. The days of envelopes stuffed with cash are over.

The saying “why hire a lawyer when you can buy a judge” should belong in the dustbin of history. “Torture” under the Judiciary should be over.

**Court buildings**

Finally, CJ Mutunga is keen on improving infrastructure and technology for the courts. Court buildings are in shambles, especially outside the major cities. In some cases, they are simply non-existent.

CJ Mutunga will oversee new major construction and upgrades of existing buildings. The temples of justice must look and feel like foundations of justice.

Technology in the courts for law reporting, administrative paperwork, and archiving of judicial opinions must utilise the most modern technology. I have no doubt this is a priority of the CJ.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC*
