The sovereignty of any state, or nation, rests on its population. But the vision and ambition of the state lie with the elite. No state can go far without an ambitious and truly learned elite. Intelligent elites make smart societies possible. I am not defining “intelligent” or “smart” as unidimensional.

I am talking about a complex matrix of brain power, emotional intelligence, patriotism, global sophistication, and ambition. History is clear about how states and nations become great. Elite leadership is the most critical key.

Fragile states lack sound leadership. Of course, there are other variables that contribute to fragility, but the calibre of the elite is the genius of greatness. States fumble in darkness until their elites discover the magic key.

Today, I focus on the proclivity for elites to fragment into thoughtless and small-minded cabals that spend all their time in internecine combat. They spend themselves fighting over nothing. For a fight for power without lofty ambitions for the state and the nation is a fight over nothing. It’s, in fact, a fight towards oblivion within the community of nations.

How can you be engaged in bilious mortal combat against each other while your neighbours plan and execute serious national projects? Democracy is not an invitation to engage in stupid fights. Elites should be different from maggots. If this sounds familiar to Kenyans, it’s because it is. Let me peel your eyes so that we can engage in an insightful conversation.

## Key national interests

The first, and most important, index of a smart elite is a clear understanding of key national interests. Smart states forge a high level of consensus on what constitutes national interests, even if there are contestable paths to achieving them. In the modern era, one clear national interest is a commitment to a democratic polity and the rule of law.

That basic foundation of the state must be unarguable. We can debate the specifics, but the core commitments should be beyond argument. These national interests shape and determine the core character of the state.

For example, they will determine the nature of education, the policies that shape the development of the youth, and the variables that go into infrastructure, agriculture, trade, and manufacturing.

The second important constant is the matter of national security. It is important to understand that the state is a creature of violence. It’s protected and perpetuated by violence, or the threat of violence from enemies, domestic and foreign. That’s why states are invested with the monopoly on the instruments of violence.

A state that doesn’t have the monopoly of violence cannot protect itself and its people. The absence of an effective state—one that cannot police its society or defend its borders—is a Hobbesian nightmare. Such a state cannot assure democracy or the rule of law. An effective state must have functional, predictable, and fair norms and institutions of governance. Citizens must be protected from one another.

We must understand that the security of the state is not a dirty word. It’s the foundation of our peace and democratic institutions. In turn, the coercive apparatus of the state and its security organs must use the power given to them by the Constitution and the people fairly and justly, without arbitrariness or the abuse of power.

In a functional democracy, the relationship between the people and the coercive apparatus of the state should not be confrontational but cooperative. We must understand that without these institutions, no one’s security or freedom can be guaranteed. That’s why Raila Odinga used to repeatedly say we must first of all have a functional state before we can even argue among ourselves.

## Thirty pieces of silver

The third index is the germination of a smart elite—an elite that isn’t hopelessly fragmented within itself. Think of a state as an airplane. Using this metaphor, think of a plane where the pilots and crew are at constant loggerheads. A crash would be inevitable.

Such an elite cannot even keep state secrets. For there are some secrets that must be held in the highest and strictest confidence by elites in power, and when they exit government. A fragmented elite will sell those secrets for thirty pieces of silver. A fragmented elite can’t even agree on what its minimum national interests ought to be, much less protect them. Elites must never compete in a manner that devours the state in a bankrupt and empty quest for power.

Finally, the purpose of governance is to create a state of empathy, not antipathy. A state that cares for all its people. No person is expendable. A state that’s committed to its perpetual and permanent existence on behalf of its people. We have seen elsewhere what elite fragmentation has done to their people.

Our neighbourhood is a textbook example. The state doesn’t sleep. It’s there 24/7 so that its people can sleep peacefully. That’s why trust between the state and the people is paramount. This is only possible if elites understand the mortal dangers of their fragmentation.
