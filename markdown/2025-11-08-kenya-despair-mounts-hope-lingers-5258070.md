In the person of [Raila Odinga](https://nation.africa/kenya/people/raila-amolo-odinga-3809512), Kenya lost its most towering political figure. The centre of gravity of Kenyan politics is gone. Forever. The country’s challenge isn’t to look for, or manufacture, another Raila. That’s simply impossible.

Nor is it to look for someone who can fill his shoes. That’s also a negative. I am sure we have Kenyans who, with time, can become even greater than the man I called my political soul-mate.

That sounds like blasphemy, or heresy, coming from me. But the dream and ambition of every great country is to produce leaders who are more iconic and commanding than those of yore. My fear, however, is our political landscape is dotted with many moral dwarfs.

We are in a season of both despair and hope. Despair that political dwarfs will collapse our democratic experiment. Hope that the greatness of the Kenyan people will reject the naysayers and reach for the stars.

That is what Mr Odinga would have wanted – for those among us, and those who come after him, to imagine the purity and beauty of a pan-Africanist nirvana. I spent many a season discussing and debating – and learning from – Mr Odinga about the limitless possibilities of the African mind. Mr Odinga believed that greatness doesn’t just happen. That it must be deliberately cultivated and forged. That was in a sentence his life’s work. He spent a lifetime in a personal jihad with self.

## Self-purification

It was self-purification without losing the ability to laugh at oneself, or to ruthlessly critique himself. This ambition extended even to his gym workouts. He would easily put many younger men to shame. I remember one time in a sauna, he endured an excruciating 20 minutes. I barely managed 10. That was self-denial and a capacity to endure pain that I thought unique. He was kind to others, even those who were unkindest to him. He forgave easily and trusted effortlessly. Gifted with a sharp memory, Mr Odinga never forgot a soul he ever met. You would hear him in easy mirth with a long lost friend. His, in my view, was a life lived in virtue. Giving, always giving.

I reveal these facts not to sanctify Mr Odinga, or pretend he was not a mortal. No – it’s to say that warts and all, Agwambo strove to be a good person. In my view, he succeeded spectacularly. He was to me the prototype of a real democrat. He never saw the world as simply black and white, although on core principles he did.

He knew life is not lived in poetry but in prose – in praxis. That’s why as we reflect upon the yawning void he has left, we must also mourn the death of decency, ethics, and basic morality in public life. This is our despair. Our moral cliffs keep widening by the day.

One example is sadly poignant. A governor of a county celebrated the demise of Mr Odinga. He said publicly that God had answered the prayers of his people and “removed” Mr Odinga from this life. That Mr Odinga was the cause of the suffering of his people and their penurious economic woes. That Mr Odinga had conspired with President William Ruto to take “all development” to Nyanza and the Luo people.

## Disgusting remarks

The nation gasped as one at such callousness, hate, and bigotry. Shockingly, those in the so-called opposition did not personally and individually condemn and denounce such disgusting remarks. There is a huge hole in souls of our leaders. Lately, I’ve seen some of them trooping to Mr Odinga’s gravesite to shed crocodile tears.

Let me say here and now that all is not lost. We are also living in a season of hope. It is a time for introspection and re-consecration of our national project. It is fashionable to lampoon and attack President Ruto in some quarters.

That’s democracy. But we only have one president at a time. I have listened to President Ruto carefully both in public and private. I’ve always said he’s a strategic thinker even at the height our political disagreements. He’s a big dreamer for Kenya. I repeat what I wrote recently – no Kenyan president has ever dreamt as big. In my view, this is one point of hope. This is partly why Mr Odinga joined him to craft the broad-based government.

Finally, I am watching carefully the diction of leaders in ODM. None of them can pretend not to know what Baba wanted of them in his absence. Nor should they twist his words to suit their short term politics. I urge them to choose their words carefully. Their role in the country’s future is weighty.

There’s no need for them to outcompete each other in daily head-spinning utterances. One hope is that they will act like statesmen and stateswomen. Let’s steer the ship Baba left us safely ashore.
