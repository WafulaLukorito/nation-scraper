# ICC not meant for ‘small fish’ like Joshua Sang 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2011-11-19T13:16:43Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/icc-not-meant-for-small-fish-like-joshua-sang--790712  

---

I have a strong feeling that journalist Joshua arap Sang – one of the Ocampo Six – may go scot free.

The International Criminal Court doesn’t generally bother with “small potatoes”, of which Mr Sang is a type.

The mandate of the court is to try those “bearing the greatest responsibility” for the “most serious crimes”.

I don’t know what the court will decide, but I’ll be damned if it decides to confirm charges against Mr Sang. I base my opinion on legal training and interrogated intuition.

The court is only interested in the “biggest fish” at “the centre” of the allegations for crimes against humanity. Mr Sang is neither a “big fish” nor “central” to the cases against the Ocampo Six.

First, the ICC is a “complementary” court to national judiciaries. It’s only activated when states are unable, or unwilling, to prosecute heinous offenders.

Even when it swings into action, the ICC can only address the most prominent and central criminal suspects. That’s for reasons of practicality and respect for national sovereignty.

It’s impracticable and pointless for the court to “fry every small fish”. That’s the job of national courts – to mop up less important “enablers” and “foot soldiers”.

We don’t know whether Mr Sang is culpable, but even if he was, he would be a “petty” participant. He’s undeserving of a full-blown prosecution by the ICC.

That’s why I predict the Ocampo Six will turn into the Ocampo Five. Secondly, the ICC is interested in “taking out” suspects with the potential to wreck countries by virtue of their prominence, resources, guile, mass following, and ability to inflict damage.

That’s why the ICC went after Muammar Gaddafi (now late) of Libya, President Omar al-Bashir of Sudan, and warlord Jean-Pierre Bemba of the Congo.

**Exercise control**

A review shows that the ICC hasn’t to date confirmed charges against a suspect of Mr Sang’s calibre.

No one can gainsay the fact that of the Ocampo Six, Mr Sang is the least likely to mobilise resources or exercise control over others to wreak havoc.

The man is simply marginal in the scheme of things. That’s not to declare him either innocent or guilty.

Thirdly, although Mr Sang may fit in the prosecution’s overall theory of the charges, he’s no more important than other “bit players” who haven’t been summoned.

By this I mean that the success of Prosecutor Luis Moreno-Ocampo’s case won’t hinge on Mr Sang. He adds nothing to Mr Moreno-Ocampo’s central thesis and theory of the case.

In fact, Mr Moreno-Ocampo can use Mr Sang’s words against the Ocampo Six without him.

If, as Mr Moreno-Ocampo charges, Mr Sang whipped up villagers through his radio station, then presumably he can enter that audio into evidence.

He doesn’t need Mr Sang in the dock to adduce such audio evidence. This is how he can use Mr Sang against the other suspects.

Fourthly, Mr Moreno-Ocampo may want to ask the court not to confirm charges against Mr Sang in exchange for “turning” against his co-accused.

He could enter into a plea bargain to win his freedom provided he volunteers evidence against the Ocampo Six. Of course the assumption here is that Mr Moreno-Ocampo has the goods on Mr Sang.

This approach reduces the prosecution’s and the court’s workload. It is a time-tested legal manoeuvre that would confine the court to its most serious suspects and conserve its resources.

But it would not absolve Mr Sang from culpability before Kenyan courts, which could go after him at a future date.

Fifthly, it would make the ICC look high-minded and interested in usurping national jurisdictions for insignificant suspects.

For Kenya, this would send a signal that the ICC hasn’t lost all faith in domestic courts. This is especially critical with the reforms under way since Chief Justice Willy Mutunga took charge.

I would think both the CJ and DPP Keriako Tobiko would want to revisit the investigation and prosecution of “smaller fish” in local courts for the 2008 mayhem.

This is not a passing comment particularly because virtually no victim of the mayhem has got justice from Kenyan courts.

The overwhelming majority of victims will only be able to get justice locally. I’d be shocked Mr Moreno-Ocampo hasn’t thought of this.

While I seek reprieve from the court’s claws for Mr Sang, I would not do so for the Ocampo Five.

The Ocampo Five – Finance minister Uhuru Kenyatta, Eldoret North MP William Ruto, Tinderet MP Henry Kosgey, head of civil service Francis Muthaura, and former police chief Hussein Ali – properly belong in the court’s front row.

They both occupied – and still do – senior positions in society. They are men of great sway, resources and influence.

**Very wealthy**

Mr Muthaura sits at the head of the state system. Mr Kenyatta is one of the 10 most senior politicos, and a leading contender for State House.

Mr Ruto commands a massive following in his native Rift Valley. Mr Kosgey is reputed to be very wealthy.

The profiles of the Ocampo Five are the reason for the existence of the ICC. It’s difficult for a kleptocratic legal and political system – like Kenya’s – to hold such mandarins accountable.

It’s the impunity of Big Men who commit unspeakable crimes – and the inability or unwillingness of governments to touch them – that drove states to create the ICC.

Mr Sang and his ilk were not contemplated by the Rome Statute. Mr Moreno-Ocampo should ask the court to drop charges against Mr Sang in exchange for evidence against the Ocampo Five.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
