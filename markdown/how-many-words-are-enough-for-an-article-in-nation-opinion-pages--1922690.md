## What you need to know:

* Opinion pieces are normally around 700 words long.
* In precis writing, you’re supposed to express your thoughts clearly, concisely, and effectively while eliminating unnecessary words, repetitions and details.

I normally don’t do this because it’s not part of my remit. But on March 25, I agreed to critique an opinion article by Solomon Mukirih. The article, titled “The Big Four and Uhuru legacy”, was 1,500 words long.

He had sent it to me instead of the opinion editor.

“Kindly critique me even as you forward to the editors,” he said.

“The article is far too long,” I responded. “Opinion pieces are normally around 700 words long.”

That was before the *Nation* was re-designed earlier this month. Two days later, he re-sent the article.

“I have addressed the issues raised,” he said. “But it lost ladha — the sweetness and narrative. Fifty per cent reduction is no longer a story but points. Hope it’s acceptable now.”

I responded, half-jokingly: “There’s nothing under the sun that can’t be told in one sentence. You just choose what to concentrate on. You don’t have to tell everything you know about a subject — unless you’re writing an encyclopedia!”

## Precis writing

Indeed, if Mr Mukirih had been to a school where precis writing was taught, he would have had no problem compressing his article without losing its “ladha”.

In precis writing, you’re supposed to express your thoughts clearly, concisely, and effectively while eliminating unnecessary words, repetitions and details.

In journalism, as in precis writing, you pack information in as few words as possible.

William Strunk, in The Elements of Style, tells us: “A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts.

“This requires not that the writer make all his sentences short, or that he avoid all detail and treat his subjects only in outline, but that every word tell.”

And one of Shakespeare’s most enduring advice is: “Brevity is the soul of wit.”

The words, spoken by Polonius, the fictional character in Hamlet, tells us you can say more — and sound more intelligent — by using the minimum number of words.

But what’s the minimum? How many words are enough? That’s the challenge.

Kennedy Buhere, communications officer in the Ministry of Education, brought home to me the challenge. He said the new format of the Nation severely limits the length of opinion articles, which we often refer to as op-eds. “It’s, in general, not easy to sound off a position on an issue, provide supporting illustrations or proofs and make a strong conclusion within the 500 words space I see allotted to columnists,” Mr Buhere lamented.

He’s right about the reduction of the word count. This column, for example, is now limited to 600 words and “Readers Have Their Say” to 300.

But not all columnists have been squeezed. Prof Makau Mutua, for instance, still has his 800 words-plus and Macharia Gaitho his 700 words-plus.

## Consuming interest

Nevertheless, how long should an op-ed be? It depends.

Looking at op-eds in various newspapers, it can be as short as 400 words. For example, The Wall Street Journal prefers 400-1,000 words.

But conventional wisdom suggests the ideal is 600-800 words. The New York Times prefers 650 words while The Washington Post prefers 750 words.

Obviously, there are cases where one would need more than 800 words to explain an idea. But that would be an exception to the rule.

Suffice it to say most readers don’t like long op-eds — unless they are of consuming interest.
