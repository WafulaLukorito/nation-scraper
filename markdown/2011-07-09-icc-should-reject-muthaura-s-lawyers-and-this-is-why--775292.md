# ICC should reject Muthaura’s lawyers, and this is why 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2011-07-09T12:32:57Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/icc-should-reject-muthaura-s-lawyers-and-this-is-why--775292  

---

They say “one bad apple spoils the bunch”. This is what may have happened to the defence team for Head of Civil Service Francis Muthaura.

Several weeks ago, QC Karim Khan, Mr Muthaura’s lead counsel, hired two former ICC prosecutors to join his team.

The two — Essa Faal and Shyamala Alagendra — had only recently left the employ of ICC Prosecutor Luis Moreno-Ocampo.

Mr Faal is Gambian, while Ms Alagendra is Malaysian.

It’s unheard of in any civilised legal jurisdiction for a lawyer to work for the prosecution, and then abscond and work for the defence in the same case.

This isn’t what happened here, but the conduct of Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra is almost certainly unethical and unprofessional.

The legal question at stake is whether Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra — while working as prosecutors for the ICC — acquired any knowledge about Mr Moreno-Ocampo’s thinking, strategies, and planning on Mr Muthaura’s case.

If so, was such knowledge limited to Mr Muthaura’s case, or did it involve the entire “PNU” case?

**Inside knowledge**

That is, did Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra also have “inside” knowledge about the cases against Deputy Prime Minister Uhuru Kenyatta and former Police Commissioner Hussein Ali?

Let’s take this to its logical conclusion. Did the two lawyers acquire any “inside” information about the “ODM” case against Eldoret North MP William Ruto, Tinderet MP Henry Kosgey and radio journalist Joshua arap Sang? That would be nuclear — really explosive

Why would any “information” or “knowledge” acquired by Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra while at the ICC on Mr Muthaura’s case — or the cases against any of the Ocampo Six — be toxic?

The answer is simple. If true, then Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra would be guilty of professional misconduct. Their behaviour would clearly be unethical.

In the United States, for example, the idea of a lawyer working on both sides of a case is unthinkable. The guilty lawyer would be severely sanctioned, and possibly disbarred.

That’s why former DPP nominee Kioko Kilukumi would have been in a legal quandary in that post because he was Mr Ruto’s lawyer against whom the State had a case. Justice is about appearances.

I understand a lawyer’s professional responsibilities. I know that every defendant and litigant, or party to a suit, deserves legal representation. Only lawyers can provide such representation.

That’s why we shouldn’t demonise lawyers who represent the mafia or any other unpopular defendants. It’s our job as lawyers to represent our clients before the courts.

That’s how justice is done, and it’s what makes a legal system function, and gives it legitimacy in the eyes of the people.

Far be it from me to ridicule so-called “lawyers for the mob,” the corrupt, or the murderous. I want to make it clear that I don’t question the right of Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra to represent Mr Muthaura.

But I question whether they are eating from both sides of the same plate. I know the Office of the Prosecutor at the ICC quite well.

It’s a small “law firm” with few prosecutors. Everybody knows everybody. Prosecutors are very collegial.

They “share” ideas and “bounce” strategies against each other. It’s a very dedicated and professional group of lawyers.

It’s not uncommon for one prosecutor to walk into the office of another to “pick their brain” on some legal question.

This is how “law firms” work. Is it possible that during any of these “collegial” chats, moments, or consultations Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra got inside information, or knowledge, about the Muthaura case, or of any of the Ocampo Six?

I believe the judges of the ICC have an obligation to determine whether Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra have behaved unethically, or unprofessionally — or even unlawfully — and whether they should be barred from representing Mr Muthaura.

But the matter of their misconduct, if established, wouldn’t end there. I believe that the court would have to bar the entire Muthaura legal team from representing him.

Why? It’s straightforward — if Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra had shared any of the “illicit” information with the rest of Mr Muthaura’s defence team, then the entire lot is tainted.

One apple spoils the whole bunch. The same goes for any of the lawyers for the other Ocampo Six — they would have to go too.

I don’t want to speculate why Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra are engaging in what seems like a high wire act.

Perhaps it’s the money Mr Muthaura has thrown at them, or the chance to represent a high profile client to establish themselves as serious defence counsel.

Perhaps it’s the adrenaline telling them to take on Mr Moreno-Ocampo and their old colleagues.

**Moreno-Ocampo’s thinking**

But I know why Mr Muthaura has hired them. He believes that they are “insiders” who know the thinking of Mr Moreno-Ocampo, his prosecutors, and the ICC judges.

It’s like having a spy from the other side on your team. It’s the next best thing to breaking into your opponent’s office and stealing his files.

But whether or not Mr Faal and Ms Alagendra have acted improperly, I believe that the ICC’s reputation has already been injured.

This “revolving door” where prosecutors can go work for the defence in the blink of an eye stinks to high heaven.

It diminishes the legitimacy and moral standing of the court. It makes the ICC look like a court in a “banana republic”.

That’s why the judges must resolve this matter in a manner that safeguards its credibility. Otherwise, justice will be viewed as having been compromised.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
