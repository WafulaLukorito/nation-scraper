The organisation and planning of a city, town, or village is a window into a society’s mind. You can see a country’s zeitgeist upon arrival at its main airport. Even more telling is the drive from the airport to the city. These are windows into a nation’s soul.

That’s why smart states pay top dollar to construct new and imposing airports. As with romantic dates, you never get another chance to make a second impression.

It’s here and gone—often forever. You know what I mean if you focus on our region. Several states are doing an exceptional job of remaking their gateways. Down south, Tanzania’s Mwalimu Julius Nyerere International Airport is a marvel. Ethiopia is building a huge airport in Addis.

Once upon a time, Embakasi Airport, now Jomo Kenyatta International Airport (JKIA), was the envy of the region. No more. While others construct architectural wonders or upgrade existing facilities to world-class status, some pin-headed politicians chastise the government for daring to take JKIA to another level.

They speak derisively of “shiny” objects. Perhaps they haven’t absorbed anything on their “benchmarking” trips abroad. It’s these shiny objects and critical infrastructure on which great economies rebound.

Whether you like it or not, President William Ruto has done in a short three years what none of his predecessors did in longer periods, including their “two terms”. He has dreamt bigger for Kenya than any of them. That’s fact, not empty conjecture.

Which brings me to planning and building. Who can contest, for example, that Kenya needs even more road networks to open up the country? The best road networks are in the Mt Kenya region.

Kenyans want to see that success replicated all over the country because no Kenyan is the child of a lesser God. It’s this infrastructure that will open up the agricultural and development potential of every part of the country. All parts of Kenya are productive.

No one should imagine that only particular cash crops must be favoured. No—but without infrastructure, none of this will be possible. Planning and then executing are key. Planning allows a state to tackle social, ethnic, and economic inequities and increase the writ of the government among its people.

In the 2010 Constitution, planning is both at the county and national levels. But let’s take the key county urban centres. Kisumu City comes to mind. For most of the last several decades, Kisumu was a sleepy and neglected town because it was perceived to be a hotbed of opposition politics.

True or not, Kisumu did not truly rise to its potential until devolution. Today, Kisumu is a shining example of great leadership and professional planning. We need to commend Prof Peter Anyang’ Nyong’o, the distinguished political scientist and political economist who, as governor, has led the county to loftier heights. Today, Kisumu puts to shame all other major urban centres in Kenya, including Nairobi. It is well planned, organised, and clean.

In contrast to Kisumu City, other cities and towns seem to think that they can build and then plan, or not plan at all. Take Nairobi, Kenya’s flagship city, for example. I don’t have the foggiest idea what’s happening. Buildings seem to spring up everywhere every second, without rhyme or reason. I know Governor Johnson Sakaja.

He’s a good man. He and the County Assembly need to get a grip on the forest of unplanned construction mushrooming everywhere, often with ugly and unseemly skyscrapers that are turning Nairobi into a catastrophe.

Previous governors and mayors did little to stem the destructive greed of the most uncouth developers, many laundering stolen loot into pathetic cement structures. Some have collapsed due to shoddy construction.

Who approves these contraptions, and why? Is there planning for water, sewage, and power before anything is done? Nairobi is not a rural village where land is plentiful and construction can more easily be done, although planning must still be a prerequisite to avoid the mess seen in large cities.

One needs only to visit major cities in America and Europe such as New York City, Los Angeles, Amsterdam, London, Geneva, and others to see the beauty of planning. Even smaller cities and villages in these countries are immaculate marvels of urban planning. Why is it so difficult for Kenyans to do even better? Is it lawlessness, impunity, or corruption? A country’s infrastructure is a window into its being.

If you look at Kenya’s urban planning, traffic culture, and general urban hygiene and sanitation, you often wonder why we don’t do better. Travel to Cape Town or Rabat—or even Addis Ababa, close to home—and you realise we aren’t doing well at all. As for an airport, we must act quickly. Either we do it at JKIA, which may not be ideal, or we use Konza. We cannot paralyse ourselves with endless debates while other countries fly past us. Let’s act now or be damned.
