# Kalonzo’s ‘strategic’ election options

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2007-10-06T21:00:00Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/news/kalonzo-s-strategic-election-options-540390  

---

If opinion polls are any guide, Mr Kalonzo Musyoka’s popularity has plummeted precipitously in the past year.

Mr Kalonzo

His paltry numbers suggest that he does not have a snowball’s chance in hell of capturing the State House.

Yet his public braggadocio does not suggest someone whose presidential campaign is on life support. Perhaps Mr Musyoka knows something that has escaped the rest of us.

I think he has discovered that the victor in the presidential elections must go through him. A critical analysis of Mr Musyoka’s strategic options reveals that he has the numbers to decide the winner.

It is perilous for a frontrunner in any presidential election to count their chickens before they hatch.

Polls are fickle and the tide can turn with several months to go before Election Day. Not long ago, Mr Musyoka sat atop the presidential polls until infighting in ODM precipitated his freefall.

President Kibaki then took top honours for months until last week when Mr Raila Odinga took the lead. But Mr Musyoka is so far behind so late in the game that only a miracle can salvage his bid. Barring any last-minute surprises, the winner of the 2007 elections will either be President Kibaki or Mr Odinga. But Mr Musyoka holds the key to the victor.

**Idiom and demeanour**

Have you listened to Mr Musyoka lately, or watched his body language? His idiom and demeanour suggest that he is now a man at peace with himself.

That is because he has come to terms with the bitter fact that there is virtually no chance that he will be the next President of the Republic of Kenya. Most significantly, the man from Mwingi North has realised that he can win by losing. He may yet have the last laugh if he plays his cards well.

My free advice to President Kibaki and Mr Odinga is that they should assiduously court Mr Musyoka if they hope to win. The truth is that he can doom either of them. Imagine that you are Mr Musyoka with a three-way electoral chessboard in front of you.

Both of your opponents are roughly matched, but one has a slight advantage over the other. But none has a commanding lead.

That is when you wear your strategic hat and calculate your options. Since you are a politician, you are most interested in your own future.

Without a doubt, the decision you make must be instrumental, not sentimental. Whatever you do must bring you closer to your ambition.

Simply put, your decision must put you within credible striking distance of State House in 2012. What, then, are your strategic options?

In 2002 when Mr Musyoka broke with Mr Daniel arap Moi and Kanu to form the Rainbow Alliance with Mr Odinga, he believed that he should have been the anointed one.

But the field was hopelessly crowded. Certainly Mr Moi did not think so, and surprised everyone by insisting that Mr Uhuru Kenyatta was the chosen one.

I watched Mr Musyoka’s body language in the heady days of the Rainbow Alliance as he spoke before frenzied multitudes at Uhuru Park and other historic venues.

That’s when he first really believed that he was presidential material, and that the Kenyan people were ready for him.

**Presidential image**

Mr Musyoka has spent the last five years cultivating a presidential image. That is why it was not a stretch for him to defy Mr Kibaki – even when he was in the Cabinet – or oppose Mr Odinga within ODM-K. According to him, he was the child of destiny.

He did not see why Mr Odinga could not realise this basic truism. For a long time, the polls seemed to vindicate this self-image.

But then the roof fell when Mr Odinga – who also believes he is the anointed one – turned the heat on Mr Musyoka in ODM-K. As they say, there cannot be two rats in one hole.

Both men had no choice but to go their separate ways. Now both must coldly reassess their relationship and decide whether to hang together, or hang separately.

Judging by his tough rhetoric and cool deportment, Mr Musyoka gives the impression that he will go all the way to the finish line – alone.

This certainly would give him kudos for courage. He could walk with his head high, defeated but unbowed. But, as practised in Kenya, politics has not been governed by principle.

In fact, nice guys normally finish last in our country. Besides, Mr Musyoka’s ardent supporters want to win, not just back a losing horse. The question is how Mr Musyoka and his fellow politicos define winning.

In making the decision, I am sure they will be guided by the regrettable – but utilitarian – adage that there are no permanent enemies in politics.

Since it would be unwise for Mr Musyoka to go all the way in a quixotic hunt for the State House, he has only two options.

He should decide which of the two likely winners – President Kibaki or Mr Odinga – he is going to back. But he cannot go to them on a bended knee. Rather, he must exact from the one he is going to back the maximum advantage possible.

For him, this must be like a business deal because there is precious little ideologically to distinguish PNU from ODM. They both draw their leadership and politics from the same pool.

**Ideology**

But that should not really concern Mr Musyoka because he also swims in the same pool.

Should Mr Musyoka support Mr Odinga and ODM? This may look like an attractive option, but it is fraught with peril. First, there is the bad blood between Mr Musyoka and Mr Odinga.

One gets the impression that it would be very difficult for them to work together. Besides, there is no place large enough in the so-called ODM Pentagon for him.

Mr Odinga is not going to remove Mr Musalia Mudavadi, his running mate, and replace him with Mr Musyoka. The local vote would flee ODM en masse.

Nor would Mr Odinga kick Mr Ruto down the ladder to accommodate Mr Musyoka. That would scuttle Ruto’s grassroots vote. Even more serious is the fact that Mr Odinga would most likely serve two terms until 2017 and then give way to Mr Mudavadi.

**Running mate**

Mr Musyoka cannot wait that long. However, if Mr Musyoka backs Mr Kibaki – and becomes his running mate – he would probably stand a better chance of inheriting the whole kit and caboodle in 2012.

This assumes that Mr Kenyatta would not be favoured to succeed Mr Kibaki.

Unless we change the ethnic matrix of our politics – and run on ideology – this is the way our politicians will continue to think.

But, as matters stand now, Mr Musyoka can call his shots, and throw the election to either Mr Odinga or President Kibaki.

*Makau Mutua is SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights Commission.*
