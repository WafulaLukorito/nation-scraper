Last week, President [William Ruto](http://) took a bold step to recognise and provide recompense to victims of protests and riots covering the period from January 1, 2017 to July 31 this year.

The move was historic and unprecedented. It’s a rare action that has not happened in Kenya, or in Africa, and around the globe for that matter.

Governments are loathe to recognise victims, and especially to compensate them, even when they are ordered to do so by courts. President Ruto appointed me to head the mechanism for the process of compensation.

I never thought such a remarkable and empathetic step would ever happen in Kenya in my lifetime. Whatever anyone thinks of President Ruto, no previous Kenyan leader has had the courage to do what he’s done.

I’ve studied, written about, lectured on, and practiced the law of transitional justice for close to three decades. The default position for most governments is to bury unspeakable pasts with the victims. Even in South Africa, which gave the world the most famous truth commission, headed by the iconic Archbishop Desmond Tutu, victims of apartheid were left to fend for themselves for the sake of “reconciliation” between the White perpetrators of apartheid and their Black victims.

Elsewhere, from Argentina to Rwanda and Sierra Leone, transitional justice mechanisms have left victims largely unrequited. In my scholarship, which is taught worldwide, I have critiqued the failures of transitional justice responses. That said, we cannot allow the perfect to be the enemy of the good.

Transitional justice normally has three limbs – compensation, reparations, or restitution; punishment or sanction of perpetrators; and reforms to make it less likely that such abuses will recur.

## Mass atrocity

Each of these three limbs represent a form of justice for victims. All three have never been achieved in any country. The reason is simple; social and political disequilibria, resource constraints, and lack of political will curtail comprehensive responses to atrocities. As such, societies and states do what’s practicable and possible.

In other cases, responses to atrocity are staggered and variegated with courts, truth commissions, and peacemaking, and reforms in the mix. The fact is that there has never been a perfect response to mass atrocity. That’s why what President Ruto has done in Kenya is so epochal.

Our compensation mandate will run out in 120 days. We have no second to waste. That’s why we have literally hit the ground running. I know that my team will do a superb job for the victims, their families, and loved ones. For those who were fatally injured, I assure them that their loss will not be in vain. For those with serious injuries, I guarantee that we will stand with you in the most meaningful ways.

This is my life’s work, and I will not let this opportunity go to waste. President Ruto would not have taken this singular and most critical action without the conviction to deliver on the mandate he has given us. I applaud him.

No one can deny that our country has been restless. The world over, we now live an age of discontent. Kenya isn’t unique in this respect. I live in the United States, the wealthiest nation in world history. Yet, even there as is widely known, the country is restless. Part of this restlessness is because of the failures of democracy and the global order that was ushered in post-1945. This Pax Americana is under stress. No new world order has risen to replace it. That’s why we all grope in the dark.

The restlessness in Kenya, with its Gen Z phenomenon and angry politics, cannot be divorced from global economic and social spasms. But we can’t be idle and not act responsibly in the middle of the storm.

That’s why this compensation process will help heal our nation and allow us to stitch our social fabric together. I believe the majority of our people, including those who have been negatively impacted by the protests and riots, support the President as he looks forward to the proceeds of our compensation work.

## Powerful politicians

We will not disappoint. Unfortunately, I’ve noted a loud and noisy corner of refuseniks who oppose this noble effort. Not surprisingly, those opposed are rich and powerful politicians who see this effort as an opportunity to score cheap political points against President Ruto. These greedy politicians and their acolytes hate the President so deeply that they would rather deny the victims compensation to deny him the credit of the exercise.

Strangely, the same people had been shouting themselves hoarse advocating for compensation until the President sanctioned it. Their hypocrisy is nauseating.

While they live in large sprawling mansions in leafy suburbs and drive as many as six or more high-end vehicles, they don’t give a whit about the parent who lost a loved one in the protests. This is callous and heartless. I condemn them.

*Prof Mutua is SUNY Distinguished Professor and Margaret W. Wong Professor at Buffalo Law School, The State University of New York. He’s Senior Advisor on Constitutional Affairs to President William Ruto. @makaumutua.*
