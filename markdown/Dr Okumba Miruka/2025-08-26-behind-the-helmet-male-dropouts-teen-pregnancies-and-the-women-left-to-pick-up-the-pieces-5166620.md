## What you need to know:

* The male-dominated boda boda industry creates jobs for thousands but fuels school dropouts, road deaths, and sexual exploitation of schoolgirls.
* The sector simultaneously empowers women traders through improved market access whilst contributing to teenage pregnancies and crime.

When fellow columnist Makau Mutua called for a total ban on boda boda operators in Kenya’s major towns, it naturally caused some disquiet with protagonists pointing out that such a move would throw thousands of youth out of employment. But Mutua’s sentiments signal an urgent need to correct what ails this industry. It is from this angle that it is also important to look at the gender dimensions of the phenomenon.

This business is dominated by male youth, which has made it take the aggressive character of its operators, best illustrated whenever they feel aggrieved. Just a few days after Makau’s article, boda boda operators in Nairobi torched a public service vehicle for allegedly running over one of them. While they see this as solidarity, it depicts the industry in extremely bad light. The aggression may be also linked to the do-or-die competition arising from too many operators chasing too few passengers.

While boda boda has employed thousands of male youth, it has also contributed to their dropping out of school. In an agriculture sector project sponsored by the World Bank, there were study findings that male youth dropped out of primary school in Kapasbet and Vihiga so as to join boda boda.

They argued that it is a source of quick money, there are successful predecessors and the pursuit of formal education no longer guarantees employment. Moreover, a motor bike is affordable and entry into the industry is not complicated. It is, thus, a low hanging fruit and the default attraction for male youth.

A critical issue is that of road accidents. Just before Makau’s article, an earlier one cited statistics confirming that the industry is the major cause of road accidents, injuries and deaths.

What are the gender dimensions of this? Quite a number of the male youth in boda boda are married and support their families. When they die or get injured, the wives and children are left destitute. Their roles are transferred to the wives, increasing the socio-economic burden.

The boda boda industry has been cited as a major contributor to teenage pregnancies that lead to school dropouts, increased population of single parents, premature motherhood, increased burden at household level and lost investments.

The study on the project already cited established that school girls are easily lured by petty incentives such as free rides and pocket money offered by the boda boda operators from their daily disposable income.

But the riders also claim that they are blackmailed by female customers due to close physical proximity and provocative dressing. Many have fallen prey to older women or those simply out for adventure.

## Female clients

In the same study, boda boda riders in Trans Nzoia cited that when sent by busy men to deliver items home, the wives often lure or force them into intercourse by threatening to accuse them of attempted rape if they do not comply. There are also female clients who feign lack of cash and instead offer to pay through sexual services. One woman who had become addicted to boda boda for sexual satisfaction in one town, for example, actually declared that “My boda boda is my comforter”.

The riders are also commonly associated with crime, either as the primary actors or abettors. Many have been linked to ferrying of criminals to and from their missions because of speed and maneuverability. This creates the impression of a volatile male youth population likely to be seen as more of a liability than an asset.

Be that as it may, the boda boda industry has many positive things about it. First, it has created ready employment for many male youngsters who would otherwise be idle. There are even university students who fund their education from boda boda income.

Second, the riders have shortened distances to and from markets, aiding millions of women traders and promoting their livelihoods. Third, boda bodas are handy in transporting women in labour to clinics for qualified attention, hence, saved lives. They are the main transporters of community health workers hence immensely enhance access to advice, services and commodities.

Fourth, the industry contributes to girls’ education merely by aiding movement to school, notwithstanding what is already said. Fifth, they can and have, in some areas, become champions of gender equality, acting as vigilantes and agile campaigners against gender-based violence (GBV). In Eldoret, for example, a boda boda rider dressed in a T-shirt emblazoned with messages against GBV chanced upon a couple fighting. He stopped and asked whether they had read the message on his jacket while his colleagues separated the two.

The double edges of the boda boda industry reminisce of a traditional musician in Siaya who always sings of his sponsors as “good on one side but bad on the other”.
