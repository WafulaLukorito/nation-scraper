# 2010 must be pivotal for key reforms 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-01-02T15:22:46Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/2010-must-be-pivotal-for-key-reforms--619442  

---

Transitions are as critical in a person’s life as they are in the life of a nation. That’s because a nation, like a person, is a living organism. Like a person, a nation has vital organs whose health is essential for its wellbeing.

Smoke, too much drink, or too much red meat and you damage vital organs. Similarly, if you abuse the nation’s social and body fabric you retard its growth, or even kill it. That’s almost what happened to Kenya in 2008 following the disputed presidential election results.

We saw the heart of the country almost torn apart. I believe that’s an experience most Kenyans want to banish forever. But that will not happen unless this year – 2010 – becomes a pivotal one for key reforms.

We must dedicate ourselves to a new beginning in 2010. I am not talking about the pedestrian New Year resolutions which are broken at the sight of the first temptation. We need to think noble thoughts, and act not only with grace and high-mindedness.

We need to take individual responsibility for every thought and act. I say this because a nation is only as noble as its inhabitants, and as clean as those who claim it. A nation of petty thieves and corrupt officials is a den of misery and mediocrity, if not worse.

A nation of scheming ethnic warlords is a snake’s pit that devours its young and lays waste to the promise of greatness. Too often, Kenya has been all of the above – captive to thieves and ethnic warlords who cannot see beyond their noses for the myopia of self-interest.

But charity must begin at home if Kenya is to realise its enormous potential, and live up to its strategic global importance. As they say, you cannot continue to do the same thing over and over again and expect different results. Our collective and individual behaviour must change.

As one holy book reminds us, “for what does it profit a man to gain the whole world, yet lose his own soul?” Think about that whether you are a leader or an ordinary citizen.

For what is the worth of personal aggrandisement if your fellow man lives in squalor? What comfort is it to you to drive the latest SUV – which pollutes our environment – through dilapidated streets with your windows up and doors tightly locked for fear of your life? Is that the country you want to call home?

That’s why I want us to commit to several national projects that we must successfully execute this year – 2010 – to re-launch Mother Kenya. The first, and most important, project for 2010 is the constitution. As if we do not understand the value of money and time, we have wasted two decades trying to reform the state by writing – and passing – a democratic charter.

At every critical juncture, small-minded and selfish leaders have shot down the process. In plain Kiswahili, *tumekubali kunzungushwa na viongozi wetu.* Our leaders have taken us round and round, and we – like sheep – have let them. I can hear them again trying to shoot down the draft constitution. Isn’t time we stood up against them?

We must firmly tell the leaders that the constitution is not about them, but about us – the ordinary *mwananchi.* That’s why we must pass this draft constitution *wapende wasipende. Madharau yao yanakwishia hapa!* We must drive this point home in the lead-up to the referendum over the draft constitution. We must do so by mass action.

You know there will be rallies galore called by politicians to hurl insults at each other and demagogue the draft constitution. We should attend the rallies if only to teach such politicos a lesson. We must seize every moment to publicly humiliate those who attack the draft constitution. We must boo every time a politician attacks the draft constitution and leave the rally if he or she persists.

After all, they cannot address an empty stadium. This is people power – we must use it. This is the only way to tame politicians with petty minds and ignoble motives. The second national project for 2010 is bringing justice to the perpetrators of post-election violence. The International Criminal Court will deal with those who bear the greatest responsibility.

But we must prosecute the littler fish that did the actual killing, raping, maiming, and looting. We must not let these monsters get away scot-free and live among us. We shall rue the day if we do because they will be back to kill, rape, maim, and loot again. They have tasted our blood and you can be sure they will come back for it unless we put them away and throw away the key.

We must deal with them without pity. That’s why we must insist on a special tribunal to bring justice to these malevolent characters. The last final national project for 2010 is excavating Kenya’s past to establish what went wrong so that we can fix it. We will be on our way to renewing Kenya if we can execute these three national projects in 2010.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights Commission.*
