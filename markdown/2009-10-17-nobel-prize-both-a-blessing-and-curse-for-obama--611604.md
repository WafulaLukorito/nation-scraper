# Nobel prize both a blessing and curse for Obama

**Date:** 2009-10-17T12:39:24Z

## What you need to know:

* His presidency must deliver on global peace initiatives and cooperation

Even to his diehard fans, the selection of President Barack Obama as this year’s Nobel Peace laureate was a stunning surprise.

Mr Obama himself seemed to have been equally taken by surprise and deeply humbled by the honour. You could not but admire his enormous grace in accepting the singular recognition. Pundits have since torn into each other over whether President Obama deserved the elevation.

**Lofty ideals**

While there are good arguments on both sides, there is little doubt that the honour is both a blessing and a curse for Mr Obama. But it will only be a curse if the Obama presidency — and not necessarily the man — fails to live up to the lofty ideals of the award.

The Nobel Peace Prize can do one of three things. It can look back, focus on the future, or combine the past with the future. If it looks back, then it is recognition for a life’s work. That presumably was the case with Mr Nelson Mandela in 1993.

If it focuses on the future, then it is an attempt to bend the arc of history. That would appear to have been the case when Chairman Yasser Arafat, Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin and Foreign Minister Shimon Peres were awarded the prize in 1994.

The Nobel Committee was nudging them to make peace. If it focuses on the past and the future, it honours a life’s work but also seeks to influence the future. That was the case when Mr Al Gore won the prize in 2007.

In Mr Gore’s case, just like in Prof Wangari Maathai’s, the Nobel Committee wanted to recognise their indefatigable work on behalf of the environment and peace while at the same time giving urgency to climate change. It was a call to everyone to reverse global warming and preserve the environment or face ecological damnation.

Mr Mandela was being rewarded for bringing South Africa to the edge of liberation while Mr F.W. de Klerk, his co-recipient, was being given a shove to end apartheid.

More often than not, the Nobel Peace Prize looks both backwards and forwards. In no case has the prize ever been given to a person who had achieved nothing. All the past recipients have been worthy and notable for their accomplishments.

This brings me to Mr Obama. Some knuckleheads have suggested that Mr Obama was not deserving of the prize because he has not done much. In fact, one of them suggested that Mr Obama should have turned down the prize!

I thought we all live on planet Earth but, apparently, some of us live on Mars. It was not hard to figure out the voices of dissent. They were mostly those who have not accepted – and find it very hard – to live with an Obama presidency. To them, the Nobel Peace Prize was adding insult to injury.

**Whites males**

But I will humour them with a response. Mr Obama has accomplished what had eluded American democracy for over two centuries. Since the founding of the republic, only white males had called the White House home.

In what must surely go down as the most historic presidential election in the United States, Mr Obama vanquished all his opponents – first a white female in the Democratic Party – and then a white male Republican in the General Election.

It was a feat no person of colour or a woman had ever accomplished. How could the deracialisation of the American presidency not be one of the most epochal moments of our times?

It was very clear that Mr Obama ascended this historic plateau because of his incredible leadership ability, inclusive and clear vision, impeccable political skills, and personal integrity. No one could have done it at this time – and now – but him and him alone. To Mr Obama goes most of the credit. That is why he is deserving of the Nobel Peace Prize.

But he is deserving of the prize for another reason. The answer lies in the campaign that Mr Obama ran. Look at the policy positions that he took. He single-handedly changed the political discourse in the country. On race, an endemic problem that continues to vex America, Mr Obama opened new vistas. On domestic policies, he underscored a return of core values of decency and humanity.

On the economy, he advocated accountability for businesses and an investment in people. On foreign policy, he turned away from the grim adventures of his predecessor.

As the Nobel Committee noted, Mr Obama’s initiatives seek to reduce nuclear arms, ease tensions with the Muslim world, and emphasise diplomacy and multilateralism over unilateralism.

I agree this is a tall order. Nor is there any doubt in my mind that the Nobel Peace Prize is a double-edged sword for President Obama. It recognises what he has done thus far, but it is also aspirational.

That is, Mr Obama’s presidency must deliver on global peace initiatives and international cooperation. The prize attempts to take the “empire” out of the American republic during the Obama presidency.

Under Mr Obama, the United States must be a good neighbour. In particular, the prize constrains Mr Obama’s choices in hot spots like Afghanistan.

Unlike his predecessor, he cannot simply bomb countries back into the Stone Age, or pre-emptively attack other states, as America did in Iraq. In that sense, the prize is a kind of a strait-jacket.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights Commission.*
