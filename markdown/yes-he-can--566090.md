**MUTUMA MATHIU in Nairobi: It’s all about how badly McCain will lose**

Let’s shoot from the hip: It is no longer a question of if Senator John McCain, the Republican candidate in the US presidential election, will lose. It is a question of how badly.

I did not learn my politics in some fancy Amherst college. I learnt it as a campaign thug in the villages and hamlets of Imenti Central.

Sometimes a candidate is doing so badly, the mood is so bad and the wave against him so big, it doesn’t really matter how many boxes you stuff. And Mr McCain is doing very badly.

**Opinion polls**

To win, he will need to win in areas where Mr Barack Obama is beating him by a mile in the opinion polls. For example, he will have to win in Pennsylvania, where Mr Obama is leading him by 10 per cent in the polls.

It will be a lot easier for Mr Obama to beat Mr McCain in Mr McCain’s Arizona home state, where Mr McCain leads Mr Obama by only five per cent in the polls, than for Mr McCain to take Pennsylvania.

To give a general local interpretation, assume President Mwai Kibaki was running against Prime Minister Raila Odinga in 2012. Assume Mr Kibaki needed to win in Rift Valley to get the Presidency and Mr Odinga was leading him there with 10 per cent in the Steadman polls.

Now assume that Mr Kibaki was only ahead of Mr Odinga by five per cent in Central and there was a chance that he could lose there. Whom would you call the election for? Exactly.

**Political campaign**

Mr McCain packaged himself as a candidate of change, but he has a conservative economic agenda, basically carrying forward some of President Bush’s tax policies.

And, for his running mate, he chose an individual who is being described as a “whack job” and a “rogue”.

Mr Obama, along with the luck and the rest of it, is the deadlier political strategist.

**KEVIN J. KELLY in New York: Forget racism, most voters are colour-blind**

It was the reaction to OJ Simpson’s conviction in early October on kidnapping and robbery charges that made me finally believe all the poll numbers pointing to victory for Barack Obama.

Everyone remembers OJ, right? He’s the former US football star acquitted in 1995 of killing his ex-wife and her friend in Los Angeles.

Responses to Simpson’s trial dramatised — and deepened — the racial polarisation that has afflicted the US since the time of slavery.

Many African-Americans saw OJ as a victim of a racist frame-up. Many white Americans believed he had literally got away with murder. The tensions and divisions between the races were stark and disheartening.

It seemed then that the US would never overcome its frightening obsession with skin colour.

Obama’s 2004 election to the Senate from Illinois — a state with a nearly 80 per cent white population — suggested that race might be starting to wane as a political factor in America. But whites accounted for all the 99 other members of the Senate.

When Obama launched his campaign for the presidency last year, most analysts rated him a long shot, at best.

Some suggested that his “un-American”-sounding name, his biracial identity and his links to Africa would turn off a critical mass of white voters, making him unelectable.

Millions of whites did vote for Obama, however, enabling him to wrest the Democratic Party’s presidential nomination from the overwhelming early favourite, Sen Hillary Clinton. Maybe racism really was becoming less pervasive in the US.

That possibility seemed to grow stronger as polls this summer showed Obama moving ahead of Republican nominee John McCain.

Then came the talk of a “Bradley Effect.” That’s a reference to former Los Angeles Mayor Tom Bradley, an African-American, who in 1982 was leading comfortably in the polls tracking a US Senate election in California.

When he lost, many observers deduced that large numbers of whites had lied to pollsters.

According to this theory, shame at admitting to racial prejudice had led many white Californians to claim they would vote for the black candidate — until they were in the privacy of the polling booth.

Dr Martin Luther King’s dream that all Americans would one day be judged on the content of their character rather than the colour of their skin does, at long last, appear to be coming true.

This Tuesday, I’m almost certain, US voters will choose the clearly superior candidate — who just happens to be black.

**MAKAU MUTUA in New York: Tuesday is the day of ultimate black history**

On Tuesday night, history will be made in America. Senator Barack, born of a black Kenyan father and a white mother from Kansas, will ascend to the most powerful office in the world.

He will become the President of the United States of America.

For me, it will be a moment of great exhilaration and hope. Exhilaration that a member of the most burdened race on the planet will lead the most powerful state.

Hope that President Obama will use the power and the might of the United States for the good of America and the world.

**Baited breath**

In the 1990s, I watched with baited breath as another iconic African-American came within a whisker of becoming a candidate for President of the United States.

That person was General Colin Powell, the decorated American soldier and diplomat who served as Secretary of State for President George W. Bush before leaving the administration in disgust.

But it was the Rev Jesse Jackson who really opened those doors in the American mind. General Powell stood on Rev Jackson’s shoulders, and Senator Obama now stands on both of their shoulders.

That is why I was so proud to see General Powell categorically — and without equivocation — endorsed Senator Obama over Senator John McCain, the Republican.

General Powell said that Mr Obama is a transformational figure. I could not agree more.

**CHARLES ONYANGO-OBBO in Nairobi: Why I’m putting my money on Obama victory**

Barack Obama will be elected America’s president this week.

I say this not because of the many opinion polls that suggest he has the US presidential election in the bag, or because his ancestors’ village in Kenya is across from mine in Uganda (in a manner of speaking), and so objectivity has deserted me.

And I don’t fear that come November 4, the over-flogged “Bradley effect”, call it the white racist vote, will break out and see his rival the curmudgeonly John McCain wipe the floor with the Illinois senator.

About three years ago I was in the US visiting with my brother and his lovely wife. One day we were driving from Washington to Maryland where they live, when he took a detour to show me the neighbourhoods where some of Washington’s moneyed and well-heeled class live.

As we drove through, I noticed quite a number of black people walking their dogs and cats, and pushing prams.

“The brothers and sisters,” I said, “seem to have made good. The first time I came to the US, I don’t remember seeing so many black people living in rich neighbourhoods as I do these days”.

Richard, who had just returned from New York on assignment for the company he was working for then, said he thought “black America is disappearing”. He said he had just been on Wall Street, and that what struck him most was that far more black people were working in the big financial companies there than most people would imagine.

Then he said something that I kept thinking about long after.

He said that he was intrigued that “there is no difference between a black and white Wall Street operator. They are all cut-throat,” he said, “and I think some of the brothers are hungrier and nastier than the white guys”.

What really existed, he argued, despite all the talk about racism was “rich black, Asian, Hispanic and white Americans on the one hand, and poor black, Asia, Hispanic, and white folks on the other”.

Two years later, when white musician Eminem became the biggest rapper in the US, a music that was born out of protest and the anger of poor black neighbourhoods in American cities, I thought Richard was right after all.

**Black vote**

When Obama threw his hat into the ring, I watched to see if he could make a naked run for the black vote. When he didn’t, I knew he had taken a big step towards winning the White House.

Reason? There are simply not enough black votes on their own to help anyone win a presidential election in America even if they got 100 per cent of it. It is not rocket science to figure that, but it takes a lot of courage to act on it as Obama did.

The Rev Jesse Jackson, a politician with far more street credibility than Obama, didn’t when he contested the Democratic Party nominations in 1984 and 1988.

Secondly, too many non-white people fear to be thought of as “being white”. If they are black, they tend to turn their backs on what, erroneously, are considered “white traits”. Thus, instead of meticulous organisation, they choose spontaneity.

Instead of the cool unemotional British upper lip (people like Presidents Kibaki and Paul Kagamme of Rwanda almost have it), they go for passion and exuberance.

They are not content with the crowds they are talking to nodding in agreement or crying in muted emotional outbreaks. They want wild cheers.

**Flawless campaign**

Obama didn’t walk that road. So when it became apparent that he had set up an awesome flawless campaign operation, and a lethal fundraising machine, I put my money on him.

When he slipped behind in opinion polls after McCain got a big bounce from the Republican party convention, Obama didn’t lose his cool.

Other politicians, like former President Bill Clinton, would go off to a club, wear dark glasses and blow the saxophone with a band. If Obama had done that, he would have fitted the stereotype of a go-happy black man.

He didn’t become excited and stayed the course. McCain, however, hasn’t. When he fell behind in opinion polls, he became a nasty table banger who frightens the children.

Can Obama lose? Mathematically, yes. But I would still ask: “Where the hell did that come from?”

**TOM WOLF in Nairobi: Heyday or doomsday for the world?**

However dramatic the contrasts between the choices the Democratic and Republican parties are offering the American electorate in this presidential election in personal terms (age, race, geographic origin-base, career path, etc.) it is the policy implications of the outcome that deserve most public attention, both within the U.S. and around the world.

This is so even if, probably everywhere – including America – politicians can be counted on to make at least some campaign promises that they know quite well are impossible to keep.

Indeed, the desperate fiscal implications of the recent banking system melt-down seem to have further encouraged such tendencies, as campaign rhetoric seeks to keep up with the public’s – and the world’s – mounting anxieties.

**Weighty issues**

But aside from such weighty issues, there is another one that is also bound to attract very intense attention come November 5 (or as soon after that as all the votes have been counted): the accuracy of the countless opinion polls carried out throughout the exhausting duration of these campaigns, stretching back to the respective parties’ state-level, primary contests.

While pollsters around the world tend to shun the predictive assumptions implied by their work, the fact that Barack Obama has led John McCain consistently in all the major polls suggests that pollsters could suffer considerable professional damage should he lose, or even win by a much narrower margin than most current polls are indicating.

But just what are these polls showing? How much do those of the various firms differ, and how much do the results depend upon the assumptions embodied in their sampling methods?

As of a few days ago, the Gallup Daily (October 28) showed how Obama’s lead varies depending upon the methodological assumption employed: 50-43 per cent among all registered voters, 49-47 per cent: among those voters considered most likely to vote based on past election turn-out patterns, and 51-44 per cent based on an assumption of a significantly increased turn-out (but still lower than if all those registered were to vote).

For its part, a few days earlier the Science Daily (October 16) offered the results of nine polls. Six give Obama the lead (ranging from 50.1 to 58.2 per cent), one shows McCain ahead, and two describe their results as “too close to call” (though all of these surveys were conducted before the October financial crash, which appears to have been a ‘surprise gift’ to Obama).

More recently (on October 29), however, a Rasmussen poll gave Obama a mere three per cent lead over McCain (50-47 per cent, with two per cent undecided), hardly outside the margin-of-error (of 2.9 per cent): in other words, a statistical tie.

Significantly, this was the first time in over a month that the difference between them has been within three per cent, and the first time since September 24 that McCain’s support exceeded 46 per cent.

Over the previous 33 days, Obama had led in this firm’s polls by between four and 8 per cent, suggesting his advantage is in decline.

**Critical sample for McCain**

One sub-section of these national samples is especially vital for McCain: that of rural (and thus, more conservative/Republican) America, constituting a fifth of the country’s population.

According to a recent survey of such voters in 13 key states (with a margin-of-error of 3.4 per cent) undertaken by the Center for Rural Studies in Whitesburg, Kentucky, significant – and for McCain, quite worrying – changes have occurred.

Whereas a month ago Obama was trailing him among rural Americans by 10 per cent (51-41 per cent), as of October 21 they were virtually tied.

Also at play, it has been suggested, is the impact of his choice of Alaska Governor Sarah Palin, as the boost McCain got initially from her has more than evaporated. Whereas a month ago her favourabe-unfavourable balance among these rural-dwellers was 48-33 per cent, it had slipped to 40-42per cent by the third week in October.

McCain’s decline among this group thus constitutes a major weakness in the closing days of his campaign, a fact underscored by the 19-point advantage President Bush had over John Kerry among these voters in 2004.

**Polls in key states**

Due to the critical importance of individual state races for the allocation of electoral college votes, it is necessary to look at some poll results at this level.

In should first be noted that in key states, a number of the actual election results may be so close that the margins of error of polls conducted at this level (ranging from 2.5-4.0 per cent, depending upon just how many people were interviewed) may be too large to predict actual outcomes.

According to Quinnipiac University Polling Institute’s poll of October 23, for example, Obama enjoyed (low) double-digit leads (with a +/-4% margin of error) in all eight states surveyed: Wisconsin, Minnesota, Iowa, Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, Illinois and Pennsylvania (enjoying a full 13-point lead in the latter), and with these states representing a hefty total of 117 (or 43 per cent) of the 270 electoral votes needed to win.

Here it should be noted these states include six of the nine closest state contests in 2004, three of which were won by Bush, and that a month earlier, in all but one Obama and McCain were in a statistical tie.

In these states, as nationally, the same two factors mentioned above (i.e., the banking-financial crisis, and the selection of Palin as his running mate) appear also to have depressed McCain’s standing.

For example, in Wisconsin, McCain’s ‘unfavourable’ rating went from 36 per cent to 43 per cent in the four weeks from mid-September (when he chose Palin); Palin’s own unfavourable rating there increased from 31 per cent to 45 period during this same period.

Yet in terms of preserving his party’s base, the biggest danger for McCain among these states is said to be Indiana, a “solid Republican” state that Bush won by 21 per cent in 2004. Ohio – another state that the Republicans won (if only very narrowly) four years ago also appears to have dropped into Obama’s pocket, where by last week he enjoyed a lead of 14 per cent.

Peter Brown, assistant director of the Quinnipiac University Poll, summed up this situation: “While a victory is still possible, Mr McCain’s path to the White House has become increasingly narrow and difficult. He has to pull off a political miracle the likes of which has not occurred since modern political polling began.”

Having looked at these results and trends, something should be said here about the wider polling environment in which this drama is being played out.

On the one hand (and as has been evident), even when the variance of these polls falls outside statistical margins-of-error, such polls rarely if ever attract the kind of suspicion and acrimony seen in last year’s Kenyan election, for example.

Given the much wider familiarity with survey methodology, the public can be confident that any deliberate falsification of results could quite easily amount to the professional-reputational suicide of the culprits.

In addition, in a situation where a dozen or more firms/media houses and academic-research groups are conducting them, consumers of polls can see for themselves the variance-range of results, and seek (and generally receive) convincing explanations for such variance, when it occurs.

On the other hand, however, there are some aspects of this particular presidential contest that present unprecedented challenges to the pollsters’ profession.

So, has Obama already won?

During the Democratic primaries, Obama often did worse than exit polls indicated. Most dramatic was the case in New Hampshire, where they showed a clear Obama win, yet when the votes were counted, Hillary Clinton emerged on top.

So how sure can anyone be about these polls? Peter Brown argues that if Obama’s day-before-election polling lead is four per cent or more, there is simply no way McCain can win.

But anything less than that brings three factors into play that will likely determine the outcome: (1) sampling bias based on the exclusion of (generally younger) voters who have no fixed telephone lines; (2) turn-out of first-time (younger) voters; and (3) latent non-white antipathy.

While the first two factors work in Obama’s favour (by underestimating his support), the third one, Brown suggests, does so in McCain’s.

**1. Sampling bias**  
All of those polls cited above were conducted by telephone, using directory-listed numbers. Yet it has been estimated that some 12 per cent of adult (Americans have no fixed-line phones, but only a mobile one, excluding them from nationally random sample surveys of this type.

In fact, it is illegal in some states to use mobile phone numbers for survey research of any kind, apparently because this would involve contacting respondents while driving vehicles.

In addition, without being able to determine where mobile phone users live, (in contrast to fixed-line respondents whose addresses are shown in phone directories) data obtained from them cannot be used in the analysis of state races, making it less useful to include even those who can be reached.

And since such mobile phone-only users tend to be younger, their exclusion can bias the sample in a way that hurts a candidate viewed as more attractive by this section of the population.

**2. Turn-out**   
The next factor is turn-out. Several aspects of which should be noted. While long-standing patterns indicate that it is likely to be lower among the less well-off (and thus more ‘Democratic’) section of the electorate, the closeness of this race, the situation the country finds itself in (e.g. the economy, foreign military involvement, etc.), the increased ease of early voting, and the excitement surrounding Obama’s candidacy (and the record expenditure in this election, especially on the Obama side) suggest that we may see the highest turn-out rate in recent U.S. history.

At the same time, while Obama has begun to admonish his supporters not to be complacent based on his lead in nearly all the polls, McCain is having to try and counter a mounting sense of defeatism among his backers, arguing that “the only poll that counts is the one on November 4.”

In other words, will Obama’s poll leads dishearten McCain’s supporters, or make them even more determined to vote so as to prevent an Obama win (and possible sweep of both Houses of Congress as well)?

History has shown that such youthful (and especially first-time) voters can make the difference.

For example, while such supporters were the vital ingredient in Senator George McGovern’s capture of the Democratic party’s nomination in 1972, his defeat by President Richard Nixon stemmed in large part from their turn-out rate that was far below that of older people, a pattern that has varied little since.

Given the extremely high levels of voter registration of such youth, however, their performance is likely to have a major impact on the outcome of this 2008 race, potentially in Obama’s favour.

**3. ‘Lying’ to pollsters**

Two aspects of such ‘dishonesty’ arise here. One has to do with conscious versus unconscious deceit. In general terms, psychological experiments designed by University of Virginia Psychology Professor Brian Nosek to test this issue revealed that “participants are often surprised to learn that they may have unconscious biases regarding candidates, or racial or religious views that are quite different from their (genuinely) stated beliefs.

**Most popular example**

For example, few people in modern [American] society are actively racist, but most of us possess implicit associations linking white people with good and black people with bad more easily than the reverse.”

With regard to voting-intention interviews, what may happen here, according to Nosek, is not lying as such, but a contradiction between the expression of genuine intent to an anonymous pollster and how the actual vote is (eventually) cast.

But sometimes respondents in such surveys may indeed be too embarrassed to tell the truth.

The most popular example in America is taken from the defeat of then Los Angeles mayor Tom Bradley in the 1982 gubernatorial race in California, from which the term ‘the Bradley effect’ is derived.

The last state-wide poll three weeks before that election showed that Bradley’s lead had dropped from 14 per cent to seven per cent over the previous month, and a Los Angeles Times exit poll likewise gave Bradley just a single-digit lead, while several polls conducted on behalf of his Republican opponent had them tied on election day. Underlying this concern in the Obama camp is the fact that since African-Americans constitute only 12 per cent of population (and even if demographic forecasts suggest that whites will constitute less than half of the American population by mid-century) a winner (especially on the Democratic side, the political ‘home’ of the vast majority of this section of the population) needs to get the bulk of his/her votes elsewhere.

So are all of those (especially whites) who have been proclaiming support for Obama really going to vote for him? As the Christian Science Monitor (of October 28) pointed out: “The presidential polls may look good for Sen Barack Obama, but many of his supporters look at the numbers and feel queasy. Are the numbers for real, or is there a quiet racism lurking behind them that’s not being picked up?...”

**Still exists**

At the same time, some pollsters question whether this ‘effect’ still exists, with many analysts arguing that no such factor has been identifiable in any race involving an African-American since 1996, even if none of those contests involved the presidency.

For example, although Harold Ford Jr. only narrowly lost his 2006 bid for a Senate seat in Tennessee, there was no contrast between his final poll numbers and his actual proportion of the vote.
