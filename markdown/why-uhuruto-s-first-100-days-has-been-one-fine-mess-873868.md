## What you need to know:

* A rather grim first 100 days of the Jubilee government only slightly lightened up last week with the diplomatic joke of boycotting the US Independence Day celebrations, apparently to retaliate President Barack Obama skipping Kenya during his recent tour of Africa

Kenya under President Uhuru Kenyatta is not exactly a failed state, as some survey has recently sought to portray it for the umpteenth time.

But it is fair to say it is one fine mess. National security is collapsing with village death squads having a free reign in places like Bungoma, Busia and Kitui.

A teachers’ strike has paralysed learning in schools in the past two weeks.

Pockets of street demonstrations in Nairobi might spread to other areas in the coming weeks against the [VAT Bill](http://www.nation.co.ke/News/-/1056/1902638/-/voa26uz/-/index.html), which seeks to tax food, medicines and other basic commodities for the poor.

A rather grim first 100 days of the Jubilee government only slightly lightened up last week with the diplomatic joke of [boycotting the US Independence Day celebrations](http://www.nation.co.ke/News/Kenyan-govt-officials-snub-US-Independence-Day-celebrations/-/1056/1904076/-/wmr98cz/-/index.html), apparently to retaliate President Barack Obama skipping Kenya during his recent tour of Africa.

**Essential contact**

A wag reckoned that Kenya was invoking its own foreign policy of keeping only ‘essential contact’ with nations that don’t show us enough respect.

The ‘essential contact’ phrase was introduced into the local political parlance in the run-up to the last elections when a number of Western nations threatened to isolate Kenya if President Kenyatta and his deputy William Ruto, who are facing crimes against humanity charges at the International Criminal Court, were elected.

The two leaders initially appeared keen to deflect attention from their ‘ICC suspects’ tag and prove the doubters wrong by making the right pronouncements about cutting the bloated public wage bill and appointing a Cabinet of technocrats.

But since then they have lurched onto one blunder after another, including trying to weaken constitutional institutions like the National Police Service Commission, silence civil society, kill devolution and engage in petty diplomatic sideshows.

So why have these leaders called ‘the dynamic duo’ by former President Kibaki at the handover ceremony performed so miserably only months after running an energetic campaign?

**Confidence crisis**

Well, I have always thought it had to do with the crisis of confidence that naturally comes with the integrity questions and the disputed election victory.

But three days ago, I asked a reflective colleague in the newsroom what he thought about Jubilee’s first 100 days and got a more interesting diagnosis.  
He said: “I always ask myself who advises them. I see a lot of nuts and bolts in Jubilee, but where is the software?”

True, Jubilee has in its ranks too many sycophants-in-chief and the fellows *Sunday Nation* columnist Makau Mutua calls ‘bloviators’.

If it were not for Jubilee’s poverty of software, they would by now have understood that you may use the courts to force a hungry teacher back to class, but you can’t force him or her to teach a Standard One pupil how to operate a laptop computer.
