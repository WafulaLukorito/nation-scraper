# Does Martha Karua stand a chance in 2012?

**Date:** 2010-10-23T13:20:52Z

## What you need to know:

**Advice:** She must assemble an astute team of advisors and listen to them

She has been acclaimed with the loftiest of words and vilified with the worst of epithets. To her supporters and reluctant admirers, she is the iron lady – fearless, bold, and plain spoken.

To her detractors, she is a feminazi – a fire-eating feminist who chews men and spits them out. To former President Daniel arap Moi, she was perhaps the devil incarnate. To some wealthy snobs, she is the peasant from Gichugu, uncouth and unrefined.

Some Kenyans may think of her as a Kikuyu chauvinist. But a large majority regards her as a tenacious, principled, and unbending champion of reform. She is Martha Karua. The civil society doyen turned politician.

Like Prime Minister Raila Odinga, Ms Karua evokes both fear and adoration. You either love or hate her. We might as well coin Karuaphilia and Karuaphobia to describe the polar emotions she attracts.

But whatever you think of her, Ms Karua is slowly and methodically carving her niche in Kenya’s political nomenklatura.

We know this for sure – she is now the first lady of Kenyan politics. That is a mantle she has grabbed from minister Charity Ngilu, once known as Mama Rainbow. Among the women, she has no peer.

She is the chairperson of Narc-Kenya and its declared presidential candidate.  
To the chagrin of her opponents, Ms Karua has consistently rated well in opinion polls among likely presidential contenders.

If wishes were horses, beggars would ride. But that is not Ms Karua’s reality. She is a doer, not a pitiable dreamer. Rather than think wishfully, she wishes thoughtfully.

This is the mark of someone who knows what success takes. That’s why she has been working like a meat grinder, meticulously confronting her opponents.

She is systematically challenging them to put their money where their mouths are. She does not accept sloppy answers, or clever witticisms that pass for sound bites by politicians. Ms Karua likes to explain complex issues.

Unlike some politicos, she does not think that the public is too dumb to digest difficult concepts. She treats the electorate as the intelligent citizenry that it is. She is a breath of fresh air. With her, what you see is what you get.

But there is a nagging question. Even with all these virtues, does Ms Karua stand a chance in 2012? The zenith of Kenyan politics has been a man’s preserve. Few women – and I mean a measly few – have ever become national political icons.

And none – except Ms Ngilu – has ever been a plausible presidential candidate. Could Ms Karua change this sorry history, or is the deck fully stacked against her?

Like President Barack Obama of the United States, she believes that negative history mustn’t be read as a bar to her electability. She is an optimist and a fighter by nature.

**Political marathon**  
That’s why she will fight to the bitter or sweet end, win or lose. Then men in the field have been warned – prepare for a political marathon because Ms Karua is in the fight to win it all.

Ms Karua seems to believe that her stock is on the rise. Many Kenyans take her seriously. The Narc-K wins in the recent by-elections in Makadara and Juja – against ODM and PNU – must buoy her.

However, some political pundits and ODM and PNU operatives were quick to pour cold water on Narc-K successes. Some argued, incredibly, that the wins were a fluke.

I don’t think so. Ms Karua played her politics right and “took over” candidates who were electable. That’s politics 101.

But we know that a Kikuyu will not sell in 2012. That’s why Ms Karua must make Kenyans forget that she is a Kikuyu.

She must run as a Kenyan, a woman, and a reformer.

To be sure, she is all three, but she must accentuate them.

She must convince Kenyans that she resigned as minister for Justice and Constitutional Affairs in 2009 because the Kibaki state was corrupt, tribalistic and opaque.

This is a tall order because she fought ferociously for Mr Kibaki after the disputed elections. As minister, she continued to defend Mr Kibaki even when it was obvious that he had not changed his stripes.

I believe Kenyans will understand the difficult choices she faced and let it go. But she must first explain herself.

Ms Karua must embrace her past as a civil society activist and a feminist. This is where she earned her reformist epaulettes.

Kenyans need to know that her history in the struggle forged her into a trusted Kenyan who can clean up the rotten state.

She must activate women, one of her key bases. She must reach out beyond the Mt Kenya region and connect – one on one – with folks in other parts of Kenya.

**Huge obstacles**  
She cannot give up on western Kenya simply because Mr Odinga is dominant there, or write off Ukambani because of Vice-President Kalonzo Musyoka. Like Mr Kibaki, the chauvinist and wealthy Kikuyu will not support her, but the Kikuyu vote could be wide open if the ICC indicts some of its leading lights.

Mr Musyoka and Mr Odinga will present huge obstacles to Ms Karua’s march to State House. But Section 138 of the new Constitution provides that the winner must garner 50 per cent plus one of the votes cast and at least 25 per cent of the votes in 50 per cent plus one of the counties.

Ms Karua may stand a chance in a three-way race. But to win, her politics cannot be business as usual. She must assemble an astute team of advisors and listen to them.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
