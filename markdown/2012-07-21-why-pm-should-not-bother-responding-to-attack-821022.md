# Why PM should not bother responding to attack

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2012-07-21T17:08:29Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/why-pm-should-not-bother-responding-to-attack-821022

## What you need to know:

* Raila shouldn’t stoop low to answer a disgruntled former employee. That’s the mark of a strong leader

Many people consider Miguna Miguna a “bloviating ignoramus”. But he’s a humongous hero to some. However, he’s a villain to the Nyando villagers who burnt him in effigy and buried him in a mock funeral.

Let’s get one thing straight – all these opposing views are totally legitimate and democratic. That’s what Kenya’s new Constitution permits – and encourages. Peaceful citizen dissent and disagreement – no matter how noisy and rancorous – sit at the centre of the democratic project.

But defamation and violence don’t. This point seems to be lost on Mr Miguna’s detractors and supporters alike.

But mark this. Prime Minister Raila Odinga ought to completely ignore Mr Miguna. That’s because Mr Odinga is a public figure who is larger than life.

I have had my differences with Mr Miguna. I’ve written two columns on him. In the first, “An open letter to ODM’s Miguna Miguna,” *Sunday Nation*, March 7, 2010, I asked PM Odinga to give Mr Miguna the sack for insubordination.

In the second, “Why Miguna’s sacking was long coming,” *Sunday Nation*, August 21, 2011, I celebrated his firing. My views about Mr Miguna haven’t changed. But I believe that he has every right to publish his “memoirs”.

However, Mr Miguna must know that he cannot commit defamation in his “memoirs”. Nor can he make up non-existent facts. Otherwise, he would face the full force of the law. Question: Did Mr Miguna commit defamation in his “memoirs”?

Whether Mr Miguna committed defamation is for the courts to decide. I know he’d better have documentary evidence to back up the explosive allegations he’s made. If not, his goose will be cooked.

I know from my legal work with former anti-graft czar John Githongo that we made sure that every allegation was backed up by an unimpeachable document, or piece of evidence. Does Mr Miguna have foolproof evidence to protect him from devastating lawsuits? Only time will tell. But I would shut up if I was him. He’s ill-served by verbal gaffes. Everything he says or writes can – and will probably – be used against him in a court of law.

I think that many of those mentioned in Mr Miguna’s book – *Peeling Back the Mask* – may respond to him. Some may take him to court. Others may simply repudiate his claims in the court of public opinion. That’s their right. Some of the most prominent names he mentioned include Lands minister James Orengo and Medical Services minister Prof Peter Anyang’ Nyong’o. Their proximity to Mr Odinga made them particularly inviting targets.

They may – or perhaps not – choose to answer him. Mrs Ida Odinga, the PM’s spouse, also came under heavy fire. But the most vociferous attacks were reserved for Mr Odinga. The Prime Minister was Mr Miguna’s reason for writing the book. It’s him Mr Miguna sought desperately to hurt. My unequivocal advice to Mr Odinga is simple and clear – leave Mr Miguna alone. Ignore him – completely. Why? First, Mr Miguna is his own worst enemy.

He’s emotional and exhibits an ungoverned anger. He’s loud and boisterous – like an insecure school yard bully who compensates for his inadequacies by attacking others. His ego is gargantuan.

Everything begins and ends with him. He’s the centre of the moral universe. He’s virtuous, brilliant, hardworking, and all-knowing. He’s the world’s gift to humanity. So were the late dictators Idi Amin Dada of Uganda and Mobutu Sese Seko of Zaire.

It’s strange Mr Miguna likes to mimic their attire. You can’t argue with a person who’s so self-assured. He could be a caricature.

Public servants of Mr Odinga’s international stature are the subject — or brunt — of many terrible books.

Imagine all the books written about President Barack Obama. Many make wild, defamatory, false, and hurtful allegations.

But Mr Obama has never responded to them. Nor has he sued any of their authors.

The same is true of other world leaders. That’s the cost of fame at the pinnacle of public life.

But it would also send a chilling message if world leaders were thin-skinned.

Democracy is stronger where citizens are free to say anything about their leaders without fear of retaliation. That’s the mark of a strong leader. Mr Odinga shouldn’t stoop low to answer a disgruntled former employee. I don’t give Mr Odinga this advice without much thought.

I know this is the “silly season”. That’s the electoral season when everybody has something to say.

I also know that Mr Odinga’s opponents on the PNU/G7 side have been egging Mr Miguna on.

I don’t believe that they can believe their luck. Mr Miguna is like a loaded gun in their hands. They hope to use him to end Mr Odinga’s political life. But there’s gathering evidence — especially with Mr Miguna’s “flight” to Canada — that their “gun has backfired”. I think Mr Odinga would be wise to take all of this in stride. It’s a storm in a teacup. It’s got no real legs.

The celebrated TV journalist, Jeff Koinange, refers to Mr Miguna as the man with the “same name twice”.

That could be an apt description for the cohabitation of two persons in one body. On the one hand, Mr Miguna seems like a bright chap.

But is he a split personality? Does he have, as political journalist Sarah Elderkin intuits, uncontrollable demons?

As they say, I ain’t no psychiatrist – and, therefore, don’t know. Food for thought.

This is one more reason Mr Odinga shouldn’t answer Mr Miguna. Let sleeping dogs lie.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at SUNY Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
