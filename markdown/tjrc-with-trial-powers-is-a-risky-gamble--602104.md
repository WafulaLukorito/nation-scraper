# TJRC with trial powers is a risky gamble 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2009-08-08T13:54:43Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/news/politics/tjrc-with-trial-powers-is-a-risky-gamble--602104  

---

The decision by the Cabinet to expand the mandate and membership of the Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission has provoked heated debate.

*Sunday Nation* writer GAKIHA WERU interviewed Law Society of Kenya chairman Okong’o Omogeni.

Excerpts.

**Under what circumstances does a country need a TJRC?**

As the name goes, a truth, justice and reconciliation commission is a body established in law with the specific mandate to uncover the truth about past [abuses], offer transitional justice to both perpetrators and victims and to foster reconciliation amongst the perpetrators and the victims.

The abuses are more often than not politically instigated and vary from internal civil unrest, ethnic clashes, massacres, political assassinations, torture, detention, exile and historical and continuing violations of economic, social and cultural rights.

It is expected to minimise revenge and societal trauma, eradicate the culture of impunity, and to achieve reconciliation and transitional justice.

**How have such commissions worked in other parts of the world?**

The Rwandan National Unity and Reconciliation Commission sought to achieve three main objectives; eradicate the culture of impunity, achieve reconciliation and justice and to build confidence among Rwandans in themselves.

Its success mainly hinged on the fact that the Rwandan judicial system had crumbled. The often cited South African truth and reconciliation commission, established after the end of the apartheid regime, granted both perpetrators and victims alike an equal opportunity to be heard and in some cases granted amnesty to the perpetrators of crimes, reparations and rehabilitation.

However, it was criticised by those who believed that justice was a prerequisite for reconciliation rather than an alternative to it and that the TRC was weighted in favour of the perpetrators of abuses.

In a nutshell, the success or failure of a commission mainly hinges on its architecture or mandate, and the political support during implementation.

**In your opinion, is TJRC necessary for Kenya now?**

The post-independence history of Kenya is bedevilled with the very acts that form the recipe for the establishment of a TJRC ranging from political assassinations to ethnic clashes to violations of economic, social and cultural rights and most recently the violence that bore genocidal characteristics.

If it takes the TJRC to heal the wounds of this country, reconcile the people for the national good and restore trust and confidence amongst each other then the establishment of a TJRC has been a long time coming.

**How far back should the TJRC inquiries go?**

Effectively, the commission’s mandate should leave no stone unturned and ensure justice for all. So long as gross violations of human rights were perpetrated on a people, the scope of inquiries ought not to be limited.

**Comment on the recent Cabinet decision to expand the Kiplagat team.**

It is true that the timing of the formation of the TJRC was ill-advised. Even though as early as August 26, 2003 the report of the task force on the establishment of a TJRC had been presented to the government, it took all this time to establish one.

**Is the TJRC really an option to a local tribunal or The Hague?**

One of the clearest findings of the task force on a TJRC led by Prof Makau Mutua was that a truth commission is not a judicial body.

The task force went on to recommend that those responsible for gross violations of human rights must be punished.

This aspect was in fact incorporated into the TJRC Act and nothing in the Act empowers the commission to prosecute.

**The Cabinet has recommended expanding the mandate of the TJRC. In your view, how practical is the envisaged commission?**

The Cabinet statement on the said expansion has been rather vague. From a political standpoint, however, it is possible that the said expansion aims at filling the void of a local tribunal and is mainly necessitated by the divisions and disagreements in Cabinet.

The TRC of South Africa was faced with among other challenges, legal action in South Africa’s highest court, challenging some of its actions as being unconstitutional.

The family of Steve Biko described the TRC as a vehicle for political expediency and sued the TRC on grounds that it did not have the power to offer amnesty for his killers.

No doubt the same challenges are bound to mar the work of our TJRC should the government seek to use it as an alternative to justice. In the end, the commission will lose its legitimacy and credibility, two elements that are crucial for its success

**What other legal challenges is the commission likely to be confronted with?**

In my mind, I cannot imagine a TJRC with prosecutorial powers.

The creation of one of such kind will require a substantial overhaul of our municipal laws, including the Constitution and the international crimes law, which effectively isolates Kenya from the international best practice and standards.

I am sure the government does not want to risk the misfortunes of taking that route.

**Does Mr Bethuel Kiplagat in your view have the moral authority to head the team?**

Given the recent aspersions that have been cast on Mr Kiplagat, his moral authority in the circumstances are no doubt expected to dwindle.

However, the most important thing right now is that he has already been appointed and sworn in as a commissioner and the only lawful procedure for removing him from office is through the establishment of a tribunal under section 17 of the TJRC Act.

In my view, initiating such procedures at the moment will only delay the process.

**Do you get the feeling that the TJRC is deliberately designed to fail?**

Judging from the Act, as it is currently, the TJRC is empowered with all the legal safeguards it needs to discharge its mandate.
