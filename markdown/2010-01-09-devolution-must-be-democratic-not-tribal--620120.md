# Devolution must be democratic, not tribal 

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2010-01-09T16:42:47Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/blogs-opinion/opinion/devolution-must-be-democratic-not-tribal--620120  

---

I know that the deadline to submit views to the Committee of Experts is long gone for the hoi polloi, but better late than never.

In any case, the hoity toitty – our overlords – will have the ears of the Committee right until the last “i” is dotted and the final “t” crossed. So I might as well put in a final word.

I am concerned that the logic of devolution in the draft is tribal. Both the regional and county governments – and the Senate – could turn out to be fora for the entrenchment of tribal interests and the pursuit of exclusivist policies.

That is why the draft constitution must have devices to make discrimination against “outsider” groups impossible. Otherwise, the constitution will harden ethnic identities and retard the project of nation-building.

I am the first to concede that group identities – such as ethnicities, races, and religions – are important and should be protected in a democratic constitution. This is particularly important if such identities are in the minority.

But the constitution must never balkanise a country into tribal enclaves unless there is clear and convincing history of the most horrific and stubborn discrimination against certain groups. Even so, such groups can be protected by other legal devices without creating “tribal ghettos”.

“Bantustans” do nothing but elevate tribal consciousness, group animosity, and nurture discrimination against minority groups. The democratic project is about learning how to live with those who are different.

I wonder whether the Committee seriously considered the challenge of crafting a devolution structure while minimising the ugly spectre of tribalism.

I agree that this is a difficult task given the fact that most parts of the country are dominated by single groups.

Each major tribe is given its own county or region. Smaller tribes are subsumed in regions or counties dominated by larger groups.

The Committee has taken the colonial map – which was tribalised to ease the domination of the “natives” by “divide-and-rule” and gerrymandered it to perfect ethnic exclusivism. Lord Frederick Lugard, the British colonial overlord who oversaw the tribal balkanisation of Africa’s British colonies, might be smiling in his grave.

The Committee should appreciate the basic fact that democracy is built on individuals, not blocs of communities. In a democracy, each person is an individual first and foremost, and then a member of other social formations. It is as an individual that a citizen contracts with the state.

Even when the constitution recognises group rights – as does the South African Constitution – it does so in the context of individual rights. Thus members of a group can be entitled to certain group rights but only in their individual capacities.

Affirmative action, for example, is accorded to eligible groups, but only to members of those groups as individuals. Redress is given to the group but only through its individual members.

The Committee seemed to have conflated group rights with ethnic exclusivism. These are two different concepts. Group rights are supposed to empower marginalised or weak groups.

On the other hand, ethnic exclusivism, which the Committee unwittingly adopted, is designed to “exclude” for the purposes of “domination” and “discrimination”.

That is what the Bantustans in South Africa were meant to achieve. That is the purpose of ghettos in the United States. The Committee could have articulated the concept of ethnic democracy if it wanted a more intelligent use of ethnicity in constitution making.

But it did not. Instead, it used un-interrogated ethnic categories because of the loud voices of ethnic warlords in the political class. The result is a devolution structure that focuses more on group identity and less on democracy.

There are two important concepts that the Committee should consider. The first is “ethnic democracy” and the second is “ethnocracy”.

Ethnic democracy refers to a political system dominated by one ethnic group but where minority groups are allowed a measure of political participation. Malaysia is thought to be one example of an ethnic democracy because it has democratic features although it officially favours the Malay.

Ethnocracy, on the other hand, refers to a state disproportionately dominated by one group which systematically discriminates over other groups. This concept has nothing to do with democracy. Apartheid South Africa was, for all intents and purposes, an ethnocracy.

Did the Committee think seriously about how dominant groups would exercise their power in the counties or regions? This is not simply a “matter of the law.”

If the Committee intended to create “ethnic democracy” in the counties and regions, how can it assure that “in fact” dominant groups will not use their numbers to discriminate against minorities and exclude them?

Even if such assurances could be granted, how do they advance the project of nation building? If the Committee decides to keep the devolution scheme in the draft, then it must craft stringent and watertight equal protection and anti-discrimination devices.

*Makau Mutua is Dean and SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo Law School and Chair of the KHRC.*
