# US sanctions on Sudan put African nations to shame

**Author:** Unknown Author  
**Date:** 2007-06-02T21:00:00Z  
**Source URL:** https://nation.africa/kenya/news/us-sanctions-on-sudan-put-african-nations-to-shame-534700  

---

The imposition by President George W. Bush of new symbolic sanctions on Sudan because of its genocide in Darfur should put African states to shame.

President George Bush delivers a statement on Darfur from the Diplomatic Room of the White House in Washington on Tuesday. He ordered US sanctions tightened against Sudan’s government for its refusal to stop the violence in the Darfur region. Photo/REUTERS

The penalties, which are largely economic, seek to put more pressure on Khartoum so that it can halt its genocidal campaign against black African Muslims in Darfur. While commendable — even if it is only symbolic — the American action serves to highlight the impotence of the African Union and the individual leaders who govern the only continent whose majority population is black. This state of impotence must end otherwise the refrain, never again, coined after the genocide of Jews by the German Nazis, will remain a meaningless slogan.

The facts about the Darfur genocide are not in dispute. Over the four years, Arab Janjaweed militias and Sudanese government troops have carried out a scotched earth campaign of mass slaughter, pillage, rape and plunder against black African Muslims. It is now estimated that as many as half a million black Darfurians have been killed and at least two million displaced from their homes and villages.

So far, the international community has responded feebly. The AU peacekeeping force is small, poorly equipped, and totally ineffective. Sudan has refused to permit the deployment of a larger and robust UN force to stop the genocide.

The genocide in Darfur would not be permitted if it were taking place in Europe. The last genocide there, in the former Yugoslavia, provoked an international outcry that led to Western intervention.

But such mass abominations in Africa as took place in Rwanda and the Democratic Republic of Congo barely registered on the international radar. Darfur has not been different. Helpless women, children, and men continue to be slaughtered in their thousands as the world looks on, unwilling to lift a finger. That is why the response by President Bush, though too little too late, could build some additional momentum against Sudan.

**30 oil companies**

The American sanctions, which built on earlier ones imposed in 1997, target 30 oil companies that are either run or owned by the Sudanese government. Another is accused of violating the arms embargo for Darfur. All 31 companies will be forbidden from doing business in the United States or accessing its financial system.

These sanctions are not expected to be costly to Sudan since it can resort to others to run its oil industry. The sanctions also target three individuals — two senior government officials and a rebel leader — who would be prohibited from doing business in the US. Most importantly, these symbolic sanctions are not expected to affect US-Sudanese cooperation in counter-terrorism intelligence activities.

Only a real arms and oil embargo against the government of President Omar Bashir can affect Sudan’s behaviour in Darfur. Slaps on the wrist — like the American or AU measures — will not do. What is needed is a comprehensive sanctions regime against Khartoum so that it understands that the world will not tolerate genocide in the twenty-first century.

Clearly, Sudan cannot continue to execute the war without access to weaponry for its forces and the Janjaweed militias. The denial of spare parts for its military aircraft is critical. International isolation in all matters military would impress on the fundamentalists that its barbaric conduct makes it a pariah.

But the most important sanctions should directly target the exploitation and marketing of Sudan’s oil. This is the most vital sector of the economy and the source of revenue for purchasing military hardware for use in Darfur.

If the world could impose an embargo on the sale of Iraqi oil under President Saddam Hussein, it is completely baffling why the same cannot be done for Sudan. Global oil companies must be banned from exploiting or selling Sudanese oil.

Sudan must be given a stark choice: either end the genocide or lose access to the international oil market. Companies or countries trading in Sudanese oil should be denied access to the global financial system.

Sanctions on the Sudanese oil industry will not succeed unless the Chinese government stops protecting the killers of Khartoum for the sake of oil. China must be placed squarely on the spot. Does it value oil so much more than Africans that it is willing to coddle a regime that is committing genocide?

China has recently joined the West in the scramble for Africa’s resources. Africa states must reject these Chinese overtures unless it starts to pursue an enlightened foreign policy in Africa.

China is vulnerable on Sudan in a number of respects. China will be playing host to the 2008 Olympics. The international community, led by African civil societies, must put maximum pressure on governments around the world to boycott the Beijing Olympics if China blocks or vetoes comprehensive UN sanctions on Sudan.

**World-beating athletes**

There is no doubt that the Olympics would be considered a flop without Africa’s world-beating athletes. How can the sons and daughters of Africa support the Olympics in a country that was protecting a perpetrator of genocide on fellow Africans? Leading Kenyan athletes should start to speak up now. They must lead this charge.

Liu Guijin, China’s roving ambassador to Africa, has even denied that there is genocide in Darfur. This is an incredible statement.

As President Bush stated this week, his “Administration has called these actions by their rightful name: genocide”.

Once that word is invoked, it imposes an international legal obligation on all states to act to stop the genocide. Passivity or complicity is not an option. But Africans must lead the struggle to end abuses on their continent. Only then will others treat us as full human beings.

Makau Mutua is SUNY Distinguished Professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo and Chair of the Kenya Human Rights Commission.
